Chapter 1

Shannon woke before the alarm, the way she had started doing sometime after the divorce, as if her body no longer trusted anything external to keep time. The room was still dark, faint gray pressing through the curtains, the kind of morning that hadn’t decided what it was yet.

She lay there a minute, listening.

The house settled. Pipes clicked somewhere behind the wall. Outside, a truck rolled down the highway, low and steady, like something passing through without intention of stopping.

Nothing in the room asked anything of her.

That was the problem.

She pushed the covers back and swung her legs over the side of the bed. The floor was cold. She stood there a second, letting it register, then moved through the routine she had built because she needed something to move through.

Coffee first. Always coffee.

The kitchen light hummed when she flipped it on. The coffeemaker was already set from the night before. She pressed the button and leaned against the counter while it started its slow, mechanical drip.

There had been a time when mornings had felt like they belonged to something. A schedule. A classroom. A list of names she knew without looking at a sheet.

Now it was just her.

And whatever she decided to do with the hours.

She picked up her phone, checked it out of habit. Nothing she needed. A few notifications she didn’t open. She set it back down.

The coffee finished. She poured a cup, black, and carried it out to the small porch off the side of the house. The boards creaked in the same places they always did.

The air had that early spring edge—cool but not sharp, the kind that carried the smell of turned dirt and something starting back up.

Fields stretched out behind the property, low and open, bordered by a fence that leaned just enough to suggest it had been leaning for a while.

She stood there, sipping, watching nothing in particular.

It wasn’t that anything was wrong.

That was what made it hard to name.

By midmorning, she was at the diner.

It sat just off the main road through town, a low brick building with a faded sign that hadn’t changed since she was a kid. Inside, the same booths, the same scuffed floor, the same rhythm of conversation layered over the clink of plates.

Shannon slid into a booth near the window. Carol brought her a second cup of coffee without asking.

“You look awake,” Carol said.

“I am,” Shannon said.

“That’s new.”

Shannon gave a small smile. “Working on it.”

Carol nodded like that was something worth acknowledging and moved on.

Shannon wrapped her hands around the mug and looked out the window. A couple of trucks in the lot. A man she recognized but couldn’t place walking toward the hardware store across the street.

Everything in town had a place. A pattern.

She just wasn’t sure where she fit into it anymore.

The bell over the door rang.

She didn’t look up at first. People came in, people went out. It wasn’t anything that needed attention.

But the room shifted.

It was small—just a subtle tightening in the way voices dropped half a notch, the way Carol glanced toward the door before catching herself.

Shannon looked up.

Deputy Wade Carter stood just inside, hat in hand, scanning the room the way he always did, not in a hurry but not casual either. He had that same posture she remembered—shoulders squared, movements controlled, like he was aware of space in a way other people weren’t.

He saw her.

Of course he did.

His gaze settled, and for a second, something like recognition softened it. Then it was gone, replaced by something more neutral, more official.

He crossed the room.

“Shannon.”

“Wade.”

He stopped beside the booth, one hand resting lightly on the back of the seat across from her. “Mind?”

She shrugged a shoulder. “Free country.”

That got the smallest shift at the corner of his mouth, not quite a smile.

He slid in across from her.

Up close, he looked the same in the ways that mattered. Older, maybe, lines a little more set, but still steady. Still contained.

“You been back long?” he asked.

“Few months.”

He nodded like he’d already known that. “Heard something about that.”

“People hear things.”

“They do.”

Carol came over, poured him coffee without asking. He thanked her, waited until she moved off before picking the cup up.

“You settling in?” he asked.

Shannon took a sip of her own coffee. “Settled enough.”

“That mean yes or no?”

“It means I’m here.”

He studied her for a second, not pushing, but not letting it pass either.

“Teaching again?” he asked.

“No.”

He didn’t ask why.

That was one of the things about Wade. He left space where most people filled it.

“Doing what, then?” he said.

“Figuring that out.”

He nodded once, like that was a reasonable answer, even if it didn’t say much.

Silence settled between them, not uncomfortable, just… present.

“You still out on your place?” she asked.

“Yeah.”

“Nothing changed.”

“Not much does,” he said.

There was something in the way he said it that made it sound like he preferred it that way.

Shannon looked back out the window.

“You always come in this early?” he asked.

“Sometimes.”

“By yourself.”

She glanced back at him. “You keeping track?”

“Just noticing.”

“That’s your job.”

“Part of it.”

His tone stayed even, but there was an edge under it, something that leaned toward assumption.

She let that sit.

“You still doing that?” she said.

“Doing what.”

“Deciding things for people before they say them.”

He didn’t look away. “I didn’t decide anything.”

“Sounded like it.”

A beat.

“Just asking questions,” he said.

“Feels like more than that.”

He leaned back slightly, one arm resting along the back of the booth.

“You always read into things,” he said.

“And you always pretend you’re not saying what you’re saying.”

That did get a faint smile. “Fair.”

They held each other’s gaze a second longer than necessary.

Then the bell over the door rang again.

This time, the shift in the room was sharper.

Shannon didn’t need to look to know something had changed. Conversations cut off mid-sentence. Chairs scraped. Someone said something low and quick near the counter.

Wade’s attention moved before his body did, his head turning toward the door, posture tightening just enough to notice.

Two men stepped in, talking fast, one of them shaking his head.

“…they picked him up this morning—”

“—no way—”

“—I’m telling you—Turner—”

The name landed.

Wade was already moving, sliding out of the booth.

“What’s going on,” he said, not a question so much as a directive.

The men looked at him, one of them gesturing vaguely back toward the road.

“Sheriff’s got Eli Turner,” he said. “Cattle theft. Up on the Hargrove place.”

The room stilled.

Even the people who hadn’t been paying attention before were now.

Wade’s expression didn’t change much, but something in it settled, like a piece had dropped into place.

“When?” he asked.

“Early. Before sunup, I heard.”

Wade nodded once.

“Appreciate it,” he said, already turning.

He glanced back at Shannon, just briefly.

“Stay out of it,” he said.

It was quiet, almost casual.

But it wasn’t a suggestion.

Then he was gone.

The door closed behind him.

The room didn’t go back to normal.

By the time Shannon stepped out of the diner, the town had already started to turn the news over in its hands.

Clusters of people stood in small groups along the sidewalk, talking low but fast. Trucks idled longer than they needed to. Someone leaned out of a driver’s window, asking a question that didn’t get a clear answer.

Eli Turner.

It wasn’t a name you heard attached to something like that.

She walked toward her car, catching fragments as she passed.

“—Hargroves don’t play around—”

“—they wouldn’t bring it unless it was solid—”

“—he’s always kept to himself—”

“—doesn’t matter—”

She opened the door, paused, hand on the frame.

Too clean.

The thought came uninvited.

She didn’t know why.

She got in the car and drove.

Emily’s office sat on the second floor of a building just off the square, above a law firm that had been there long enough to feel permanent.

Shannon took the stairs two at a time, the old wood creaking under her weight.

The door was open.

Emily sat at her desk, papers spread out in front of her, phone tucked between her shoulder and ear.

“Yes, I’ve got it—no, I understand—just give me a minute—”

She looked up when Shannon stepped in, held up a finger.

Shannon leaned against the doorframe, waiting.

Emily finished the call, set the phone down, and exhaled.

“You heard,” she said.

“Hard not to.”

Emily nodded, already reaching for a file.

“It’s real,” she said. “Not rumor.”

Shannon stepped further into the room. “What do you know.”

Emily flipped the file open, scanning.

“Sheriff’s office picked him up this morning. Charges tied to cattle taken off the Hargrove property over the last few weeks. They’ve got records, apparently. Sales. Movement.”

“Eli Turner?”

“That’s what it says.”

Shannon watched her sister’s face.

Emily was good at this—processing, organizing, fitting pieces together. There wasn’t much she missed.

“How solid is it,” Shannon said.

Emily hesitated, just a fraction.

“On paper?” she said. “Pretty clean.”

“Clean how.”

“Documentation lines up. Dates match. Witness statements—at least preliminary—support it. They’re saying he moved the cattle through a secondary buyer out of county.”

Shannon crossed her arms.

“That doesn’t sound like him.”

Emily glanced up. “You know him?”

“Not really.”

“Then—”

“It doesn’t sound like him,” Shannon said again.

Emily closed the file halfway, considering.

“The Hargroves are pushing this,” she said. “That matters.”

“In what way.”

“In the way that means it’s not going away.”

Shannon nodded slowly.

“And you think it’s right,” she said.

Emily didn’t answer immediately.

“I think,” she said carefully, “that it’s structured to hold up.”

“That’s not the same thing.”

“No,” Emily said. “It’s not.”

They let that sit.

“You going to see him?” Shannon asked.

Emily’s brows drew slightly. “Why would I—”

“You’re already looking at it.”

“I’m looking at paperwork.”

“You always do more than that.”

Emily studied her for a second.

“You’re interested,” she said.

“No,” Shannon said.

Emily waited.

Shannon looked away. “I’m just asking.”

“That’s not just asking.”

Shannon didn’t respond.

Emily sighed, closed the file fully.

“He’s being held for now,” she said. “They’ll move him over to the courthouse later today for initial processing.”

Shannon nodded once.

“Okay.”

She turned toward the door.

“Shannon.”

She paused, glanced back.

“Be careful,” Emily said.

“Of what.”

Emily held her gaze. “Of getting pulled into something you don’t need to be in.”

Shannon considered that.

“Seems like I already am,” she said.

Then she left.

The courthouse sat at the center of town, white columns out front, the steps worn smooth from years of use.

By the time Shannon got there, a small crowd had gathered, not large, but tight, the kind that watched more than it spoke.

A sheriff’s vehicle pulled up just as she reached the edge of the steps.

The back door opened.

Eli Turner stepped out.

He wasn’t what she expected.

Not in any dramatic way. He just… didn’t match the shape of the story people were already building.

He was calm.

Not defiant. Not broken.

Just steady.

His hands were cuffed in front of him, the chain short, forcing a certain kind of posture, but he didn’t fight it. He moved when directed, stopped when told.

His gaze lifted briefly, scanning the space ahead of him.

It passed over the crowd without catching.

Then, for a moment, it landed on Shannon.

It didn’t linger.

But it didn’t flinch either.

There was no plea in it. No attempt to reach out or connect.

Just acknowledgment.

Then it moved on.

A deputy guided him toward the steps.

“Watch your footing,” the man said.

Eli nodded once, like he’d heard him.

They went inside.

The door closed.

The crowd shifted, voices starting up again, low and speculative.

Shannon stayed where she was.

That look—

It wasn’t what someone looked like when they were about to fight for their life.

Or maybe it was.

Just not in the way she expected.

She didn’t go in.

Not right away.

She walked the perimeter of the square instead, letting the movement burn off something she couldn’t quite name.

Every conversation she passed circled the same points.

“—Hargroves wouldn’t make a mistake—”

“—they’ve been watching that land for years—”

“—he’s always been quiet—”

“—quiet don’t mean innocent—”

She stopped near the edge of the park, looking back at the courthouse.

Too clean.

Emily’s words.

Structured to hold up.

It was the same thing, said different ways.

Her phone buzzed in her pocket.

She pulled it out.

A message from Emily.

Initial hearing in an hour.

Shannon stared at it a second.

Then she slipped the phone back into her pocket and started toward the steps.

Inside, the air was cooler, quieter.

The hallway outside the small courtroom was already filling, people taking positions along the walls, waiting.

Shannon found a spot near the back.

She didn’t know what she expected to see.

Probably something clearer.

Something that would settle the feeling that had been building since the diner.

The door opened.

They brought Eli in.

Same posture. Same calm.

He took his place at the front, beside a public defender Shannon didn’t recognize.

The judge entered. Proceedings started.

It moved quickly.

Charges read. Basic statements. Scheduling.

Nothing dramatic.

Nothing that didn’t fit.

Everything aligned.

That should have been enough.

It wasn’t.

Eli spoke once, when asked.

“Yes, ma’am.”

His voice was low, even.

No strain.

No edge.

He didn’t argue.

He didn’t explain.

He just answered what was put in front of him.

Wade stood off to the side of the room, not directly involved, but present.

Watching.

At one point, his gaze flicked toward Shannon.

It held a second longer than Eli’s had.

Different, though.

Assessing.

Then it moved on.

The hearing ended.

People started to file out.

Shannon stayed where she was until the room thinned.

Eli was led out through a side door.

He didn’t look back.

Chapter 2

Outside, the afternoon had settled in, warmer now, the light sharper.

Shannon stood at the top of the courthouse steps, looking out over the square.

Everything looked the same.

But it wasn’t.

She could feel it.

Not in a way she could explain.

Just—

Off.

A voice came up behind her.

“You should’ve stayed out of it.”

Wade.

She didn’t turn right away.

“Seems like everybody’s in it,” she said.

“Not the same way.”

She faced him.

“You mean your way.”

“I mean involved.”

“That’s a sliding scale.”

“Not today.”

He stepped closer, not crowding her, but closing the distance enough to make the conversation feel contained.

“This isn’t small-town gossip,” he said. “You don’t need to be standing around in the middle of it.”

“I was in a courtroom.”

“You were watching something you don’t understand.”

“Then explain it.”

His jaw tightened, just slightly.

“It’s not your concern.”

“That’s not an explanation.”

“It’s enough.”

She held his gaze.

“No,” she said. “It’s not.”

A beat.

Something shifted in his expression—not anger, not exactly, but a firmer line, something that edged toward command.

“You need to let this go,” he said.

There it was.

Not a suggestion.

She felt it, the way it pressed.

Shannon didn’t step back.

“Why,” she said.

“Because it’s handled.”

“That’s not what it looks like.”

“What does it look like.”

She considered that.

Then she said, “Too clean.”

Wade’s eyes narrowed, just a fraction.

“Clean’s what you want,” he said.

“Not always.”

Silence stretched between them.

“You’re reading into something that isn’t there,” he said.

“Maybe.”

“But you’re not sure,” he added.

“No.”

He studied her.

Then he nodded once, like he’d made a decision.

“Stay out of it,” he said again.

He turned and walked down the steps, not looking back.

Shannon watched him go.

The square moved around her, people crossing, cars passing, the normal rhythms reasserting themselves.

But the feeling didn’t leave.

She stood there a long moment.

Then she turned, not toward her car, not toward home, but back toward the courthouse doors.

She didn’t go inside.

Not yet.

She just looked at it.

At the place where everything had lined up exactly the way it was supposed to.

And felt, quietly, without any drama or urgency—

That something didn’t fit.

Shannon didn’t mean to start asking questions.

It came out sideways, like most things did these days—slipped into conversation, light enough to pass as idle curiosity.

At the Piggly Wiggly, she mentioned Eli Turner’s name to the cashier while bagging her own groceries.

“He still out at that place off County Line?” she asked, keeping her tone neutral.

The cashier, a woman Shannon recognized but couldn’t name, paused just long enough to notice.

“Who?”

“Turner. Eli.”

The woman’s mouth tightened, just a fraction. “Don’t know him.”

But Shannon had seen the flicker. Recognition, then something like dismissal.

She let it drop.

Outside, the air held that damp early-spring weight, the kind that clung to your clothes without rain. She loaded her groceries into the back of her SUV and sat for a moment before starting the engine.

It wasn’t the denial that bothered her.

It was how quick it came.

At the hardware store, the reaction was different.

Old Mr. Blevins stood behind the counter, sorting receipts into a neat, uneven stack. He looked up when Shannon stepped in, gave her a small nod.

“You need anything?”

“Just looking.”

She wandered the aisles, letting the place settle around her—the smell of fertilizer, oil, something metallic. Familiar. Grounding.

When she came back to the counter with a box of nails she didn’t need, she said, “You ever hear of Eli Turner?”

Mr. Blevins didn’t pause.

“Everybody’s heard of Eli Turner.”

Shannon waited.

He slid the box into a bag, rang it up. “You want a receipt?”

“Yes, sir.”

He handed it over, then finally met her eyes. “You staying out on your sister’s place?”

“For now.”

He nodded once, like that confirmed something. “Then you don’t need to be worrying about him.”

“Why not?”

“Because it ain’t your business.” His tone wasn’t sharp. Just settled. “And because folks around here got long memories.”

Shannon folded the receipt without looking at it. “That doesn’t answer the question.”

“It does,” he said. “Just not the way you want.”

She didn’t push further.

But as she walked out, she felt it again—that same undercurrent she’d caught at the grocery store.

Recognition.

And something else layered under it.

Not fear, exactly.

Something closer to caution.

By the time she got back to Emily’s place, the sky had gone flat and gray.

Emily was in the kitchen, phone wedged between her shoulder and ear, flipping through a stack of papers.

“I’ll call you back,” she said quickly when Shannon walked in. “Yeah. No, I’ll check on it. Okay.”

She hung up and looked at Shannon. “You’ve got that look.”

“What look?”

“The one where you’ve decided something and haven’t told me yet.”

Shannon set the bag on the counter. “I asked around about Eli Turner.”

Emily froze for half a second, then kept moving. “And?”

“Depends who you ask.”

“That’s not comforting.”

“No.”

Emily sighed, leaned against the counter. “Shannon.”

“I’m not doing anything.”

“That’s not what it looks like.”

“I asked a couple questions.”

“That’s how it starts.”

Shannon met her eyes. “You don’t think it’s strange?”

“I think it’s none of our business.”

“That’s not what you said yesterday.”

Emily hesitated. “Yesterday was… different.”

“How?”

Emily pushed off the counter, crossed her arms. “Because yesterday he was just a guy in a file. Today you’re walking around town saying his name out loud.”

Shannon let that sit.

“I’m not looking for trouble,” she said.

Emily gave a small, humorless smile. “Trouble doesn’t need you to look for it.”

Wade was already waiting when Shannon stepped out onto the porch later that afternoon.

He leaned against his patrol car like he belonged there.

She stopped at the top of the steps. “You always this subtle?”

“Depends who I’m dealing with.”

She came down the steps slowly. “What’s this about?”

Wade straightened. His expression was neutral, but there was something tight in it.

“I heard you’ve been asking about Eli Turner.”

“Small town.”

“Small enough,” he said.

Shannon crossed her arms. “I asked a couple questions. That’s not illegal.”

“I didn’t say it was.”

“Then what is this?”

Wade took a step closer. Not aggressive. Controlled.

“It’s me telling you to leave it alone.”

There it was. Clear, direct.

“Why?” she asked.

“Because it’s not your problem.”

“That’s not an answer.”

“It’s the only one you’re getting.”

Shannon held his gaze. “You think I can’t handle it?”

His jaw tightened. “I think you don’t understand what you’re stepping into.”

“Then explain it.”

“No.”

The word landed harder than she expected.

Wade took another step, close enough now that she could see the faint lines around his eyes, the edge of something protective that bordered on command.

“You’re here for a few weeks,” he said. “You help your sister, you get your feet under you, and you move on. That’s the plan.”

“You don’t get to decide my plan.”

“No,” he said. “But I get to decide what becomes my problem.”

“And I’m your problem?”

“You will be if you keep going.”

Silence stretched between them.

Shannon felt the weight of it, the way his presence filled the space without raising his voice.

“Is he dangerous?” she asked.

Wade didn’t answer right away.

“That’s not the right question,” he said finally.

“Then what is?”

“The right question is whether you want to find out.”

Shannon didn’t respond.

Wade studied her for another second, then stepped back.

“Leave it alone, Shannon.”

He got into his car without another word and drove off.

She stood there long after the dust settled.

She didn’t leave it alone.

The first time she saw Eli again, it wasn’t planned.

She’d taken the long way out past the edge of town, the road narrowing into something more suggestion than pavement. Trees crowded in, branches low and uneven, the light breaking through in thin strips.

His truck was pulled off to the side near a fence line.

She almost kept driving.

Almost.

Instead, she slowed, then stopped a few yards ahead. Sat there with the engine running, hands on the wheel.

Then she turned it off.

The quiet settled fast.

When she stepped out, the air felt different—still, but not empty.

Eli stood on the other side of the fence, one hand resting on the top rail. He didn’t look surprised to see her.

“Lost?” he asked.

“No.”

She walked closer, stopping a few feet from the fence. “I was in the area.”

He gave a small nod, like that was enough explanation.

They stood there for a moment, neither speaking.

Up close, he looked the same—steady, self-contained. Nothing in his posture invited conversation, but he didn’t shut it down either.

“I asked around about you,” Shannon said.

“Figured you might.”

“You don’t seem concerned.”

“Should I be?”

“Depends on what I find.”

Eli watched her for a second. “You won’t find anything you can use.”

“That’s not what I’m looking for.”

“What are you looking for?”

She hesitated. “The truth.”

He almost smiled. Not quite.

“That’s a broad target.”

“Then narrow it.”

Eli shifted his weight, glanced out over the land behind him. “You should go home.”

“That’s not an answer.”

“It is,” he said. “Just not one you like.”

She felt a flicker of irritation. “You and Wade should compare notes.”

“He talked to you.”

“Yes.”

“And you’re still here.”

“Yes.”

Eli looked back at her, something unreadable in his expression.

“Then you’re not listening.”

“Or you’re both wrong.”

Silence again.

This time, it stretched longer.

Finally, Eli pushed off the fence. “You done?”

“No.”

“Then you’re going to be.”

He turned, started walking back toward his truck.

Shannon stood there, watching him go.

“Eli,” she said.

He stopped but didn’t turn around.

“You didn’t do it, did you?”

A beat.

“No.”

Simple. Direct.

Then he kept walking.

Shannon stayed where she was until his truck door shut and the engine started.

The sound carried longer than it should have.

Colt Hayes didn’t look like someone who blended into a room.

Shannon noticed him before she meant to.

It was the way he moved—easy, like he took up space without asking for it.

He stood near the counter at the diner, talking to the waitress like they’d known each other for years. Broad shoulders, worn jeans, a shirt that fit just close enough to suggest he didn’t spend his time sitting behind a desk.

Shannon looked away almost immediately.

Not her business.

She slid into a booth across from Emily.

“You’re staring,” Emily said.

“I’m not.”

Emily glanced over her shoulder, then back. “Colt Hayes.”

“Okay.”

“You know who that is?”

“No.”

“You should.”

“I don’t need to.”

Emily studied her for a second, then shrugged. “Suit yourself.”

The waitress came over, and the conversation shifted.

A few minutes later, Colt moved past their table on his way out.

“Emily,” he said, stopping just long enough to nod.

“Colt.”

“Your paperwork came through.”

“Already?”

“Yeah.”

“Thank you.”

“Don’t thank me yet,” he said. “Still got to make it stick.”

She smiled. “I’ll take what I can get.”

He gave a small nod, then glanced at Shannon. Not lingering. Just enough to acknowledge.

“Ma’am.”

Shannon returned the nod. “Evening.”

Then he was gone.

Emily watched him leave, then looked back at Shannon.

“What?”

“Nothing,” Shannon said.

And meant it.

The more Shannon asked, the less clear it became.

Land. That was the common thread.

Disputes that stretched back years, maybe longer. Property lines that didn’t match old maps. Families that claimed pieces of land based on memory instead of paperwork.

Eli’s name sat in the middle of it.

Not loud.

Just consistent.

“He’s got a claim out there,” one man told her outside the feed store. “Old claim. Pre-dates half the records in this county.”

“Then why’s it an issue?”

“Because somebody else wants it.”

“Who?”

The man shrugged. “Depends who you ask.”

It always came back to that.

Depends.

The second time she found Eli, it wasn’t by accident.

She told herself she was just driving.

But she took the same road.

Slowed at the same spot.

This time, his truck was already there.

She parked without thinking about it.

He was closer to the road now, standing by the fence.

“You keep ending up here,” he said as she approached.

“So do you.”

He didn’t argue that.

“You going to tell me what’s really going on?” she asked.

“No.”

“Why not?”

“Because it won’t help you.”

“That’s not your decision.”

“It is when it involves me.”

She stopped a few feet away. Close enough now that the space between them felt intentional.

“I’ve heard about the land,” she said. “The claim.”

Eli’s expression didn’t change. “People talk.”

“Is it yours?”

“Yes.”

“Then why is anyone disputing it?”

He looked at her, steady. “Because they can.”

“That’s not how ownership works.”

“It is here.”

Shannon frowned. “That doesn’t make sense.”

“It doesn’t have to.”

She studied him, trying to read past the surface.

“You’re not telling me everything.”

“No.”

“At least you’re honest about it.”

“I try to be.”

Silence settled again, but it felt different this time. Less like a wall. More like something held in place.

“Why are you still here?” Eli asked.

Shannon blinked. “What?”

“You asked your questions. You got your answers.”

“I didn’t.”

“You got enough.”

“Not for me.”

He watched her for a moment, then nodded slightly, like he’d reached a decision.

“My grandfather filed that claim,” he said. “Before the county redrew half the lines out here.”

Shannon waited.

“It was never contested,” he continued. “Not until a few years ago.”

“Why then?”

“Because the land started to matter.”

“How?”

Eli didn’t answer right away.

“There’s water under it,” he said finally. “Good access. Runs year-round.”

Shannon felt something click into place.

“And now people want it.”

“Yes.”

“Who?”

Eli shook his head. “Doesn’t matter.”

“It does to me.”

Shannon met his gaze.

“You’re very certain about what I need.”

“I’m certain about what’s best.”

“For who.”

A flicker.

Brief.

Then gone.

“For you.”

The answer came too easily.

Like it had already been decided.

Shannon looked away, out toward the road.

Cattle don’t disappear without someone gaining something.

The thought slipped in uninvited.

She frowned slightly.

“What happens to the land?” she asked.

Wade’s attention sharpened.

“If he’s convicted.”

“Yeah.”

“It’ll get tied up. Probably sold off to cover damages.”

“Who buys it.”

He watched her.

“Depends.”

“On what.”

“Who’s interested.”

She nodded slowly.

“And who’s interested?”

A longer pause.

“Why are you asking that?”

Shannon turned back to him.

“Because it feels like someone should be.”

Wade’s jaw tightened.

“Shannon.”

“Who benefits from him being out of the way.”

“That’s not how this works.”

“It is, though.”

“No,” he said, firmer now. “It’s not.”

She held his gaze.

“Then tell me who loses.”

Silence.

Just long enough.

That was all she needed.

Not an answer.

A hesitation.

A small break in the structure he’d been holding so tightly.

Shannon’s mind moved, pieces shifting.

The neighbor’s testimony.

The timing.

The chain.

The way everything lined up just clean enough.

Too clean.

“Timeline,” she said, more to herself than to him.

“What.”

She looked back toward the courthouse.

“He fixed the chain the next morning.”

“Yeah.”

“But the cattle were already gone.”

“That’s the claim.”

“So what was the chain for.”

“To cover his tracks.”

“Or,” she said slowly, “to keep something out.”

Wade’s expression hardened.

“You’re reaching.”

“Maybe.”

She took a step back, creating space.

“Or maybe I’m just looking at it different.”

“You’re looking at it wrong.”

“That’s your opinion.”

“It’s my job.”

“And it’s my life.”

The words came out before she could soften them.

They hung there between them.

Wade straightened slightly.

“You need to let this go,” he said.

Not a suggestion.

A conclusion.

Shannon nodded once.

“I hear you.”

It wasn’t agreement.

He knew that.

They both did.

He held her gaze for another second, then picked up his hat.

“Stay out of it,” he said.

Then he turned and walked away.

Shannon stood there, the pieces still moving in her head.

Not settled.

Not clear.

But no longer aligned the way they had been inside that courtroom.

Something was off.

She didn’t know what yet.

But she could feel it.

And that was enough.

The courtroom didn’t feel like a place where anything changed.

Shannon had been in it enough to know that. The same benches. Same scuffed rail. Same tired hum of the air unit fighting the Mississippi heat. Even the same handful of faces that seemed to orbit every hearing—men in work boots, women with folded arms, a few people who came just to watch.

Still, something had shifted.

She could feel it before the judge spoke.

Eli stood at the defense table, hands loose at his sides. No suit. Same worn shirt he’d worn the last time. Nothing about him suggested he expected anything different today.

Emily sat beside Shannon, a legal pad balanced on her knee. She’d been quieter this morning, focused in a way that meant she’d seen something in the file Shannon hadn’t.

“What changed?” Shannon asked under her breath.

Emily didn’t look at her. “Chain of custody issue. One of the deputies logged evidence late. It’s enough.”

“Enough for what?”

“Enough to make the prosecutor nervous.”

Shannon glanced toward Wade.

He stood near the back wall, not in uniform today but close enough—pressed shirt, badge at his belt. Arms crossed. Watching everything without looking like he was watching anything.

When his eyes flicked toward her, it was brief. Not a warning. Not approval. Just acknowledgment.

The judge cleared his throat.

The prosecutor stood. Papers shuffled. He started talking, and Shannon caught the tone before the words.

Measured. Controlled. Adjusting.

“…in light of newly reviewed procedural concerns…”

Emily’s pen paused.

“…the State is prepared to amend the charge…”

Shannon felt it land before it finished forming.

Not innocent.

Not guilty.

Just… less.

The words came out clean: reduction. Misdemeanor. Time served. Release pending administrative processing.

The room didn’t react all at once. It shifted in pieces. A cough. A chair leg scraping. Someone muttering under their breath.

Shannon didn’t look at Eli right away.

She watched the judge instead. The way he nodded, like this was routine. Like nothing about this mattered beyond clearing the docket.

Then she looked.

Eli hadn’t moved.

No relief. No tension leaving his shoulders. Just stillness.

Like he’d expected it.

Chapter 5

Outside, the courthouse steps were bright enough to make her squint.

People filtered out in loose groups. Conversations already forming, already reshaping what had just happened into something cleaner, easier to carry.

Emily stayed inside a few minutes longer, finishing something with the clerk.

Shannon stood near the railing, arms folded against the heat. The air smelled like dust and sun-warmed concrete.

Eli came out alone.

No one approached him. Not directly. But she could feel the attention shift toward him anyway. Side glances. Quick looks away.

He paused at the bottom of the steps, like he wasn’t sure which direction to take.

Then he saw her.

There was a beat where neither of them moved.

Shannon stepped down first.

“You’re out,” she said.

He nodded. “Looks that way.”

“That’s it?”

“For today.”

She studied him. “You don’t sound surprised.”

“I’m not.”

“Did you know?”

“No.”

“Then why—”

He shrugged, small. “It was getting thin.”

That wasn’t an answer. Or maybe it was.

She glanced back at the courthouse doors. Wade hadn’t come out yet.

“People aren’t going to see it that way,” she said.

Eli followed her gaze, then looked back at her. “They don’t have to.”

“That’s not how it works here.”

“I know.”

A truck drove past, slow. The driver looked at Eli, then at Shannon, then back to the road.

Shannon felt it settle in her chest.

“You’re not cleared,” she said.

“No.”

“You know that.”

“Yeah.”

“And you’re just… fine with it?”

He held her eyes. “No.”

It was the first honest thing that had weight in it.

She let that sit.

“You could’ve fought harder,” she said.

“For what?”

“For something cleaner than this.”

“There isn’t one.”

The certainty in his voice was quiet. Not defensive. Just final.

Shannon looked away.

She’d spent the last weeks pushing, asking, turning things over. Trying to find the clean version of the story.

It wasn’t there.

“You’re free,” she said, softer.

“For now.”

“That’s not nothing.”

“No.”

He didn’t say thank you.

She didn’t expect him to.

By the time Emily came out, Eli was already walking toward the parking lot.

“Wait,” Shannon called.

He stopped.

Emily jogged down the steps, slightly out of breath. “Paperwork’s going to take a few hours. They’ll clear you through the back.”

Eli nodded.

She looked between them, reading something she didn’t comment on. “You’ll need to sign a couple things before you go.”

“Alright.”

“I’ll meet you inside.”

He started back toward the building.

Shannon stayed where she was.

Emily lingered a second.

“You going to say something?” she asked quietly.

“I don’t know what to say.”

Emily studied her. “Then don’t say anything yet.”

Shannon let out a breath she hadn’t realized she was holding.

“You think he did it?” she asked.

Emily didn’t answer right away.

“No,” she said finally. “But I don’t think he’s clean either.”

“That’s not helpful.”

“It’s not supposed to be.”

Emily touched her arm, brief. “This place doesn’t do clean.”

Then she went back inside.

Shannon stayed on the steps a while longer.

Watching people leave.

Watching the town absorb what had happened and turn it into something else.

By afternoon, the story had already changed.

At the grocery store, two women at the end of the aisle lowered their voices when Shannon passed.

“…technicality…”

“…doesn’t mean he didn’t—”

“…you know how it is…”

Shannon kept walking.

At the register, the cashier hesitated before making eye contact.

“You hear about that case?” she asked, too casually.

“I was there,” Shannon said.

The cashier blinked. “Oh.”

“That all you needed?”

The woman flushed. “I didn’t mean—”

“It’s fine.”

Shannon took her receipt and left.

Outside, the air felt heavier.

Not because of the heat.

Because nothing had changed.

Eli was gone from the jail by the time Shannon drove past.

No one told her where he went.

She didn’t ask.

Not right away.

She went home instead.

The house was quiet in a way that made her aware of every small sound—the refrigerator kicking on, the faint creak of the floor when she shifted her weight.

She sat at the kitchen table, hands wrapped around a glass of water she wasn’t drinking.

Her phone buzzed once.

Emily.

He’s out. Went toward the highway.

That was it.

Shannon stared at the message.

Toward the highway.

Not home.

Not into town.

Out.

She set the phone down.

Then picked it back up.

Then set it down again.

It was close to evening when she saw him.

Not by accident.

Not exactly.

The gas station at the edge of town had two pumps that worked and one that didn’t. Eli stood at the working one, leaning against his truck while the tank filled.

The light had started to soften. Long shadows stretching across the lot.

Shannon pulled in slow.

He saw her before she shut off the engine.

Didn’t wave.

Didn’t look away.

Just waited.

She got out.

“You leaving?” she asked.

“Not yet.”

“Looks like it.”

He glanced at the pump. “Just getting gas.”

“For where?”

He didn’t answer.

She stepped closer.

“You going to tell me?”

“Eventually.”

“That’s not an answer.”

“It’s the one I’ve got.”

The pump clicked.

He pulled the nozzle out, set it back in place.

Shannon watched his hands. Steady. Controlled.

“You could’ve come by,” she said.

“For what?”

“To say something.”

“I’m saying something now.”

“That’s not the same.”

“No.”

He closed the gas cap, wiped his hands on a rag from the bed of the truck.

Shannon felt irritation rise, sharper than she expected.

“You’re just going to walk out like it doesn’t matter?”

He looked at her then.

“It matters.”

“Then act like it.”

He held her gaze for a long second.

Then: “What do you want me to do?”

“I don’t know,” she said, and it came out more honest than she intended. “Something.”

He leaned back against the truck.

“I don’t do things for show,” he said.

“This isn’t a show.”

“No,” he agreed. “It’s worse.”

She let that settle.

“You’re not going to stay,” she said.

It wasn’t a question.

He shook his head once.

“Why?”

He looked out toward the road. Cars passing, headlights starting to come on.

“This place…” He stopped.

She waited.

He didn’t finish the sentence.

“It’s what?” she pressed.

He looked back at her.

“It holds onto things.”

“That’s vague.”

“It’s accurate.”

She crossed her arms.

“People live here,” she said.

“I know.”

“They make it work.”

“Some do.”

“And the rest?”

“They don’t leave.”

The way he said it wasn’t judgment.

It was observation.

Shannon felt something shift inside her.

“You think I’m stuck here,” she said.

“I think you’re here.”

“That’s not the same.”

“No,” he said. “It’s not.”

A silence stretched between them.

Not empty.

Just full of things neither of them was saying.

They ended up sitting on the edge of the truck bed.

No plan. No decision. Just the easiest place to stop moving.

The sun dropped lower. The sky turning a dull gold that made everything look softer than it was.

Shannon rested her hands on her knees.

“You could’ve told me more,” she said.

“About what?”

“About you. About… all of it.”

He considered that.

“Would it have changed anything?”

“Yes.”

“How?”

“I would’ve known what I was dealing with.”

“You knew enough.”

“That’s not the same as the truth.”

“No,” he said. “It’s not.”

She turned to look at him.

“So what is the truth?”

He met her eyes.

“I’ve done things I wouldn’t put in a report.”

Her stomach tightened.

“That’s not an answer.”

“It’s the one I’ve got.”

“You didn’t steal those cattle.”

“No.”

“But you’re not… innocent.”

“No.”

The word landed heavier than the others.

She looked away.

Cars passed. Someone laughed near the store entrance. Life moving around them like this wasn’t happening.

“You ever think about staying?” she asked.

“Here?”

“Anywhere.”

He huffed a quiet breath. “Sometimes.”

“And?”

“It doesn’t stick.”

“Why not?”

He didn’t answer right away.

Then: “Because I know how it ends.”

“How?”

“Same way it always does.”

“That’s not very hopeful.”

“No.”

She let that sit.

“You’re wrong about one thing,” she said.

“What’s that?”

“People can change.”

He looked at her.

“I didn’t say they couldn’t.”

“You implied it.”

“I said places don’t.”

The distinction hung there.

Shannon felt it.

Uncomfortable because it might be true.

The quiet stretched until it wasn’t quiet anymore.

Until it felt like something needed to break.

She stood.

He watched her.

“You’re really leaving,” she said.

“Yes.”

“When?”

“Soon.”

“That’s not a time.”

“It’s the one I’ve got.”

She let out a breath that was almost a laugh.

“You do that on purpose,” she said.

“What?”

“Answer without answering.”

“It saves time.”

“It wastes it.”

He didn’t argue.

She took a step closer.

“You’re not going to ask me to come with you,” she said.

“No.”

“Why not?”

“Because you wouldn’t.”

“You don’t know that.”

“I do.”

The certainty in his voice wasn’t dismissive.

It was grounded.

She searched his face for something softer.

Found it, but it was buried.

“You could ask anyway,” she said.

He shook his head.

“That’s not fair.”

“To who?”

“To you.”

She almost smiled at that.

“You don’t get to decide what’s fair for me.”

“No,” he said. “But I can decide what I do.”

“And what do you do?”

“I leave.”

The simplicity of it hit harder than anything else.

They didn’t say goodbye at the gas station.

They just… paused.

Then she got in her car.

And he got in his truck.

And they drove in opposite directions.

Chapter 6

The town didn’t change the next day.

Or the day after.

Shannon went back to her routine because there wasn’t anything else to do.

Grocery store. Coffee. Short conversations that stayed on the surface.

But everything felt slightly off.

Like the shape of things had shifted just enough to notice.

At the diner, Wade sat in his usual booth.

He didn’t wave her over.

She sat anyway.

“You’re busy,” she said.

“Always.”

He set his coffee down.

“He’s out,” he added.

“I know.”

“You went to see him.”

It wasn’t a question.

“Yes.”

Wade studied her.

“You need to be careful,” he said.

“About what?”

“About how people see that.”

“I don’t care.”

“You should.”

“Why?”

“Because it matters.”

“To who?”

“To everyone.”

She leaned back.

“That sounds like a problem for everyone.”

“It becomes a problem for you.”

His tone hadn’t changed.

Still controlled. Still even.

But there was something under it now.

Edge.

“I’m not doing anything wrong,” she said.

“That’s not how it works.”

“I’m starting to notice that.”

He held her gaze.

“You think the system’s broken,” he said.

“I think it’s… convenient.”

“For who?”

“For whoever’s holding it.”

Wade didn’t react.

“You want to fix it?” he asked.

“I want to understand it.”

“That’s the same thing.”

“No,” she said. “It’s not.”

A pause.

“You’re getting close to something you don’t understand,” he said.

“That sounds familiar.”

“It should.”

She let that sit.

“You ever think about leaving?” she asked.

The question seemed to catch him off guard.

“No,” he said.

“Why not?”

“Because this is where I’m needed.”

“That’s one way to look at it.”

“It’s the right way.”

She watched him.

“Or it’s the only way you’ve let yourself see it.”

His jaw tightened.

“Careful,” he said.

“There it is,” she replied.

“What?”

“The part where it stops being a conversation.”

He didn’t deny it.

“Some things aren’t up for debate,” he said.

“Everything is.”

“Not here.”

She stood.

“Maybe that’s the problem.”

Wade’s eyes followed her.

“You think you’re different,” he said.

“I think I’m paying attention.”

“That won’t protect you.”

“I’m not asking it to.”

She left before he could respond.

Eli didn’t come back.

Not that week.

Not the next.

People stopped talking about the case as much.

Which meant they’d already decided what they thought.

Shannon heard it in pieces.

“…got off on a technicality…”

“…always knew something wasn’t right…”

“…you can’t trust—”

She stopped listening.

The call came on a quiet afternoon.

Unknown number.

She almost didn’t answer.

“Shannon.”

His voice was the same.

“Where are you?” she asked.

“Out.”

“That’s helpful.”

“It’s not far.”

A pause.

“I wanted to see you,” he said.

Her chest tightened.

“Why?”

“Before I go.”

“Where?”

He gave her a location.

A stretch of road outside town. Near the old bridge.

She knew it.

“I’ll be there,” she said.

The place was quiet when she arrived.

The bridge wasn’t used much anymore. Newer road a mile over had taken most of the traffic.

The water below moved slow.

Eli’s truck was already there.

He stood by the railing, looking out.

Shannon parked and walked over.

“You always pick places like this?” she asked.

He glanced at her.

“They’re quieter.”

She leaned against the rail beside him.

For a while, neither of them spoke.

The sound of the water filled the space.

“You’re really going,” she said finally.

“Yes.”

“When?”

“Tomorrow.”

That landed.

“Early?”

“Before it gets busy.”

She nodded.

“Where to?”

He gave her the name of a town she’d heard of but never been to.

“Job?”

“Yes.”

“Doing what?”

“Work.”

She almost smiled.

“You’re not going to make this easy.”

“No.”

She looked out at the water.

“You think this place traps people,” she said.

“I think it keeps them.”

“That’s not the same.”

“It is when you don’t notice.”

She let that settle.

“You think I don’t notice.”

“I think you’re starting to.”

She turned to him.

“And that’s why you’re leaving?”

He met her eyes.

“That’s part of it.”

“What’s the other part?”

He didn’t answer.

She studied him.

“You don’t trust yourself here,” she said.

It wasn’t a guess.

He didn’t deny it.

“That’s closer,” he said.

The honesty in it was quiet.

Real.

Shannon felt something shift again.

“You could stay,” she said.

“I could.”

“And?”

“I won’t.”

“Because?”

He looked back at the water.

“Because it’s easier to leave than to fight something that doesn’t change.”

She absorbed that.

“And me?” she asked.

He didn’t look at her.

“You’ll figure it out.”

“That’s not an answer.”

“It’s the one I’ve got.”

She stepped closer.

“That’s not good enough.”

He turned then.

Close enough now that she could see the small details she’d missed before. The faint line at his jaw. The way his eyes held steady even when he wasn’t saying everything.

“It’s what I can give you,” he said.

She held his gaze.

“Then I’ll take it.”

The words surprised her as much as him.

Something shifted between them.

Not new.

Just clearer.

The kiss wasn’t sudden.

It built in the space between them.

In the way neither of them stepped back.

In the way the air seemed to hold still for a second longer than it should have.

He moved first.

Slow.

Giving her time to stop him.

She didn’t.

His hand came up, light against her face.

Not claiming.

Just there.

The kiss itself was… quiet.

No urgency.

No need to prove anything.

Just contact.

Real.

Grounded.

She felt it in the way he didn’t push.

In the way he held the moment instead of rushing through it.

When he pulled back, it wasn’t abrupt.

Just enough to breathe.

Their foreheads stayed close.

Shannon didn’t move away.

Neither of them spoke.

There wasn’t anything to say that wouldn’t make it smaller.

After a moment, she stepped back.

“You’re not going to ask me again,” she said.

“No.”

“You’re not going to stay.”

“No.”

She nodded once.

“Alright.”

It wasn’t acceptance.

But it wasn’t resistance either.

He watched her like he was memorizing something he didn’t expect to see again.

“Take care of yourself,” he said.

“You too.”

A pause.

Then, quieter: “Don’t get stuck.”

She almost smiled.

“You don’t get to tell me that.”

“I know.”

He didn’t apologize for it.

She didn’t ask him to.

They didn’t linger.

That was the part that made it real.

He walked back to his truck.

She stayed by the railing.

She didn’t watch him get in.

Didn’t watch him start the engine.

But she heard it.

Felt it in the way the quiet shifted.

When the sound faded, she turned.

The truck was already gone.

The next morning came the same way it always did.

Light through the blinds.

Coffee brewing.

The town waking up in slow, familiar rhythms.

But something was different.

Not outside.

Inside.

Shannon stood at the kitchen sink, looking out at the yard.

Same fence. Same tree line. Same stretch of road beyond it.

Nothing had moved.

And yet.

She picked up her phone.

No new messages.

No missed calls.

She set it back down.

At the diner, Wade was already there.

Same booth. Same coffee.

He looked up when she walked in.

Didn’t ask.

Didn’t need to.

“He’s gone,” he said.

“Yes.”

Wade nodded once.

“That’s probably for the best.”

“For who?”

“For you.”

She slid into the seat across from him.

“You always decide that?” she asked.

“Most of the time.”

“That must be convenient.”

“It is.”

He took a sip of his coffee.

“You’re still here,” he added.

“I noticed.”

“That matters.”

“Why?”

“Because this place doesn’t change.”

The words echoed something she’d heard before.

She held his gaze.

“Maybe I do,” she said.

Wade studied her.

Something unreadable passed through his expression.

“Maybe,” he allowed.

A pause.

“You going to stay out of trouble?” he asked.

“No.”

That earned the faintest shift at the corner of his mouth.

“Thought so.”

The town kept moving.

It always did.

People went to work. Kids got on buses. Conversations rose and fell and reshaped themselves around whatever was easiest to hold.

Eli’s name came up less.

Then not at all.

Which didn’t mean anything had been resolved.

Just buried.

Shannon noticed things she hadn’t before.

The way certain doors stayed closed.

The way certain people stopped talking when she got too close.

The way Wade’s presence carried weight even when he wasn’t saying anything.

It wasn’t new.

She just hadn’t been looking at it straight on.

One evening, she found herself back on the same road near the bridge.

No reason.

Just… there.

She parked in the same spot.

Walked to the railing.

The water moved the same way it had before.

Slow. Steady. Unconcerned.

She rested her hands against the metal.

Cool under her palms.

For a second, she let herself imagine a different version of things.

Him staying.

Or her leaving.

Or something in between.

It didn’t hold.

Not because it wasn’t possible.

Because it wasn’t what happened.

She let it go.

A truck passed on the distant road.

For a second, her chest tightened.

Then it didn’t.

She exhaled.

The feeling didn’t disappear.

It just… settled.

Less sharp.

More like something she could carry.

When she turned back toward her car, the town stretched out in front of her.

Same as it had always been.

Same roads.

Same houses.

Same people who would keep doing what they’d always done.

The system still there.

Wade still part of it.

Watching. Holding. Deciding.

That hadn’t changed.

But she had.

Not in a way anyone else would see right away.

Not in a way that would announce itself.

Just in the way she stood.

The way she looked at things.

The way she didn’t look away as quickly.

She got in her car.

Started the engine.

Sat there for a second longer than necessary.

Then pulled onto the road.

Not leaving.

Not staying the same.

Just… moving forward.

And aware, now, that whatever her role had been in this place—

it wasn’t that anymore.

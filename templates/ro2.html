Chapter One

Shannon woke before the alarm, the way she had started doing sometime after the divorce — as if her body no longer trusted anything external to keep time. The room was still dark, gray light pressing through the curtains, the kind of morning that hadn't decided what it was yet.

Nothing in the room asked anything of her.

That was the problem.

She carried her coffee out to the small porch off the side of Emily's house. The air had that early-spring edge — cool but not sharp, carrying the smell of turned dirt and something starting back up. Fields stretched out behind the property, low and open, bordered by a fence that leaned just enough to suggest it had been leaning for a while.

There had been a time when mornings belonged to something. A schedule. A classroom. A list of names she knew without looking at a sheet.

Now it was just her, and the hours, and whatever she decided to do with them.

By midmorning, she was at the diner — a low brick building just off the main road, faded sign unchanged since she was a kid. Same booths, same scuffed floor, same Carol.

Shannon slid into a booth near the window. Carol brought her a second cup without asking, said "You look awake" like it was news, and moved on.

The bell over the door rang.

She didn't look up at first. People came in, people went out. It wasn't anything that needed attention.

But the room shifted.

It was small — just a subtle tightening in the way voices dropped half a notch, the way Carol glanced toward the door before catching herself. Shannon looked up.

Deputy Wade Carter stood just inside, hat in hand, scanning the room the way he always did — not in a hurry, but not casual either. He had that same posture she remembered from high school, from the years after, from every interaction they'd had since she'd come back: shoulders squared, movements controlled, like he was aware of space in a way other people weren't.

He saw her.

Of course he did. His gaze settled for a fraction of a second, something like recognition softening it. Then it was gone, replaced by something more official.

He crossed the room.

"Shannon."

"Wade."

He stopped beside the booth, one hand resting lightly on the back of the seat across from her. "Mind?"

She shrugged a shoulder. "Free country."

That got the smallest shift at the corner of his mouth. He slid in across from her.

Up close, he looked the same in the ways that mattered. Older, maybe, lines a little more settled, but still contained. He'd always been good at contained.

"You been back long?" he asked.

"Few months."

He nodded like he'd already known that. "Heard something about that."

"People hear things."

"They do."

Carol came over, poured him coffee without asking. He thanked her, waited until she moved off.

"You settling in?" he asked.

"Settled enough."

"That mean yes or no?"

"It means I'm here."

He studied her for a second, not pushing, but not letting it pass either. That was one of the things about Wade — he left space where most people filled it. She'd found it attractive once. Now it just felt like a technique.

"Teaching again?" he asked.

"No."

He didn't ask why.

"Doing what, then?"

"Figuring that out."

He nodded once, like that was a reasonable answer. Silence settled between them, not uncomfortable. Just present.

"You still out on your place?" she asked.

"Yeah. Nothing changed."

"Not much does," she said.

There was something in the way he received that — not agreement, exactly. More like satisfaction.

The bell over the door rang again.

This time, the shift in the room was sharper. Conversations cut off mid-sentence. Chairs scraped. Someone said something low and quick near the counter.

Wade's attention moved before his body did, his head turning toward the door, posture tightening just enough to notice.

Two men stepped in, talking fast, one of them shaking his head.

"...they picked him up this morning—"

"—no way—"

"—I'm telling you. Turner. Eli Turner."

The name landed in the room like a stone dropped in still water. Wade was already moving, sliding out of the booth.

"What's going on," he said. Not a question. A directive.

The men looked at him, one gesturing back toward the road. "Sheriff's got Eli Turner. Cattle theft. Up on the Hargrove place."

The room stilled.

Wade's expression didn't change much. But something in it settled — like a piece had dropped into place.

"When?" he asked.

"Early. Before sunup."

He nodded once. "Appreciate it." Already turning. He glanced back at Shannon, just briefly.

"Stay out of it," he said.

Quiet. Almost casual. But it wasn't a suggestion.

Then he was gone.

By the time Shannon stepped out of the diner, the town had already started turning the news over in its hands.

Clusters of people stood in small groups along the sidewalk, talking low and fast. Trucks idled longer than they needed to. Fragments caught her as she passed.

"—Hargroves don't play around—"

"—they wouldn't bring it unless it was solid—"

"—he's always kept to himself—"

"—doesn't matter—"

She opened her car door, paused, hand on the frame.

Too clean.

The thought came uninvited. She didn't know why. She filed it away and drove.

Emily's office sat on the second floor of a building just off the square. Shannon took the stairs two at a time.

The door was open. Emily had the phone to her ear, the cord stretched toward the window like she'd been pacing before she made herself stop. She held up a finger at Shannon and kept talking.

"I understand that, but I'm asking what was filed, not what's going to be filed — yes — okay. Call me back."

She hung up. Looked at Shannon. "You heard."

"Hard not to."

Emily moved back to her desk, dropped into her chair. "I'm working with fragments right now. Courthouse won't have anything official until this afternoon at the earliest. What I know is what Carol told me, what I just got off the phone with the clerk's assistant, and what I overheard outside."

"Put it together."

"Sheriff's office picked him up this morning. Charges tied to cattle taken off the Hargrove property — not just this week, apparently, they're claiming it's been ongoing. They say they have records. Sales logs, movement."

"Where'd the records come from?"

"That's what I'm trying to find out." Emily tapped the desk. "Hargrove filed a complaint three weeks ago. So either the investigation moved fast, or someone had the documentation ready to go."

Shannon crossed her arms. "That doesn't sound like him."

Emily glanced up. "You know him?"

"Not really."

"Then—"

"It doesn't sound like him," Shannon said again.

Emily was quiet for a moment. "The Hargroves are pushing this. That matters."

"In what way."

"In the way that means it's not going away."

"What do you think?" Shannon asked.

Emily considered it with the particular care she used when she wasn't sure yet. "I think whatever's in those records, somebody prepared them with purpose."

"That's not the same thing as him being guilty."

"No," Emily said. "It's not."

They let that sit between them.

Emily studied her. "You're already interested."

"I'm asking questions."

"That's how it starts with you."

"Shannon." Emily leaned back. "Be careful."

"Of what."

"Of getting pulled into something you don't need to be in."

Shannon considered that. "Seems like I already am," she said. Then she left.

The courthouse sat at the center of town — white columns, steps worn smooth from years of use.

By the time Shannon got there, a small crowd had gathered. Not large, but tight. The kind that watched more than it spoke.

A sheriff's vehicle pulled up just as she reached the edge of the steps.

The back door opened.

Eli Turner stepped out.

He wasn't what she expected.

Not in any dramatic way. He just didn't match the shape of the story people were already building.

He was calm. Not defiant. Not broken. Steady — in a way that suggested it cost him nothing, which was either the sign of an innocent man or someone who had practiced appearing like one. She couldn't tell which. That bothered her more than the charges.

He was tall, broader through the shoulders than she'd expected, with the kind of stillness that didn't come from ease. It came from discipline.

His hands were cuffed in front of him. He moved when directed, stopped when told. His gaze lifted and scanned the space ahead of him.

It passed over the crowd without catching.

Then, for a moment, it landed on Shannon.

It didn't linger. But it didn't flinch, either. No plea in it. No attempt to connect. Just acknowledgment — the way you'd acknowledge a stranger who was watching you too closely.

Then it moved on.

A deputy guided him toward the steps. "Watch your footing," the man said. Eli nodded once, like he'd heard him.

They went inside.

The door closed.

Shannon stayed where she was.

That look.

It wasn't what someone looked like when they were about to fight for their life. Or maybe it was. Just not in the way she expected.

Chapter Two

Shannon didn't mean to start asking questions. It came out sideways, the way most things did — slipped into conversation, light enough to pass as idle curiosity.

At the Piggly Wiggly, she mentioned his name to the cashier. Got a flicker of recognition, then something like deliberate dismissal.

At the hardware store, old Mr. Blevins didn't pause at all. "Everybody's heard of Eli Turner," he said, and slid her a bag with a box of nails she didn't need. He met her eyes at the last second. "You staying out on your sister's place?"

"For now."

He nodded once, like that confirmed something. "Then you don't need to be worrying about him."

"Why not?"

"Because it ain't your business." His tone wasn't sharp. Just settled. "And because folks around here got long memories."

Shannon folded her receipt without looking at it. "That doesn't answer the question."

"It does," he said. "Just not the way you want."

She drove back to Emily's place with the same thread pulling at her — not fear, not urgency. Just the quiet, persistent feeling that something didn't fit.

Recognition.

And underneath it, something closer to caution.

Emily was in the kitchen when Shannon walked in, phone wedged between her shoulder and ear, flipping through paperwork.

"I'll call you back," Emily said when she saw Shannon. "Yeah. I'll check on it."

She hung up and looked at Shannon with the expression she reserved for situations she'd already decided not to like. "You've got that look."

"What look."

"The one where you've decided something and haven't told me yet."

"I asked around about Eli Turner."

Emily froze for half a second. "And?"

"Depends who you ask."

"That's not comforting."

"No."

Emily pushed off the counter, crossed her arms. "Shannon."

"I'm not doing anything."

"That's not what it looks like."

"I asked a couple questions."

"That's how it starts."

Shannon met her eyes. "You don't think it's strange?"

"I think it's none of our business."

"That's not what you said yesterday."

Emily hesitated. "Yesterday he was just a guy in a file. Today you're walking around town saying his name out loud." She sighed. "Trouble doesn't need you to look for it."

Wade was already waiting when Shannon stepped out onto the porch that afternoon.

He leaned against his patrol car like he owned the road it sat on. She stopped at the top of the steps.

"You always this subtle?" she asked.

"Depends who I'm dealing with."

She came down the steps slowly. "What's this about?"

Wade straightened. Something tight in his expression, but controlled. "I heard you've been asking about Eli Turner."

"Small town."

"Small enough," he said.

Shannon crossed her arms. "I asked a couple questions. That's not illegal."

"I didn't say it was."

"Then what is this?"

Wade took a step closer. Not aggressive. Controlled. Everything about him was controlled. "It's me telling you to leave it alone."

"Why?"

"Because it's not your problem."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the only one you're getting."

She held his gaze. She'd known Wade Carter most of her life — had loved him, once, or something close enough to it that the distinction hadn't mattered at the time. Junior and senior year of high school, then again for almost two years after, the kind of on-again-off-again that people in small towns do when they don't know how to finish things and can't quite leave them alone. He'd asked her to stay when she left for college. She hadn't. That had been the first real ending, though not the last time they'd circled back to each other when one of them was between something else and not paying close enough attention. The last time had been seven years ago, right before she'd met the man she'd married. A few months after that, Wade had married Lena Pruitt — the daughter of Ray Pruitt, who ran cattle operations on the eastern side of the county and sat on half the boards worth sitting on. Shannon had heard about the wedding from Emily. She'd sent a card and not thought about it much. Or told herself she hadn't.

She'd watched him become exactly the kind of man people in this town trusted because he never surprised them. Solid. Certain. Lena's husband. Ray Pruitt's son-in-law. The deputy who knew where everything was and how it worked and why it had to stay that way.

That had been the attractive thing once. Now she noticed the other side of it. The way solidity could look, from certain angles, like a wall.

"You think I can't handle it?" she asked.

His jaw tightened. "I think you don't understand what you're stepping into."

"Then explain it."

"No."

The word landed harder than she expected. He stepped closer — close enough now that she could see the faint lines around his eyes, the edge of something protective that bordered on command.

"You're here for a few weeks," he said. "You help your sister, you get your feet under you, and you move on. That's the plan."

"You don't get to decide my plan."

"No," he said. "But I get to decide what becomes my problem."

"And I'm your problem?"

"You will be if you keep going."

Shannon felt the weight of it — the way his presence filled the space without raising his voice. She recognized what this was. She'd spent eleven years married to a man who used the same architecture. Calm on the surface. Immovable underneath.

She'd thought it was strength, then.

She'd learned the difference.

"Is he dangerous?" she asked.

Wade didn't answer right away. "That's not the right question."

"Then what is?"

"The right question is whether you want to find out."

Shannon didn't respond. Wade studied her a second longer, then stepped back.

"Leave it alone, Shannon."

He got into his car without another word and drove off.

She stood there long after the dust settled.

She didn't leave it alone.

The first time she saw Eli again, it wasn't planned. She'd taken the long way out past the edge of town — told herself she was driving, that she just needed air — and somehow ended up on the road that ran along the Turner property fence line.

His truck was pulled off to the side.

She almost kept going. Almost.

Instead, she slowed, then stopped a few yards ahead. Sat there with the engine running. Then turned it off.

When she stepped out, the air felt different out here. Stiller. Less managed.

Eli stood on the other side of the fence, one hand resting on the top rail. He didn't look surprised to see her. He looked the way he'd looked coming down those courthouse steps — calm in a way that didn't read as comfortable. More like someone who'd decided a long time ago that reacting was its own kind of surrender.

"Lost?" he asked.

"No."

She walked closer, stopping a few feet from the fence. "I was in the area."

A small nod. Like that was enough explanation, or like he didn't believe it and didn't bother saying so.

"I asked around about you," Shannon said.

"Figured you might."

"You don't seem concerned."

"Should I be?"

"Depends on what I find."

Eli watched her. "You won't find anything you can use."

"That's not what I'm looking for."

"What are you looking for?"

She hesitated. "The truth."

He almost smiled. Not quite. "That's a broad target."

"Then narrow it."

Eli shifted his weight, glanced out over the land behind him. The light was low, the fields going gold. He looked like he belonged to this land in a way people usually only looked when they didn't know anyone was watching.

"You should go home," he said.

"That's not an answer."

"It is." He looked back at her. "Just not one you like."

She felt a flicker of irritation. "You and Wade should compare notes."

Something shifted in his expression at that. Brief. "He talked to you."

"Yes."

"And you're still here."

"Yes."

He held her gaze. "Then you're not listening."

"Or you're both wrong."

Silence. This time it stretched longer. The breeze moved through the grass on his side of the fence. She could smell the land from here — hay, something faint and mineral, something that was just him. She noticed that and told herself not to.

Finally, Eli pushed off the fence. "You done?"

"No."

"Then you're going to be."

He turned, started walking back toward his truck. Shannon stayed where she was.

"Eli," she said.

He stopped but didn't turn around.

"You didn't do it, did you?"

A beat. Long enough that she thought he wasn't going to answer.

"No."

Simple. Direct. Like it was the easiest thing he'd said to her.

Then he kept walking.

Shannon stayed where she was until his truck door shut and the engine started. The sound carried longer than it should have.

She stood there after it faded too.

The more she asked, the less clean it became.

Land. That was the common thread. Disputes that stretched back years, maybe longer. Property lines that didn't match old maps. Families that claimed pieces based on memory instead of paperwork.

Eli's name sat in the middle of it — not loud, just consistent.

"He's got a claim out there," one man told her outside the feed store. "Old claim. Pre-dates half the records in this county."

"Then why's it an issue?"

"Because somebody else wants it."

"Who?"

The man shrugged. "Depends who you ask."

It always came back to that.

She was at the diner with Emily when she first noticed Colt Hayes.

Not because she was looking. That was the thing. She'd been looking at the window, at the square outside, at nothing in particular — and then he walked in and the room made a small, involuntary adjustment the way rooms did around certain people, and she looked.

Tall, easy in his body, the kind of man who took up exactly the space he needed and didn't apologize for it. Dark hair, work-worn hands that didn't match the sharpness of everything else about him. He stopped at the counter to say something to Carol that made her laugh, and he did it without performing anything.

Shannon looked away. Picked up her coffee.

Across the table, Emily hadn't looked up from her phone.

"Who's that?" Shannon asked, keeping her voice flat.

Emily glanced toward the counter without urgency. "Colt Hayes." Back to her phone. "We went to school together."

"What does he do?"

"Title work, mostly. Some land transaction stuff. He knows where every record in this county is filed and who filed it." Emily set her phone down. "Why?"

"Just asking."

Emily gave her the look she reserved for statements she didn't believe. "He's been back about two years. His family had land out past the highway. He doesn't talk much about what happened to it."

Shannon nodded. Filed it away.

Colt crossed the diner, and as he passed their table he glanced over — not lingering, just the natural sweep of someone moving through a space. His eyes met Shannon's for exactly one second.

She felt it somewhere she had no business feeling anything.

Then he was past them, out the door, gone.

She drank her coffee. Thought about Eli's fence line. About the way the land ran out there, flat and open and full of things that ran underneath it.

She didn't think about Colt Hayes again.

Not much.

The second time she found Eli, it wasn't by accident. She took the same road. Slowed at the same spot. And she parked without thinking about it, which was its own admission.

This time he was closer to the road.

"You keep ending up here," he said as she approached.

"So do you."

He didn't argue that.

"You going to tell me what's really going on?" she asked.

"No."

"Why not?"

"Because it won't help you."

"That's not your decision."

"It is when it involves me."

She stopped a few feet away. Close enough now that the space between them felt occupied. She could see the salt-crust on his collar, the way the late light caught the hard line of his jaw. He had a way of standing that made you aware of him — not because he performed it, but because he didn't. There was nothing for sale in his posture.

That was new, for her. She'd spent eleven years with a man whose every expression was a negotiation.

"I've heard about the land," she said. "The claim."

Eli's expression didn't change. "People talk."

"Is it yours?"

"Yes."

"Then why is anyone disputing it?"

He looked at her, and for a second the distance felt shorter than the air between them. "Because they can."

"That's not how ownership works."

"It is here."

She studied him, trying to read past the surface. "You're not telling me everything."

"No."

"At least you're honest about it."

"I try to be."

The silence settled differently this time. Less like a wall. More like something held in place on purpose, by both of them, because letting it go meant something.

"Why are you still here?" Eli asked.

Shannon blinked. "What?"

"You asked your questions. You got your answers. Why are you still here."

She opened her mouth. Closed it.

He waited. He was very good at waiting.

"I didn't get answers," she finally said. "I got more questions."

"That's not a reason to stay."

"Maybe it is for me."

He looked at her for a long moment — the way you look at something you're trying to decide about. Then something shifted, some internal calculation reaching its conclusion.

"My grandfather filed that claim," he said. "Before the county redrew half the lines out here."

Shannon waited.

"It was never contested," he continued. "Not until a few years ago."

"Why then?"

"Because the land started to matter."

"How?"

Eli didn't answer right away. When he did, his voice was quieter. "There's water under it. Good access. Runs year-round."

Shannon felt something click into place. "And now people want it."

"Yes."

"Who?"

He shook his head. "Doesn't matter."

"It does to me."

"It shouldn't."

She stepped closer without deciding to. Close enough now that she had to look up at him slightly, close enough to see the tension he carried in his shoulders that wasn't quite his own weight.

"You're telling me just enough to keep me asking," she said.

"I'm telling you enough to understand why you should stop."

"Is that what this is? A warning?"

"Yes."

"From you or from them?"

Eli held her gaze. "From me."

The exactness of it caught her. He wasn't trying to soften it or wrap it in something else.

Neither of them moved.

Then his hand shifted on the silvered wood of the fence rail, and hers mirrored it on her side — not touching, just a mirror, a small unconscious thing she didn't catch herself doing until it was already done.

She pulled back first. The sudden absence of that warmth was enough to notice. He noticed too.

"Are you going to lose it?" she asked. "The land?"

Eli looked past her, toward the road. "Not if I can help it."

"That doesn't sound certain."

"It isn't."

She nodded slowly. "Then I guess that makes it my problem."

Eli's gaze snapped back to hers. "No," he said. "It doesn't."

Shannon held his eyes.

But she didn't argue.

Not out loud.

Chapter Three

Emily was on the porch when Shannon pulled in, phone in hand, expression already resigned.

"You've been out," Emily said.

"Yes."

"Want to tell me where?"

"No."

Emily studied her. "I'm not going to fight you on it."

"That's new."

"I'm picking my battles."

Shannon gave a small, tired smile. "Good strategy."

Emily hesitated. "Just be careful."

"I am."

Emily didn't look convinced. She went inside first. Shannon stayed on the steps a minute longer, looking out at the fields going dark in the distance.

She thought about the fact that she had nowhere pressing to be. Her apartment lease didn't start until June. Emily had asked her to stay through the end of April to help sort their mother's remaining estate paperwork, which had stretched, the way those things always did, into something larger and less finite. There were real reasons she was still here.

She'd stopped needing them to be the actual reason.

It wasn't that she didn't know what she was doing.

It was that she was starting to not care that she shouldn't.

The next morning, Wade was waiting outside the courthouse before Shannon even parked.

He crossed the lot toward her with the particular walk of a man who had decided something and needed her to understand it.

"We need to talk," he said.

"Apparently."

He steered her toward the side of the building, away from the front steps and the people drifting in and out. Not threatening — just efficient.

"They found evidence on his property," Wade said. "Branding records. Clean cuts, recent."

Shannon kept her expression neutral. "What kind of evidence?"

"The kind that closes a case."

"Or the kind that was put there to close it."

Wade's eyes narrowed. "You don't know what you're saying."

"I know exactly what I'm saying."

He stepped closer, lowering his voice. Not angry — controlled. Always controlled. “Shannon. I’ve known you your whole life. I’m asking you, as someone who knew you when — leave this alone. Not because I’m telling you to. Because some things here are bigger than a stranger’s clean conscience.”

She looked at him for a long moment. "Who benefits if he loses the land, Wade?"

A beat. Just long enough.

"I don't know what you're implying."

"Yes you do."

His jaw tightened. "The Hargroves have legitimate grievances. He moved cattle that weren't his. The evidence is—"

"Clean," she said. "Everybody keeps saying that. Clean. Solid. Lines up."

She let it sit.

"Doesn't it bother you," she said, "when things line up that perfectly?"

Wade's expression didn't waver. But his eyes did. Just for a second.

"Stay out of it," he said.

He walked away. She watched him go.

She thought about the way he hadn't answered her question.

The third time she found Eli, he was expecting her.

He was sitting on the tailgate of his truck when she pulled up, forearms resting on his knees, looking out at the land like he was trying to memorize it. She parked and walked over and sat up there beside him without asking.

He didn't say anything for a minute. Neither did she.

The sun was going down behind the treeline, painting the sky in layers of amber and rust, and the light did something to his face that she tried not to look at directly.

"They found branding evidence," she said finally. "On your property."

"I know."

"Did you—"

"No."

She nodded. Believed him. That was getting easier to do, which she recognized as a problem.

"Your public defender. Briggs. I think he's been compromised."

Eli looked at her.

"He didn't flag a date discrepancy in the transport logs that I found in twenty minutes with public records. Either he's incompetent or he's been told to stand down."

"I know," Eli said.

That stopped her. "You know."

"I've known since the first meeting. He wouldn't look at me."

"Then why haven't you—"

"Said something to who?" He looked at her steadily. "The sheriff's office filed the supplement. The public defender won't fight it. The judge has known the Hargroves for thirty years."

Shannon was quiet. The shape of it was coming clear — not just the frame-up, but the infrastructure holding it in place.

"Wade knows something," she said. "About who's behind this. He wouldn't say it, but I could see it."

Eli was quiet for a moment. "Hargrove's not the one driving this."

"Then who?"

He shifted to look at her. This close, on the tailgate, their shoulders were almost touching. She could feel the warmth coming off him. He was looking at her with the particular expression of a man deciding whether to trust someone — not whether she deserved it, but whether he could afford it.

She let him decide. Didn't push.

He reached into his jacket.

"There's a company," he said. "Agricultural water consortium. Been buying up land all through this county, anywhere with reliable underground access. They use intermediaries." A pause. "Local ones."

"Wade," she said.

Eli said nothing.

"Not Wade directly," she worked it out. "But someone he's protecting."

"His father-in-law owns thirty percent of Hargrove's operation," Eli said. "Has for four years."

She stared at him. "How do you know that?"

"Because I started looking after the first threat."

"What kind of threat?"

He was quiet for a moment. Then he reached into his jacket and pulled out a folded piece of paper. Handed it to her.

She unfolded it. Read it. It was brief, handwritten, and it didn't leave much to interpretation.

"Jesus," she said.

"Yeah."

"When did you get this?"

"Three months ago."

She looked up at him. "And you didn't go to—" She stopped. Heard how that sounded. "Right."

"There's nobody to go to," he said. "Not here."

The light had gone soft. The fields were going gray at the edges. Somewhere down the road a truck moved through, its lights sweeping briefly across them.

Shannon was aware of how close they were sitting. Of the fact that neither of them had moved to create more space.

"Why didn't you leave?" she asked. "When the threats started."

He turned to look at her. His expression was something she hadn't seen on him yet — not the controlled stillness, not the deliberate quiet. Something underneath that.

"It's my land," he said.

Simple. Like it explained everything.

She looked at his face. The set of it. The particular kind of stubbornness that lived so deep it had become something else entirely.

"That's not a good enough reason to stay somewhere you're being framed," she said.

"Maybe not," he said. "But it's mine."

She thought about the house she'd left. The marriage she'd dissolved. The job she'd stepped back from. All the things she'd walked away from because staying had become a kind of slow erasure.

She thought about standing in the kitchen of Emily's house and feeling the hours ask nothing of her.

"I understand that," she said.

He looked at her, and she could see him deciding how much to believe it.

She let him decide.

It happened later than she expected. Not planned. Not built toward in any way she could point to later.

They'd been going over what she knew — the land records she'd pulled, the name of the consortium, the chain of ownership that led from Hargrove through a shell company to a real estate arm that shouldn't have existed in an agricultural operation. It was good. It was something. Not enough on its own, but the shape of proof.

Chapter Four

The new evidence was a set of transport logs.

Shannon stood in Emily's office, coat still on, reading the copies Emily had managed to get from a paralegal who owed her a favor. Dates, weights, buyer codes. Eli stood at the defense table, beside a public defender named Briggs who had the look of someone managing six cases at once and billing hours on four of them. She held it.

He looked away first. That was something.

"Opportunity. Presence. Post-incident alteration. Financial strain," he said. "The picture is clear."

Chapter Six

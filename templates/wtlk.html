Chapter 1

Old River Road

Shannon woke before the alarm, same as always, with the dark still against the windows and the house holding the last of the night in its floors.

For a minute she lay still and listened. The refrigerator kicked on in the kitchen. A truck went by somewhere out on the highway, far enough away to be only a wash of sound. Something small moved under the porch, not heavy enough to worry about. The house had its own noises, and after ten months in it she knew most of them.

At 5:08 the alarm went off anyway, a soft bell she had never gotten around to changing from her teaching years. She reached over, silenced it, and sat up.

The room was spare in a way that still felt temporary, though she had stopped calling it that. One chest of drawers. The iron bed she’d taken from her aunt’s guest room. A chair in the corner with two cardigans over the back. Her divorce had not been the kind that left broken plates or police reports or anything people could point to and nod over. It had mostly left rooms thinned out. A house in Starkville she no longer lived in, because Scott had kept the house and half the things in it had been chosen for that house anyway. Books in boxes she still hadn’t unpacked. A habit of waking before daylight for work she no longer did.

She stood, pulled on a T-shirt and jeans, and padded down the narrow hallway to the kitchen.

The little house sat off Old River Road, past a soybean field and two pastures, on a strip of land that had belonged to a cousin of a cousin before it became one more place somebody rented out because there was no use letting it stand empty. The kitchen window over the sink looked east toward a line of pines. In the dark it only gave back her own reflection: hair tied up carelessly, face still puffy with sleep, shoulders a little tighter than she’d meant to carry into another day.

She put coffee on, scooped food into the dented metal bowl on the back step, and opened the door.

The morning air came in damp and faintly cool, the first little mercy after months of heat. The red dog trotted out from beneath the camellia bush as soon as he heard the bowl hit the boards. He wasn’t hers exactly. He had shown up in June with one torn ear and the manners of something that had belonged to too many people already. Shannon had fed him twice. After that he had stayed.

“There you are,” she said.

He ate with his whole body in it, hips working, collarless neck bent over the bowl. Shannon stood with her coffee mug warming both hands and looked out over the yard.

The grass needed cutting again. The ditch by the road still held a little silver fog. Beyond it the pasture on the Walker place lay flat and pale, fence wire beaded with dew. Three cows stood near the far gate, dark and patient as fence posts. One lifted its head at the sound of a truck changing gears somewhere beyond the trees.

Mississippi mornings out there worked their way in slow. First the birds in the brush. Then a yard light clicking off across the road. Then the school bus on the blacktop a mile over, not visible from where she stood but loud enough if the air was right. It was right this morning. She heard the brakes hiss, heard them release, and then the bus moved on.

She took a drink of coffee and felt the old reflex rise in her anyway: first period, attendance, the stack of grammar quizzes she used to leave clipped together at the corner of her desk. She had taught ninth and tenth grade English for twelve years, long enough that the hours of the school day had got into her blood. Even now, a year out, she still knew where the buses would be, when teachers would start unlocking doors, when the intercom would crackle on. She still saw apostrophes gone missing in handwritten signs and had to resist correcting them in her head.

She drank the rest of the coffee, went back inside, and stood at the counter with the mug in her hand. The kitchen held only what she used. Two plates in the rack. A bowl of peaches starting to freckle. A stack of mail she had not dealt with because every envelope lately seemed to want either money or a decision. She turned one over, saw it was from the school district again, and slid it to the bottom of the pile.

She had told the superintendent maybe after Christmas when he asked if she wanted back in. She had said that in March, and again in July. He kept calling because small counties always needed someone steady and because Shannon had once been the kind of woman people described that way. Maybe she still was. She paid her rent. She got to work on time. She kept the dog fed and the house clean and her own name off everybody else’s list of trouble.

By six, she had made the bed, rinsed her mug, packed a pimento cheese sandwich into a plastic container for later, and changed into a blouse decent enough for town. She locked the back door, jiggled it once to make sure the latch caught, and went out to her car.

The Corolla had more miles on it than she liked to think about and a long scrape on the passenger side from somebody backing into her in a Kroger parking lot three years ago. It started on the second turn of the key. She backed down the gravel, careful of the rut at the end of the drive, and headed toward town with the windows cracked.

Old River Road curved through flat pasture and then dipped into a stand of pine before it met the two-lane. Kudzu had climbed one abandoned barn all the way to the roofline. A hand-painted sign beside a church announced REVIVAL SUNDAY SEPT 27 in red letters thick enough to bleed at the edges. At the Dollar General, two boys in orange vests unloaded water from a truck while the manager smoked by the side door. Shannon passed the elementary school just as the crossing guard stepped out with her sign.

Marlow was small enough that people still said into town and meant six blocks, the square, the courthouse, the Piggly Wiggly, the funeral home, the diner, two churches close enough together for their bells to overlap on Sundays, and whatever business had managed not to close yet. The county roads spidered out from it through soybeans, timber, old family land broken up and sold and still referred to by the names of people long buried.

She parked behind Mize Farm & Garden at 6:23 and went in through the side door with her key.

The store smelled like feed dust, fertilizer, old wood, and the sweeter note of potting soil stacked near the greenhouse doors. Shannon liked that smell more than she had expected to when she started. The fluorescent lights buzzed awake one row at a time. She turned on the register, set the coffee pot behind the counter to brewing, and rolled up the front shade enough to let the square show through.

By six-thirty she had the first load of invoices spread before her and the first customer at the door.

“Morning, Miss Shannon.”

“Morning, Mr. Tullie.”

Mr. Tullie came in every Tuesday and Friday for range cubes whether he needed them or not. He wore overalls over a white undershirt in weather too cool for it, and he always paid in exact cash from a rubber-banded fold he kept in his front pocket.

“Y’all got that mineral tub in yet?” he asked.

“By the back wall, same as last week.”

He nodded and went lumbering toward it. Shannon marked the invoice for Mr. Mize, answered the phone twice, and rang up a woman from out on Cedar Creek who wanted chick starter and a sack of dog food. She moved through the morning the way she did most mornings now: quietly, capably, with half her mind on what her hands were doing and the other half noticing things.

The radio behind the counter stayed low on a country station Mr. Mize liked. The store cat slept under the display of rubber boots. The old men gathered by the seed racks drifted in and out of talk about rain, diesel, college football, somebody’s gallbladder. A boy from the ag department at the high school came in for fence staples, all elbows and acne and politeness. Shannon wrote up the charge slip while he shifted from one boot to the other.

At seven-fifteen, when the school buses were all run and the town had settled into itself, she found herself looking toward the front windows the way she used to look toward her classroom door before first bell, expecting a rush that no longer belonged to her.

Mize’s didn’t give her that. The place had a steady rhythm instead. Men came in before field work. Housewives came later for mums and seed. Hunters started buying corn before dawn as soon as deer season got close. Nobody brought her essays on The Great Gatsby and wanted her to make them care. Nobody cried in her doorway over grades or breakups or a mother in rehab or a father in jail. Nobody needed her to be patient past the point where patience came naturally.

Mr. Mize arrived a little before eight in his mud-streaked F-250, carrying a sausage biscuit and acting as if he had been there all along.

“You mind taking the greenhouse inventory after lunch?” he asked.

“I can.”

He set down his biscuit. “School called last week. Asked if I knew whether you were coming back.”

She looked up from the ledger. “Why would they ask you?”

He shrugged. “Everybody asks everybody in this town.”

“That sounds about right.”

He chewed, swallowed, and gave her a look over the tops of his reading glasses. “You thinking on it?”

“I always am.”

“That ain’t the same as deciding.”

“No, sir.”

Mr. Mize grunted in a way that could have meant anything. “You do good here. Just don’t get stuck out of convenience.”

She smiled a little because he had earned the right to be plain. “I’ll try not to.”

By ten-thirty the sun had burned the last of the damp off the square. The courthouse flag hung limp. Two men from the county road crew came in for gloves and a box of contractor bags. Shannon rang them up, counted down her drawer for the lunch shift, and wiped the counter where somebody had spilled coffee granules from a torn packet.

“I’m going over to Rosie’s,” she told Mr. Mize. “Back in thirty.”

“Bring me a tea if she ain’t too crowded.”

“She’s always too crowded.”

“Then bring me one anyway.”

Shannon took her sandwich out of the little store fridge, decided against it, and left it there. Rosie’s would smell like bacon and biscuits, and she was human enough not to fight that.

The square was fully awake now. A man from the tax assessor’s office smoked in the alley beside the courthouse. Two women came out of the beauty shop with foils still in their hair and stood in the sun talking. The brick around the square held heat already. Shannon crossed to the diner, opened the door, and stepped into the mix of coffee, fry grease, cold air, and conversation.

Rosie’s had been there longer than Shannon had been alive, which meant the booths had learned the shape of half the county. Rosie herself stood behind the register with a pencil tucked into her hair and three tickets between her fingers.

“Late for breakfast,” she called.

“I know.”

“You want the usual?”

Shannon slid into the booth by the window. “Yes, ma’am.”

“Eggs over easy, biscuit, tomatoes.”

“And coffee.”

Rosie gave her a look. “I know how to run my own place.”

Shannon smiled and set her purse in the seat beside her.

She liked this booth because it let her see the square without having to be fully in it. The courthouse steps across the street. The florist on the corner. Cars coming through the four-way, hesitating longer than they had to because nobody in Marlow ever seemed in a true hurry unless somebody was bleeding.

A man in work boots was washing the windshield of a sheriff’s deputy SUV at the curb when the bell over the diner door rang again.

Shannon didn’t turn right away. She knew the sound of Wade Carter’s boots on old tile before she looked up.

He had been back in Marlow a little over six months, transferred in from a neighboring county after whatever had happened there that people mostly didn’t ask about directly. He and Shannon had known each other since high school, the way people from towns like Marlow knew one another: through school and ballgames and church and parents who had once sat together at fish fries and funerals. Not close exactly, not then. But familiar enough that his face had always lived in the back room of her memory, and when he reappeared in a deputy’s uniform that spring it had felt less like somebody new arriving than like some old piece of the place getting put back where it had always belonged.

He saw her, changed direction without asking, and slid into the booth across from her as if there had been no chance he might sit anywhere else.

“Morning,” he said.

“Morning.”

He set his hat on the seat beside him. Up close he looked more substantial than he had at twenty-two, less sharp around the edges. The years had broadened him through the chest and shoulders. There were faint lines beside his eyes now, and the kind of stillness in him that came from being listened to in a uniform for long enough to expect it. His hair was cut close. He had his radio clipped at one shoulder and his badge catching the light from the window.

Rosie appeared with Shannon’s coffee and another mug she set down in front of Wade without being asked.

“I didn’t order that,” he said.

“You never have to,” Rosie said. “Y’all want anything else?”

“I’m fine,” Wade said.

Shannon lifted a brow at him once Rosie was gone. “You just sit where you want now?”

“I’ve sat in worse places.”

“That wasn’t what I asked.”

He looked at her a second, then took a drink of coffee. “You mind?”

“A little.”

He gave the smallest hint of a smile. “You can tell me to move.”

“And you’d listen?”

“Depends.”

That drew a huff of laughter before she meant it to.

She wrapped both hands around her mug. “Aren’t you supposed to be somewhere?”

“I am somewhere.”

“In my booth.”

“It ain’t deeded.”

She looked out the window instead of at him. “How’d you know I’d be here?”

“I saw your car at Mize’s. Figured you’d come over when he took lunch.”

“You keep track of everybody’s lunch schedule?” she asked.

“Not everybody’s.”

Rosie brought Shannon’s plate and set it down between them. Eggs, sliced tomatoes, biscuit big as her fist. Wade moved the jelly caddy out of the way before she asked him to.

“You been by your place lately after dark?” he asked.

She cut into an egg. “I live there, so yes.”

“Your porch light’s out.”

She looked up then. “You been by my place lately after dark?”

“Passed it two nights ago.”

“Why?”

“Because Old River turns into County Nine and County Nine was where I was headed.”

“That an answer?”

“It’s enough of one.”

Shannon put a little salt on her tomatoes and let that sit between them for a moment.

“It’s just a bulb,” she said.

“The back latch still sticking?”

She stopped with the fork halfway to her mouth. “Who told you that?”

“Emily.”

Shannon closed her eyes once, briefly. “Of course she did.”

“She said you had to shoulder it shut last week.”

“She notices too much.”

“Runs in the family.”

Shannon ate a bite of egg and biscuit so she’d have a second to decide how much of this she felt like entertaining.

“I can buy a bulb and fix a latch,” she said.

“I know you can.”

“Then why are we discussing it like I need supervision?”

His expression didn’t change much, but something in it settled harder. “Because you’re out there alone on a road with three empty houses between you and anybody hearing you holler.”

“I’m not in a fainting novel, Wade.”

“No.”

“Then maybe don’t talk to me like I am.”

“You’ve got a bad latch. No porch light. That road’s dark as a pocket after sunset. There was a break-in at the Deason hunting camp last week, and if somebody comes trying doors out that way, yours is one of the few houses with a woman by herself in it.”

The eggs had gone cool on the plate. Shannon set down her fork.

“You say that like I picked the place to inconvenience you.”

“I didn’t say that.”

“You didn’t have to.”

Wade let out a breath through his nose. It wasn’t quite a sigh. “I’m telling you what I know.”

“And I’m telling you I heard you.”

“Then let me fix the latch.”

She gave him a look. “You already decided you were going to.”

“I’ll ask if that helps you any.”

“Does it?”

He drank coffee. “Probably not.”

Despite herself, Shannon felt the corner of her mouth move. “That’s honest, at least.”

He glanced toward the window, then back to her. “I can swing by after shift.”

“I may not be home.”

“You usually are by six.”

Shannon spread a little butter over her biscuit and kept her eyes on the plate. “Do you hear yourself?”

“Most days.”

“You make it sound like I’m a route.”

“You’ve got habits.”

“So do you.”

“Yeah.”

He didn’t ask what she had noticed about his, and she didn’t tell him. That he came into Mize’s more lately than a man with no garden and no cattle had reason to. That he parked facing the door. That he spoke less now than he had as a boy and somehow got more said that way.

Rosie passed with a coffeepot and topped off their cups.

“You eating?” she asked Wade.

“No, ma’am.”

“You ought to.”

“I’ll live.”

Rosie moved on.

Shannon picked up a tomato slice. “How’s your mother?”

“Bossy.”

“So healthy, then.”

“Healthy enough to send over a bag of okra yesterday and ask why I hadn’t taken it to you yet.”

Shannon paused. “She sent me okra?”

“She sent extra.”

“That wasn’t what I asked.”

Wade’s mouth shifted, not quite a smile this time. “You want it or not?”

“That depends.”

“On?”

“Whether your mother actually sent it to me, or whether she said, ‘Take this to that poor divorced woman on the edge of town because somebody ought to check if she’s eating vegetables.’”

“She likes you.”

“That’s not an answer either.”

He looked down into his coffee. “She asked after you. I said I’d seen you. She handed me a bag.”

“So a little of both.”

“Probably.”

Shannon shook her head and went back to her breakfast. She could picture his mother doing exactly that, leaning over the counter in her kitchen, pushing a paper sack into his chest with a look that allowed no discussion. Marlow was full of women who handled concern by way of produce.

“You don’t have to carry messages from half the county,” she said.

“I know.”

“But you do.”

“Sometimes.”

She ate in silence for a few seconds. Wade didn’t seem bothered by silence.

“So,” he said after a minute, “you going back to teaching?”

She looked at him over the rim of her coffee. “That’s a hard turn.”

“It was a question.”

“Mr. Mize ask you to ask it?”

“No.”

“Emily?”

“No.”

“Then why?”

He turned the mug once between his hands. “Because you looked like yourself when you talked about school before.”

“I still talk about school,” she said.

“Not like that.”

Shannon set the mug down. “Maybe I don’t feel like being looked at closely over breakfast.”

“Fair enough.”

But a beat later he said, “They’d take you back tomorrow if you wanted.”

“There’s a ringing endorsement. Desperate school district hires breathing woman.”

“You know what I mean.”

“Do I?”

His eyes stayed on her. “You’re good with people.”

“That may be the first time anybody working law enforcement has said that without irony.”

“One thing don’t cancel the other.”

“I don’t know if I want to go back,” she said.

Wade waited.

“It wasn’t only Scott,” she added, and hated the need to clarify it as soon as his name left her mouth. She almost never said her ex-husband’s name in town if she could help it. Not because there was scandal around him. There wasn’t. It was worse in a way. He was perfectly decent on paper. Tax accountant. Good family. No public drinking, no gambling, no women calling at midnight. He had simply spent years making every corner of their life efficient and correct until she one day realized she had gone quiet inside it.

Wade said nothing, which let her keep going.

“I got tired,” she said. “That’s the plain version.”

“Of him or the work?”

“Yes.”

He nodded once, as if the answer did not surprise him.

She looked out the window again. “I keep thinking if I go back because it’s what I know, I’ll wake up five years from now and realize I just kept choosing the easiest door.”

“There are worse reasons than knowing how to do something.”

“There are.”

“But you don’t want easy.”

She gave a small, humorless smile. “I wanted clear. That didn’t work out either.”

Outside, a man in a John Deere cap crossed the street carrying a stack of file folders under one arm. The courthouse clock clicked over the quarter hour.

Wade said, “You heard from him lately?”

Shannon turned back slowly. “Scott?”

He nodded.

“No.”

“You tell me if that changes.”

“There it is.”

“What?”

“That tone.”

He leaned back against the vinyl seat. “What tone?”

“The one where you say something like it’s already decided.”

“You don’t want me to say it?”

“I didn’t say that.”

“Then what’d you say?”

She looked at him for a long second. “You don’t ask so much as announce.”

A muscle moved once in his jaw. “Comes with the badge, maybe.”

“No, I think that was there before.”

That brought a clearer flicker of amusement into his face. “Might’ve been.”

“And it worked on people when you were seventeen, so why would you give it up now.”

“You saying it worked on you?”

“I’m saying you were bossy young.”

“I’m bossy now.”

“I noticed.”

A call crackled faintly from his shoulder radio, too garbled to make out. He touched it but didn’t answer yet.

Shannon said, “For the record, I can change my own porch bulb.”

“I know.”

“And if I decide the latch needs work, I can call a locksmith.”

“You could.”

“And yet you’re still planning to show up.”

“Probably.”

Rosie stopped by with the check tucked under the sugar jar. “Deputy, you paying for your own coffee today or letting the county take the hit?”

“I’ll pay.”

“You’d better. County don’t tip.”

Wade reached for the ticket at the same time Shannon did. His hand got there first.

“I’ve got it,” he said.

“For my breakfast?”

“Yeah.”

“No.”

He glanced up. “No?”

“I am fully employed.”

“That wasn’t the question.”

“It is now.”

He held her gaze another beat, then set a bill under the ticket anyway. “Then you can buy next time.”

“There may not be a next time.”

“Sure there will.”

The radio on his shoulder barked again, clearer this time. He picked it up, listened. Whatever came through changed nothing visible in his expression, but all at once his attention was no longer in the booth.

“Carter,” he said into it.

A burst of static. A voice Shannon couldn’t make out.

Wade stood before the transmission ended. He put his hat on and looked down at her.

“I need to go.”

“Sounds urgent.”

“It is.”

He took one step, then stopped and looked back. “Keep your phone on.”

“I always do.”

He hesitated a fraction of a second, as if weighing something he wasn’t going to say in front of the room. Then he gave a short nod and headed for the door.

At the register, Rosie asked, “Everything all right?”

“Not sure yet,” Wade said.

Then he was gone, crossing the square fast, one hand already at his radio again.

Shannon watched him get into the deputy SUV and pull away hard enough to make the gravel spit from under the tires. The square seemed to notice his leaving. Conversations thinned, shifted, turned.

Rosie came over and lifted Wade’s coffee mug. “That man can’t sit still.”

“He didn’t really sit,” Shannon said.

“No. He hovers.” Rosie glanced toward the window. “Something up?”

Shannon reached for her wallet. “Apparently.”

At the counter, two men in seed-company caps were already talking lower than before.

“Down Sand Creek way,” one said.

“Culpepper place?”

“Sounded like it.”

Rosie set Wade’s mug in the bus tub. “Lord help whoever got themselves into Culpepper business before lunch.”

One of the men snorted. “If it’s who I think, help won’t go far.”

Shannon looked over. “Who?”

The older man turned toward her, pleased enough to have an audience. “Haven’t you heard? Eli Turner got picked up this morning.”

“Picked up for what?”

The other man answered before the first could. “Cattle theft.”

Rosie leaned one hand on the counter. “From the Culpeppers?”

“Six head missing off that south tract,” the older man said. “Maybe more. They been looking into it a while.”

“No they haven’t,” Rosie said. “They’ve been griping a while. That ain’t the same.”

A waitress carrying three plates slowed just enough to listen.

The man shrugged. “Deputies were at Turner’s place at daybreak. Hauled him in.”

The waitress whistled softly. “Eli Turner?”

“That’s what I said.”

Shannon knew the name without being able, right off, to place a face to it. Turner land lay out past Sand Creek, west side of the county where the roads got narrower and the cell service fell off. The Turners had been there forever, or long enough for people to talk like it was forever. She knew there had once been more of them. A father maybe. An older brother who moved to Alabama. Eli she knew only as a name that drifted around fencing jobs, hay hauling, the sort of work a man did when he belonged to land but not enough of it.

“Maybe he bought some that weren’t clean,” Rosie said. “That happens.”

The older man shook his head. “Not what they’re saying.”

“What are they saying?”

He lowered his voice anyway, though nobody in the room had failed to hear him so far. “They’re saying the tags came off and new ones went on. Sale barn in Winston County flagged numbers.”

The waitress stopped outright now. “Well.”

The seed-cap man went on, enjoying the weight of the room. “Culpepper boys been mad since July. If they can prove he moved even one across county lines, they’ll hang the moon off it.”

Rosie snorted. “You don’t know what they can prove.”

“No,” he said, “but I know what they’ll spend trying.”

Across the diner, a couple at the far booth went still in the middle of their own conversation. A man at the counter folded his paper lower. Nobody raised their voice.

The Culpeppers had cattle, timber, a trucking company, a niece on the school board, a son on the bank board, and a habit of getting what they wanted slowly enough that it looked natural by the time it was done. Old Mack Culpepper had donated the new lights at the football field and half the stained glass in First Baptist. His picture still ran in the paper when the livestock scholarship fund gave out checks every spring. If one of his grandsons lost a dog, half the county kept an eye out. If one of them lost cattle, the sheriff’s department did not treat it like a misunderstanding.

“You believe it?” Rosie asked nobody and everybody.

The older man lifted one shoulder. “I believe nobody arrests Eli Turner by accident.”

“Plenty of folks get arrested by politics,” Rosie said.

He gave her a look. “Not around here.”

That made the waitress laugh once without humor. “Baby, especially around here.”

Shannon paid her ticket. She should have gone back across the street to the feed store and inventory sheets and mums needing water. Instead she stood there one beat longer than necessary, listening.

The younger of the two men said, “He’s finished if it sticks.”

“More than finished,” the older one said. “You don’t steal from the Culpeppers and go back to business like before.”

Rosie shot him a dry look. “You planning the sentencing now?”

“I’m saying what everybody knows.”

The bell over the door rang and three men came in from the square talking hard. One of them, a deputy from the other side of the county, said to Rosie without greeting, “You heard?”

She nodded toward Shannon and the others. “Heard enough to know y’all got yourselves a long day.”

“Long week,” he said.

She picked up her purse and went out into the brightness.

Two sheriff’s vehicles now instead of one. People on the courthouse steps not lingering so much as waiting. A black dually truck with a CULPEPPER LAND & CATTLE decal crooked across the rear window idling at the curb.

Mr. Mize was on the phone when she came through the side door. He held up one finger, listened, grunted, hung up.

“Well,” he said.

“You heard.”

“You’d have to be dead not to by now.”

Shannon moved behind the counter. “Is it true?”

Mr. Mize rubbed one hand over his jaw. “Depends what part.”

“That Eli Turner got arrested.”

“That part, yes.”

“For stealing cattle.”

He tilted his head. “That’s the charge.”

She started to ask more, then stopped. Mr. Mize had lived in Marlow all his life. He knew better than most what was worth saying out loud.

He lifted a stack of seed invoices and put them in front of her. “You can inventory mums later. I need those entered before one.”

So she worked. She sat on the stool behind the register and typed numbers into the old computer while the store filled and emptied around her. Men came in for feed and lingered. Somebody’s phone went off every ten minutes with another update from somewhere. A boy from the tire shop stopped by just to ask if Mr. Mize had heard anything more.

“About Eli?” Shannon heard him say as he stacked mineral blocks. “All I know’s what everybody knows.”

“Which is?”

Mr. Mize glanced toward Shannon and then away. “That them boys from Culpepper ain’t laughing.”

By noon the whole county seemed to have organized itself around the news.

Shannon left for the Piggly Wiggly because Mr. Mize had remembered, too late, that he’d promised his wife a bag of onions and because she needed dog food anyway. The parking lot shimmered in the heat. Two women from her mother’s old church stood by the cart return talking low and fast enough to keep their mouths close together. When Shannon passed, one of them stopped just short of saying the name she had clearly been in the middle of.

Inside, the air conditioning hit cold and heavy. She got onions first, then coffee filters, then a twenty-pound bag of dog food she regretted as soon as she lifted it into the cart.

At the meat counter Miss Lila from the beauty shop leaned over her buggy and said, “You hear about all that?”

“I heard,” Shannon said.

Miss Lila had a lacquered helmet of brown hair that did not move in weather. “Well, honey, that is bad business.”

“What business are they saying it is now?”

Miss Lila glanced down the aisle as though the canned vegetables might be listening. “I heard they found tags in his shed.”

“Who told you?”

“My niece’s husband’s cousin works out at the sheriff’s office.”

“That’s a strong chain of custody.”

Miss Lila smiled thinly. “You were a teacher too long. I’m just telling you what folks are saying.”

An older man choosing pork chops two feet away said, without turning, “Folks are saying a sight more than they know.”

The neighborhoods thinned into scattered houses, then pasture again. A tractor moved slow along the edge of a field. Someone had hung fresh sheets on a clothesline under pecan trees.

Ordinarily that drive peeled the town off her in layers. Today it did not.

At Mize’s, the afternoon dragged under the weight of what everybody had already heard. The house met her the way it always did, plain and waiting. The red dog lifted his head from the porch shade and thumped his tail twice against the boards before trotting toward her. She hauled the dog food inside, changed out of her blouse, and stood at the sink rinsing peaches without remembering she had taken them out of the bowl.

She cut one peach open anyway, ate it standing over the counter, and watched juice run down her wrist.

It was none of her business.

It was none of her business whether Eli Turner had stolen six head of cattle from the Culpepper south pasture. None of her business whether the sheriff had rushed the case or whether the Culpeppers meant to make an example out of a man whose name most of the county had only been half-aware of until morning. None of her business what Wade Carter thought about any of it. She had come back to Marlow to get smaller for a while, not bigger. To make her life manageable. To work, sleep, pay bills, answer no questions she didn’t feel like answering, and choose nothing she didn’t absolutely have to choose.

She carried a glass of tea to the back porch and sat in the metal chair by the door. The dog lay down at her feet with a groan of contentment and one paw over the other like an old gentleman.

The pasture beyond the ditch had gone gold at the edges. A pickup rattled past, then another. Somewhere a mower started up. The world was ordinary in all the ways it had to be, indifferent to one man in cuffs at the courthouse.

Shannon looked toward her back door.

The latch did stick a little. She had to lift and push. Wade wasn’t wrong about that. The porch light was out too. She had noticed it the night before when she let the dog out, meant to replace the bulb, then forgotten because there had been dishes and a phone call from an old college friend and one more small thing after that.

She stood, went inside, and changed the porch bulb herself.

The old one came loose warm in her hand. She screwed the new bulb in, flipped the switch, watched the yellow light bloom against the evening.

The latch took longer. She found a screwdriver in the junk drawer and worked at the plate, tightening one screw, then the other, opening and shutting the door until it caught better. The whole time she could hear Wade’s voice in memory—You’re out there alone on a road with three empty houses between you and anybody hearing you holler—and resented him for being practical enough to make resentment feel childish.

By dark, she had fed the dog, warmed leftover beans for supper, and showered. She put on a clean T-shirt and sat at the kitchen table with a book she had already read once, a novel dense enough that, under normal conditions, it could pull her out of her own head.

Three pages in, she realized she had not taken in a single sentence.

She set the book down and got up.

The kitchen window over the sink reflected the room back at her. The porch light made a wedge across the yard now. Beyond it the ditch and pasture disappeared into dark. Somewhere across the road a cow bawled once, low and long. Another answered.

She could still see Eli Turner in the hallway if she let herself. The worn denim shirt. The bruise near his thumb. The way he had said I know what it is without any extra language hung on it.

She went to the refrigerator, got the tea pitcher, poured a second glass she did not want.

At nine-thirty, her phone buzzed with a text from Emily.

Still alive. Barely.

Shannon typed back: You home?

Eventually.

After a minute another text came: Don’t ask me anything else tonight. My brain is pudding.

Shannon smiled despite herself and sent: Eat something.

I had half a turkey sandwich and three lies, Emily replied.

Then: Seriously. Leave it alone till tomorrow.

Shannon stared at that last line.

Leave it alone till tomorrow.

Not leave it alone. Till tomorrow.

She put the phone down faceup on the table and looked around the kitchen as if the room had somehow changed while she wasn’t paying attention. Same peaches in the bowl, softer now. Same mail stack. Same dish towel over the oven handle. Yet there was a tautness in her, fine and persistent, like a string drawn tight enough to hum if touched.

A thing can be serious and still not be simple.

Mr. Borden had said that over pork chops like it was nothing.

Still.

She went to the trash, lifted the lid, then let it fall again without dropping anything in. The local paper lay folded on the sideboard where she had set it coming in. She had barely glanced at it because most days the paper was two obituaries, a football score, and old news arranged politely. Tonight she picked it up and spread it open on the table.

No article yet. Too soon for print. But the sheriff’s blotter column from the weekly insert sat in a box at the bottom of page three, and someone at the paper had already penciled tomorrow’s expected court docket into the margin by hand before distributing the advance sheets to a few businesses. Shannon had seen it at Mize’s earlier and not thought much of it then.

Now she found herself smoothing the page flat.

District Court — Preliminary Matters — 9:00 a.m.

Nothing named. Just time and place.

She folded the paper once, then unfolded it.

At the sink she rinsed her empty glass and left it there. In the bedroom she pulled the chair away from the corner and draped clean jeans over the back. She set an alarm for 6:00 instead of 5:08, then changed it to 5:45 without a reason she cared to say out loud.

When she came back through the kitchen, the folded paper was still on the table.

She stood there in the porch-light glow and looked at it for another moment.

Then she tore the docket strip off neatly along the fold, wrote 9:00 in the margin with the pen from beside the phone, and slipped the scrap into her purse with her keys.

Chapter 2

Nine O’Clock

At five till nine the courthouse square was already hot enough to shine.

Shannon parked along the curb in front of the hardware store instead of in the lot behind the courthouse, though there were spaces there. She told herself it was because the shade still reached the front spaces from the line of sycamores and because she only meant to stay a few minutes. The red dog had watched her leave from the porch, head cocked, then sat down hard on the top step like she’d offended him personally.

The docket page from the paper was folded in her purse with a lipstick she never wore and two receipts from Mize Farm & Garden. She did not take it out. She knew the time. She knew the room number. She knew that if anybody asked what she was doing there, the honest answer was nothing at all.

The courthouse doors were propped open with a yellow caution cone that had lost half its lettering. Inside, the air changed from hot wet June to a dry chill that smelled faintly of old paper, mop water, and coffee that had been sitting too long. The floors were terrazzo, worn pale along the middle where decades of feet had tracked the same lines. There was a bulletin board just inside the security station with notices pinned over notices, edges curled. Tax sale. Veterans breakfast. Volunteer fire department fish fry. A flyer for a missing bluetick hound with a phone number written twice.

Deputy McNair was at the magnetometer with his shirt dark at the spine. He knew her by face, if not name, and lifted his chin without smiling.

“Mornin’.”

“Mornin’.”

She set her purse in the gray tub. He waved her through before the machine had finished its little sweep over the bag.

“You teach school still?” he asked, because in Marlow time moved sideways and facts trailed years behind them.

“Not lately.”

He nodded like that could mean six things. “Courtroom Two’s runnin’ behind already. Clerk’s got a mood.”

“Good to know.”

She took her purse and moved past him before he could ask why she was there. He probably knew anyway. Everybody knew who was on that morning’s criminal docket. A cattle theft charge could still pull a crowd in a county where men had gone to the sale barn every Saturday for forty years and talked about rain like it was scripture.

The hallway outside Courtroom Two held the usual courthouse collection of people who had not planned to spend their morning there and had dressed for other errands. Men in clean caps. Women with tote bags. A young mother with a toddler on one hip and a packet of paperwork folded soft in her hand. An older Black man in pressed overalls sitting very straight on the bench nearest the window. Two boys in boots leaning on the wall beside a vending machine that hummed louder than conversation. A lawyer in a blue suit stood half in the doorway talking to the clerk inside, head tipped to hear over the scrape of chairs.

Shannon kept moving until she reached the far end of the hall and could choose her place without looking as if she were choosing it. There was an empty bench under a framed photograph of the old courthouse before the 1978 renovation, when the clock tower had still been intact. She sat there, crossed one ankle over the other, and set her purse in her lap.

If she’d been teaching, this was the part she would have noticed first in a classroom—the current under the room before the lesson started. Who was waiting because they had to. Who was waiting because they wanted to see. Who already believed they knew what would happen.

A man in feed-store coveralls she recognized as Lonnie Breaux from three miles past the river came in and stood with the two boys by the vending machine. His voice carried without seeming to.

“Told you they ain’t gonna fool with bond on somethin’ like this. Not with Culpepper standin’ out there sayin’ he’s missin’ more than six head.”

One of the boys, all elbows and sunburn, said, “He sold ’em in Winston, though, didn’t he?”

“That’s what they say.”

The boy heard his own words come back at him and glanced around like he was checking whether he’d stepped wrong. Lonnie spat into a folded tissue and put it in his pocket.

“That’s what they say,” he repeated.

The older woman beside Shannon had a grocery list on the back of an envelope. She’d written cornmeal, bleach, green beans, and something that looked crossed out twice. Shannon recognized her after a second. Mrs. Faye Hollis, who had once handed out Band-Aids in the elementary school office and remembered every child by the way they cried.

Mrs. Hollis turned and looked at Shannon’s face like she was sorting it into place. “You’re Joan’s oldest.”

“Second oldest.”

“That’s right. Emily’s the baby.” She lowered her voice without lowering it enough. “You here for this Turner thing?”

Shannon looked toward the courtroom door, letting the question pass over a beat too long to be easy. “I had some time.”

Mrs. Hollis nodded as if that confirmed something. “Whole town’s actin’ like they been waitin’ on it.”

“On him?”

“On somethin’ to happen.” Mrs. Hollis clicked the tip of her pen against the grocery list. “Folk get restless when nothin’ changes for too long. They’ll make a pattern out of any little thing.”

“I didn’t say he was innocent. I said people are eager.”

The courtroom door opened. The clerk stepped out with three files tucked to her chest and called a name that meant nothing to Shannon. A thin man in a church shirt stood, tucked in his chin, and went inside. The people on the benches shifted without getting up, everybody adjusting to the fact that they were still going to be there a while.

At nine fifteen Wade came down the hall from the side entrance.

He was not looking for her at first. That was what Shannon noticed. He moved like a man with a set number of stops between where he was and where he needed to be, attention split cleanly among them. Uniform crisp, sleeves short, radio at his shoulder, duty belt pulling his hips square. He spoke to the bailiff in the doorway, took a paper from him, glanced at it once, then looked up and found her.

Nothing changed in his face. He only knew now where she was.

He crossed the hall, not quick enough to call it a beeline, and stopped in front of her bench.

“Didn’t know you had court business.”

“I don’t.”

“Then what’ve you got?”

She met his eyes. “Time.”

His mouth moved once at the corner, not a smile. “Nine a.m. at the courthouse is a strange place to spend it.”

“It’s shady.”

Mrs. Hollis looked steadfastly at her grocery list, which was the politest thing Shannon had ever seen.

Wade shifted his gaze to the older woman and touched two fingers to the brim of his nonexistent hat. “Morning, Miz Hollis.”

“Deputy.”

Then he looked back at Shannon. “Judge Hensley’s in a mood.”

“So I heard.”

“You here for Turner?”

The hall noise seemed to draw back around that name. Shannon kept her voice flat. “It’s a public hearing.”

“That wasn’t my question.”

She felt Mrs. Hollis’ stillness sharpen beside her. “Then ask the one you want to ask.”

“You didn’t sleep much,” he said instead.

“You keeping track?”

“I notice things.”

He glanced toward the courtroom door. “Stay on the back bench if you go in. Don’t let the clerk stick you up front just ’cause she knows your mama.”

“Thank you for the seating advice.”

He held her gaze a second longer, then turned away because a deputy from the jail detail had appeared at the end of the hall with a ring of keys and a paper wristband hanging from one finger. They disappeared through the side door that led to the holding room. Shannon’s stomach went still. Wade did not look back as he went.

By the time the clerk called Eli Turner’s name, there were no empty seats left on the benches in the hall. People stood along the wall, shoulders almost touching, as if the hearing itself might take less if they crowded it. Shannon rose when the flow moved toward the courtroom and let herself be carried in. The clerk did, exactly as Wade predicted, motion her toward an opening near the front. Shannon shook her head and took a place on the rear bench instead, where the wood had a splintered edge under the varnish.

Courtroom Two was not much of a room. Low ceiling. Two flags in brass stands. One state seal mounted slightly crooked over the bench. The acoustics were bad in a way everybody had stopped noticing except newcomers and the hard of hearing. A ceiling vent rattled every time the air kicked on. The judge’s bench sat too high for the size of the room and made Judge Hensley look smaller than he was when he settled into the chair with a sigh and a pair of reading glasses he wore low.

The clerk called the case. The prosecutor stood at the left table, a young assistant district attorney named Mercer from Tupelo who wore his certainty like it had been pressed into his shirt. Mr. Vance took the right table, gray suit, legal pad, glasses down his nose. Shannon had seen him in church and once at Emily’s office Christmas supper. He had the same patient expression then he had now, as if nothing much surprised him and that was its own advantage.

When the side door opened, a hush went through the room that would have embarrassed everybody if it had been named.

Eli came in with his hands free but held in front of him like they had only just been uncuffed. Same worn denim shirt as yesterday, or one near enough to it. Clean this time, though wrinkled at the shoulders. His hair looked as if he’d put water on it and then quit caring halfway through. The bruise near his thumb had gone darker. He did not look around the room on first entry. He looked at the table, the chair, the judge. Then he sat when Wade touched the back of the chair with two fingers and stepped away.

From the back bench, Shannon could see the left side of Eli’s face and the line of his forearm on the table. He wore no ring, but the pale band around the base of his finger still showed where one had been.

Judge Hensley adjusted his glasses. “Mr. Turner, you understand the charge before the court?”

“Yes, sir.”

His voice was lower than she expected.

The prosecutor laid out the state’s argument in clean, clipped lines. Six missing heifers from Culpepper south pasture on Sand Creek. Identification tags altered. Sale records in Winston County connected to a truck and trailer matching witness description. Evidence recovered from a shed on Turner property including cut tags and bolt cutters. Prior felony conviction relevant to conditions of release and community risk. The phrases came one after another, smooth enough that they stopped sounding like facts and started sounding like a shape designed to be stepped inside.

Mr. Vance did not object much. He let the state set its table, then asked where, precisely, the recovered items had been found. Asked whether the shed in question was locked. Asked who else had access to the property. Asked whether the witness description included a plate number, a make, or even a color the witness could state with confidence. Asked whether sale records bore Mr. Turner’s signature.

The prosecutor answered some of it and stepped around the rest. The shed was on family property. Access to rural land did not negate possession. Witness statements would be developed further. No, the paperwork did not include a direct signature. It included enough.

Enough for what, Shannon thought.

Judge Hensley listened with the weary irritation of a man who had heard the county explain itself in circles for twenty-five years and knew how often the loudest telling outran the straightest one. He denied full release, set a bond lower than the prosecutor asked and higher than Shannon had hoped. Conditions. Travel restrictions. No contact with named witnesses. No access to livestock sale facilities pending further hearing.

The clerk scribbled. The bailiff shifted his weight. Somebody in the back breathed out through their teeth, disappointed there would not be a cleaner display of punishment.

Mr. Vance leaned toward Eli and murmured something. Eli nodded once.

Then Judge Hensley mentioned a file number from 2021 in connection with the state’s argument over access to the Turner acreage, and Mr. Vance straightened.

“With respect, Your Honor, the prior boundary and easement dispute was civil and unresolved in material part. I would object to any characterization of that matter as settled ownership against my client’s interests.”

The prosecutor said, “The state is not retrying a land dispute.”

“No,” Mr. Vance said mildly. “The state is benefiting from the fog it left.”

There was a small stir in the room.

“We are not litigating Sand Creek boundaries today,” he said. “I am aware of the file. I will remain aware of it. Move on.”

Shannon looked, involuntarily, toward Emily’s office in her mind. The 2021 file. Property dispute. Fog.

Mercer moved on. The hearing ended not with drama but with chairs scraping and people standing too quickly, pleased to have something to carry back out into the square. Shannon stayed seated as the benches emptied in front of her. She did not want to be in the stream that passed within arm’s reach of the counsel table.

From where she sat, she saw Eli stand when Wade came back to him. Vance put a hand on Eli’s shoulder, light, professional, and spoke a few words too low to hear. Eli glanced toward the gallery then, maybe only because the movement of people caught his eye. He looked directly at the back bench.

At her.

It lasted less than a second. Nothing in his face changed.

She stood once most of the room had cleared. By the time she stepped back into the hallway, the conversations had already started up in knots.

“He’ll make bond by supper, you watch.”

“Not from where? With what money?”

“Brother in Alabama might—”

“That family’s been trouble since before Junior Turner died.”

“That ain’t true and you know it.”

“No, what’s true is Culpepper wouldn’t’ve pushed this hard if he wasn’t sure.”

“Culpepper pushes hard because he can.”

Shannon moved toward the stairs instead of the elevator. On the landing she paused because voices drifted up from below through the open well. Not gossiping voices. Measured. Office voices.

Mr. Vance: “I don’t want him talking to anybody.”

Emily: “He won’t.”

Wade, lower and flatter: “That goes double for family friends that think they’re helping.”

There was a short silence after that, not empty. Emily said, “You can leave my family out of your warnings till you’ve got one addressed to me directly.”

Shannon kept moving before she could hear the answer. Her pulse had picked up in a way that irritated her.

Outside, the square had brightened another shade toward noon though it was not yet ten. A man wheeled a hand truck stacked with copier paper across the sidewalk toward the chancery annex. Somebody laughed from the barber shop porch. Life had already resumed its ordinary posture, except that now nearly everyone in sight had the same fresh story in their mouth.

She reached her car, opened the door, then stood with one hand on the hot metal and looked back at the courthouse windows.

She got in, laid the folded docket on the passenger seat, and drove to Mize Farm & Garden because it was Thursday and because the mums out front would need watering by eleven.

It happened that afternoon at Mize Farm & Garden, while she was stacking seed packets into a cardboard display shaped like a windmill. The store held heat even with the fans running, a blend of fertilizer, cedar shavings, feed corn, and rubber hose. Mr. Mize had gone to lunch and left the register to Shannon and Cody Ray, who was nineteen and spent whole stretches of the day whistling through his teeth without seeming aware of it.

Around two, Mrs. Janice Brewer came in for fly spray and a bag of layer pellets. She wore church capris and a visor and always bought three things she needed and one thing she did not. Today the extra was a citronella candle in a tin bucket printed with sunflowers.

Mrs. Brewer leaned both hands on the counter before Shannon had finished scanning the pellets. “About time, if you ask me.”

Shannon picked up the candle and turned the barcode toward the scanner. “For what?”

Mrs. Brewer blinked. “Well. For stealin’ cattle.”

“That what you know or what you heard?”

“I heard they found tags and cutters on his place.”

“They did,” Shannon said, because she had heard that too.

Mrs. Brewer nodded, encouraged. “There you go.”

“But I also heard the shed wasn’t locked.”

Janice’s mouth thinned. “In the country, not every shed is locked.”

“No.”

“And folks don’t just wake up and accuse Culpepper stock for no reason.”

Shannon slid the fly spray into the bag. “Probably not.”

Janice studied her another second, then changed course in the way women did when they wanted to keep their ground without committing themselves too far to it. “His daddy always was proud. Proud people do strange things when they get behind.”

“Junior Turner’s dead.”

“I know he’s dead.” Janice took the bag handles from Shannon’s hand with more force than she needed. “I said he was proud, not present. Traits carry.”

Shannon almost smiled despite herself. Traits carry. Like eye color. Like high blood pressure. Like theft.

As Janice turned away, she added, “You better not let Emily get mixed up in that over at Vance & Harlow. There’s no gratitude in defending folks like that.”

Cody Ray looked up from the hose fittings aisle, instantly alert in the way of young men around adult conflict. Shannon kept her voice even.

“I’ll pass along your concern.”

Janice left with the fly spray clanking against the pellets in her cart. The bell over the door gave one tired ring.

Cody Ray wandered up to the register after a minute. “She mean Eli Turner?”

Shannon bagged the citronella candle Janice had forgotten and set it aside. “You know another Turner in trouble?”

“Nah.” He rubbed the back of his neck. “My cousin said he seen Eli pullin’ a trailer late Tuesday.”

“Your cousin says a lot.”

He grinned. “That’s true.”

“Did he say what kind of trailer?”

“Just one with cattle boards. Maybe dark blue truck. Maybe black.” Cody Ray shrugged. “He was comin’ back from his girlfriend’s and it was after midnight.”

“So he didn’t see much.”

He leaned one hip against the counter. “Enough to tell it three times by breakfast.”

Shannon looked at him until he laughed and held up both hands.

“All right. Not enough.”

“You know Eli?”

“Little. Bought fence staples in here once. Didn’t talk much.” Cody Ray tipped his chin toward the front windows, where the heat over the parking lot seemed to vibrate. “I don’t know. He don’t strike me stupid enough to sell local cattle one county over with tags half cut.”

“Plenty of guilty people are stupid.”

“Sure. But there’s stupid and there’s askin’ to get caught before coffee.” He glanced toward the office, lowered his voice. “You didn’t hear that from me if Mr. Mize asks. He’s on the Culpepper timber board and gets real careful all of a sudden.”

“Duly noted.”

He went back to the hoses. Shannon set Janice’s forgotten candle under the register and looked out at the parking lot a moment longer than necessary.

At five-thirty, on her way home, she turned into the gas station by the bypass because the car was low and because she could put off the quiet of the house a little longer under fluorescent lights. The station sold bait, cigarettes, discount laundry soap, and biscuits in the morning that people swore by out of habit more than quality.

At the pump nearest the road, a man she knew only as Deacon Reese from New Hope Baptist was filling two red cans in the bed of his truck. He nodded to her through the nozzle handle.

“You hear they found more head missin’?”

Shannon swiped her card. “Has anybody actually said they found more missing, or is that how the story’s growing?”

“That’s what my nephew said. South pasture and maybe three from near the creek crossing besides.” He clicked the pump and watched the numbers climb. “Culpepper ain’t gonna let this one slide.”

Deacon gave her a sideways look, thoughtful rather than suspicious. “That’s fair.” He capped one can. “Truth is, I don’t know. I know cattle go missin’ some every year and half the time they turn up through a bad fence and nobody talks about it after. But tags cut in a shed—”

“On land more than one person could access.”

He set the can down. “You sound like a lawyer.”

“My sister would be offended by that.”

He huffed a laugh. Then his face settled. “Junior Turner and my brother had words once, years back, over gravel hauled across a line road. Junior could hold a grudge like a tick holds skin. Eli ain’t Junior, though.”

“No?”

“Not from what I saw.” Deacon screwed the cap on the second can. “That boy kept his mouth shut at his daddy’s funeral while grown men around him made a spectacle. I remember thinkin’ he had more discipline than the room deserved.”

Shannon watched the gas numbers roll.

“So you think he didn’t do it.”

Deacon tipped one shoulder. “I think Culpepper men have a way of bein’ certain before the counting’s done. I also think desperate men make bad choices. Both can be true at once.”

When she finished pumping, she stood a second with the cap in her hand.

“Who’s your nephew hear things from?”

Deacon smiled without looking at her. “Now we’re gettin’ somewhere.”

She did not smile back, which only amused him more.

“He hears things from his wife’s people down near the sale barn and from his own imagination, same as every other man in this county. If he comes up with somethin’ that can stand on two legs, I’ll let you know.”

She shut the gas door. “Please don’t.”

“Mm-hm.”

That night she ate a tomato sandwich at the kitchen counter and left the plate there too long after she was done. The red dog slept under the table with his feet twitching. The back door latch still caught unless she lifted and pulled at the same time.

On Saturday morning, Emily called while Shannon was in the yard trying to convince the dog not to drag half a crepe myrtle branch onto the porch.

“Are you free for ten minutes or pretending to be?”

“Pretending. Why?”

“I’m at Rosie’s. Come get coffee.”

Rosie’s diner held its usual Saturday crowd, all of them louder before noon than after. The square was full of trucks, church sedans, and one mud-splashed Jeep that looked transplanted from another county. Inside, the booths shone with old vinyl and the air smelled like bacon grease, sweet tea, and the bleach Rosie herself believed in more than prayer.

Emily had a corner booth and legal pads spread around her like place mats. She wore her dark hair clipped up in a way that would not hold past lunch and had already drunk half her coffee.

“You look like you slept,” Emily said.

“Maybe I did.”

“You lie in a very rested tone.”

Shannon slid into the booth. Rosie set down a mug without asking. “You want eggs?”

“Just coffee.”

Rosie gave her a look that suggested divorce had made her unreliable in every area of life and moved on.

Emily pushed one legal pad aside. “I’m not talking to you as a paralegal.”

“That sentence has never once led to anything simple.”

“It means I am not giving you privileged information.” Emily wrapped both hands around her mug. “But I am telling you people are talking in all directions and half of it doesn’t line up.”

Shannon added sugar and did not stir it. “That seems to be the town’s gift.”

Emily glanced toward the counter to make sure nobody was near enough to enjoy this. “The witness with the trailer story changed his timing.”

“How?”

“Early report said around eleven-thirty. Then by the written statement it had moved closer to one-fifteen.”

“That’s a wide move.”

“Mhm.”

“Why?”

Emily shook her head. “Could be nothing. Could be because one makes him sober and one doesn’t.” She took a breath. “There’s also the issue of the sale records. Everybody says Winston County sale barn like it’s one thing, but there are two places livestock can pass through over there and one of them barely keeps proper paper on a good day.”

Shannon stirred her coffee now. “Did Eli sell there before?”

“Not that I know.”

“That’s not an answer.”

“It’s the one I have.” Emily’s gaze narrowed. “Have you been asking around?”

“Casually.”

“That word does not mean what you think it means.”

Shannon did not answer, and Emily sighed into her coffee.

“I texted you to leave it alone till tomorrow, not collect contradictions like coupons.”

“You’re the one who told me the evidence felt unfinished.”

“I know what I told you.” Emily leaned back as Rosie swooped past with a tray. “I also know how this town works. People decide what side of a thing you’re on before you open your mouth. Then every question sounds like a declaration.”

Shannon watched the front door open and shut around two men from the feed mill. “Maybe I’m not on a side.”

Emily’s expression said that was no longer credible. “Wade came by the office yesterday.”

“What did he want?”

“To ask whether our office intended to make an issue of the 2021 file at the next hearing.” Emily lifted one shoulder. “Also to stand in my doorway like he owned the hinges.”

“That sounds right.”

“He asked if you’d been in the courtroom Thursday.”

Shannon set down her spoon. “And what did you say?”

“That you have a constitutional right to boredom.” Emily’s mouth twitched. “Then he asked if you were asking questions.”

“Are you telling me this as my sister or as counsel for the defense?”

“As your sister, because he was not really asking me about the defense.” Emily’s eyes moved over Shannon’s face. “What is this, exactly?”

Shannon looked past her to the pie case, where coconut and chocolate sat under glass domes fogged from the kitchen. “Nothing.”

“That’s a bad answer.”

“It’s the only one I’ve got.”

Emily let the silence sit. Finally she said, “The land matter isn’t clean.”

“No.”

“Not just because there’s a dispute file. Because access across that west strip has been messy for years. Easements, old family permissions, livestock crossings, creek washouts. Half the county still uses roads by permission that was never written down. If the state starts talking like every fence line out there is settled and secure, they’re lying from convenience.”

Shannon looked up. “Specific enough for court?”

Emily shook her head. “Not yet. But enough to matter.”

Rosie arrived with a plate of two scrambled eggs and toast and set it in front of Shannon anyway. “You can fight me or eat.”

Shannon stared at her. “I didn’t order this.”

“I know what you didn’t order.” Rosie thumped Emily lightly on the shoulder with a fresh pot of coffee and moved off.

Emily smothered a laugh. “Rosie’s the last benevolent dictatorship in Mississippi.”

Shannon broke off a piece of toast mostly to keep from saying what had risen to her tongue, which was that she had not been able to stop thinking about Eli Turner since he looked up from the defense table and found her in the back row like she belonged in the same room as his trouble.

Instead she said, “Who else had access to the Turner shed?”

Emily lowered her voice. “That I can’t tell you from file. I can tell you, generally speaking, rural properties with dead fathers, moved-away brothers, borrowed equipment, cousins, old workers, and no money for proper gates are not fortresses.”

“General advice from counsel.”

“Exactly.”

The bell over the diner door rang again. Wade came in with another deputy, sun on his shoulders. He paused half a beat when he saw their booth, then walked on to the counter without acknowledging them.

Emily blew out a breath through her nose. “And there goes your weather report.”

Shannon looked down at her eggs. “He’s not my anything.”

Emily let that lie there, too.

The deputy with Wade was laughing about something to do with catfish and a citation. Wade was not listening much. He took the stool that gave him a straight view of the door and, by extension, of their booth reflected in the pie case glass.

Parking facing the door. Sitting where he could see the exits.

She cut into the eggs she had not wanted and tasted almost nothing.

She had gotten home from Mize with a sack of dog food and a headache behind one eye. The dog met her at the car, tail making hopeful, impractical circles, and immediately tried to nose into the sack as she carried it to the porch. She fended him off with her knee, set the bag inside, then came back out to lift the porch light globe and swat the dead bugs out of it because she had meant to do that for a week.

A truck door shut across the yard before she heard the engine cut. She looked up and saw Wade stepping out of his county SUV, parked at the edge of her gravel as if he had considered coming all the way in and thought better of it.

He did not call out. He crossed the yard with that same stripped-down purpose he brought everywhere, eyes taking in the house, the road, the porch, the dog, her.

Shannon set the light globe carefully on the porch rail. “You need something official?”

“Yes.”

She waited.

He stopped at the bottom step. The dog trotted over, gave Wade’s boots a suspicious sniff, then decided anyone arriving in a government vehicle might have snacks and sat down hard. Wade ignored him.

“I told you to stay on the back bench,” he said.

She laughed once, unbelieving. “That’s your official business?”

“I told you to stay out of the middle. You haven’t.”

“I sat in a courtroom.”

“You’ve been askin’ questions.”

“According to who?”

“According to half the square and your sister’s face.”

“Emily’s face is not evidence.”

“No,” he said. “But Janice Brewer talkin’ at church supper like you cross-examined her over layer feed counts for somethin’.”

Heat came into her neck. “Janice Brewer does not require much to feel cross-examined.”

Wade took one step closer to the porch, not enough to crowd, enough to shift the angle. “Shannon.”

“Don’t.”

“Don’t what?”

“Say my name like I’m halfway to some cliff you’ll have to talk me off.”

His expression tightened. “I’m saying it because you’re not hearing the rest.”

“Then try saying the rest plain.”

“All right.” He looked straight at her. “Stay out of it.”

The yard went very still around them. Somewhere farther off a mower droned. The dog scratched his collar with his hind foot and flopped over against Wade’s shin as if this were not the first hostile exchange they’d had on Shannon’s property.

“Why?” she asked.

“Because it’s not your business.”

“That’s thin.”

He lifted his eyes to the porch light she had half disassembled, then back to her. “This county will decide you’re tied to whatever side you keep standing near. You know that.”

“Watching a hearing is not standing near.”

“Questioning every version you hear is.”

“So what is it, Wade? You worried about me or worried about the case?”

“Both.”

“At least that’s honest.”

He took another step, now close enough that she could see the faint stubble under his evening shave and the sweat dried pale at the edge of his collar. “You don’t know what this is.”

“I know what I’ve seen.”

“Which is almost nothing.”

“Then why are you so bothered that I saw it?”

His mouth flattened. The question landed, and she knew it had because he looked away for the first time since crossing the yard. Not far. Just toward the road, where a pickup went by slow, driver glancing over as if even traffic on Old River Road could feel a scene taking shape.

When Wade looked back, his voice was quieter. “Because you have a way of tugging on threads once you catch one.”

She folded her arms, partly because the evening had cooled and partly because he was too close now for comfort. “That is not a crime.”

“No. But it makes people notice you.”

“People already notice me. I came back divorced to the town where I grew up. That bought me six months of free observation from every pew and produce aisle in Marlow.”

“This is different.”

“How?”

He exhaled through his nose, controlled. “Because the names on this aren’t just Turner. It’s Culpepper too.”

The name sat between them with its own pressure.

Shannon looked at him. “And?”

“And that means there’s weight on it you don’t understand.”

She almost said, Don’t tell me what I understand. Instead she said, “You keep using that word like it’s a shield. Weight. Pressure. Folks.” She uncrossed her arms. “Say what you mean.”

He held her eyes. “I mean that if Culpepper stock got stolen and the case goes sideways, it won’t stay in court papers. It will spill into roads and jobs and people you know. I mean that if Eli Turner didn’t act alone, or if someone thinks he didn’t, asking the wrong question in the wrong place can put your name in somebody’s mouth who shouldn’t have it there. I mean I don’t want that.”

She looked past him at his SUV, the county seal on the door gone dusty. “You don’t get to decide what I want.”

“This isn’t about what you want.”

“Then stop standing in my yard like it is.”

For the first time he looked angry.

“You think I’m trying to run your life.”

“I think you’ve gotten very comfortable arranging the edges of it.”

“That’s not fair.”

“No?” Her voice stayed level, and that made it cut cleaner. “You came into Rosie’s and told me about my own porch light. You knew my schedule. You know my door latch sticks. You notice when I sleep and where I sit in court. You warn my sister off on my behalf.” She let that stand. “Tell me what part of that is fair.”

He put one hand on his hip, thumb hooked in his belt. “I fixed things for you because they needed fixing.”

“I didn’t ask.”

“You shouldn’t have to ask for some things.”

“See, that right there.” She stepped to the edge of the porch, bringing herself eye level with him in a different way. “That is exactly the part you don’t hear.”

He went still.

“Maybe I do not want to be provided for as a default,” she said. “Maybe I don’t want somebody deciding what I should have noticed, or needed, or accepted, and then calling it care after the fact.”

His eyes stayed on hers, unreadable for a second too long. “You think that’s what this is.”

“I think you don’t know when concern turns into control.”

The dog got up and moved away, wise enough to leave weather when it broke overhead.

Wade spoke after a beat that scraped on both of them. “You want plain? Fine. I’m concerned because every time I look up lately, you’re near him.”

Shannon felt something low and hot move through her, half anger, half the shame of being understood from the side. “That’s a reach.”

“Is it?”

“Yes.”

He studied her. “Then tell me I’m wrong.”

She said, “This isn’t about attraction.”

His face closed a degree. “Didn’t say it was.”

She looked down at the porch boards once, then back up. “I’m interested because the story doesn’t fit.”

“And because he looked at you once in court?”

She did not move. Neither did he. The distance between them had become small enough to measure in breaths.

Finally she said, “You need to leave.”

“Answer the question.”

“No.”

His mouth hardened. “That answer’s answer enough.”

She stepped back from the porch edge. “Leave.”

He did not move for one terrible second. Then he looked at the light globe on the rail, the bag of dog food just inside the screen door, the old latch plate gouged bright from years of sticking, and something in him eased or hid.

“You are too smart to wander into this by pride,” he said.

“Maybe you should try not driving into it by habit.”

The words hit. He nodded once, very slight, as if acknowledging a clean shot. Then he turned and walked back across the yard.

At his SUV door he stopped and looked back. The dusk had flattened color from everything but the sky.

“If you need anything at this house,” he said, “you call me before you call anybody else.”

“I won’t,” she said.

He stood a second more, then got in and backed out without another word.

After he left, Shannon stayed on the porch with the dead bugs in the light globe and the screwdriver beside them until the mosquitoes found her ankles and the dark turned the road to a strip of absence between ditches. Only then did she go in and lock the back door with the same lift-and-pull motion it had always needed.

Chapter 3

The First Conversation

The first real conversation with Eli happened because Emily left a file on Shannon’s passenger seat and Shannon, distracted, forgot to give it back that evening.

The next morning Emily called at eight-ten, already annoyed. “Do not look at it.”

“I haven’t.”

“Good. Bring it by the office before lunch.”

Shannon put the manila folder on the passenger seat beside the coffee she’d picked up at Rosie’s drive-up window. She told herself the file was just a file. Emily left legal pads and grocery lists and a cardigan in Shannon’s car at least once a month. It was ordinary. So was driving through the square at ten-thirty and finding the courthouse side street blocked by two work trucks unloading copy paper for the chancery office, which meant she had to park behind Vance & Harlow instead.

The law office sat in a converted brick storefront with narrow windows and a brass plate beside the door. Inside it smelled like toner, lemon oil, and old books. The reception desk was empty. Somewhere in back a printer ran and stopped. Shannon heard voices before she saw anyone—Emily’s low and brisk, Mr. Vance’s gentler, another voice she did not know at first because she had only heard him say yes, sir and no, sir in court.

She stopped in the hallway with the file tucked under her arm.

The conference room door stood partly open. Emily was at the table with her glasses on, one hand flat over a spread of papers. Mr. Vance leaned against the credenza, reading from a document. Eli sat nearest the door in a straight chair that looked too formal for him, forearms on his thighs, attention fixed on the page in Vance’s hand.

He wore a clean T-shirt under an open work shirt, sleeves rolled once. Not court clothes, not his Sunday best. Civilian again.

Emily saw Shannon first and straightened. Surprise flashed, then caution.

“You forgot this,” Shannon said, holding up the folder.

Mr. Vance looked over his glasses. “Morning, Shannon.”

“Sorry to interrupt.”

“You’re not.” Emily crossed to the door and took the file, eyes warning. “Give me two minutes.”

Shannon nodded and stepped back into the reception area. There was a faded sofa against one wall and a table stacked with old Mississippi Bar journals no one had ever read on purpose. She sat because standing there would make her look like she was waiting for something specific.

Through the thin wall she could hear only the shape of conversation, not the words. A chair scraped. A faucet ran in the kitchenette. Somebody’s boots crossed the back hall. The office was too small not to hold everybody’s proximity.

After a minute the conference room door opened. Mr. Vance came out first, already thumbing through a legal pad. Emily followed him toward the copy room, saying something about easement surveys. Eli stepped into the reception area last, maybe headed for the side door to the parking lot.

He stopped when he saw her looking up.

The room did not get smaller exactly, but it got more exact.

“Morning,” she said.

“Morning.”

He put one hand lightly on the back of the sofa, not leaning, just giving himself a place to stop. Up close she could see the shave shadow on his face and the tiredness around his eyes that court distance had hidden. The bruise near his thumb had yellowed at the edge.

“I didn’t mean to interrupt,” she said.

“I know.”

She glanced toward the hall where Emily’s voice still moved in pieces. “You made bond.”

“Yeah.”

Bond had been posted late the night before, quick enough to quiet some of the talk without stopping it.

“How are they treating you?”

His gaze moved over her face once, reading something, then settled. “Like I’m charged.”

A corner of her mouth almost lifted. “Fair enough.”

He looked toward the front windows where the square passed in fragments between the blinds. “You was at the hearing.”

“I was.”

“Why?”

“I wanted to hear it for myself.”

He nodded once. Silence fell. Not awkward yet. Just spare.

She could hear the copier in the back start up again, pages pulling through. Eli’s hand left the sofa back and went to the inside of his elbow, rubbing once as if he had old soreness there.

“People are talking,” she said before deciding whether she should.

“Yeah.”

“That bother you?”

His eyes came back to her. “You think I got a say in it?”

“No.”

“Then no use lettin’ it bother me much.” He looked toward the blinds. “My mother heard it in line at the Piggly before Vance got me out. That part gets old.”

Emily’s footsteps came down the hall. Eli spoke first. “You ought not ask around too much.”

Shannon held his gaze. “That advice from you or your lawyer.”

“Both.”

“Why?”

His expression did not change. “Folks fill in blanks meaner when they think somebody’s lookin’ for one.”

Emily emerged with a stack of copied pages in hand, taking in the shape of the room in one quick sweep. “All set?”

“Just leaving,” Shannon said.

Eli stepped aside to give her room to pass, though there was enough already. As she moved by him, close enough to catch clean soap and sun-warmed cotton under the courthouse-paper smell of the office, she felt the exactness of not touching. Her arm stayed close to her side. So did his. The narrow band of air between them seemed to have texture.

Then she was past him, at the door, hand on the brass push plate.

“Thank you,” she said, not sure what for.

He gave the smallest shake of his head.

Outside, the sun off the square made her blink. She stood on the sidewalk a second before moving toward her car.

Emily came out after her and shut the door harder than necessary. “What did you say to him?”

“Hello. Congratulations on your bond. Lovely weather.”

“Shannon.”

“He told me not to ask around too much.”

Emily’s expression changed, concern mixing with annoyance. “Then you should listen to him.”

“I am listening. Doesn’t mean I’ll obey.”

Emily rubbed the bridge of her nose. “You are impossible in a very quiet way.”

“That’s family stock,” Shannon said, and got in her car before Emily could answer.

She did not look back at the office windows as she pulled out, and because she did not, she knew exactly when she was passing them.

She noticed Colt Hayes the same way she noticed everything else: because he did not fit the frame cleanly.

It was Friday at the Marlow Co-op lot, where Mize had sent her to pick up a pallet of feed mineral that had come in on the wrong truck. The co-op yard sat beyond the gin and always smelled like diesel, grain dust, and old rain caught in tin roofing. Men moved in and out of the loading bay with clipboards and forklifts and the weary competence of people who did not waste motion if they could help it.

Shannon parked beside a line of flatbeds and went into the office to find the delivery ticket. The place was busy enough that no one looked up at first. A radio near the coffee pot muttered farm report numbers. A stack of invoices leaned like a failed card trick beside the computer monitor.

The man at the counter was turned half away from her, signing something. Tall, broad through the shoulders in a way that came from work rather than intention, dark hair cut close at the sides, forearms browned hard by sun. He wore a faded pearl-snap shirt with the sleeves shoved up and a ball cap pushed back enough for her to see the line of his brow. There was grease ground into the heel of one hand that no amount of washing would have taken.

He said, “Need that spreader back by four, Jimmy, or I’m comin’ to get it myself,” in a voice low enough not to be a performance and flat enough that Jimmy probably believed him.

Jimmy laughed the kind of laugh men used when they knew a joke was covering a truth. “Yes, sir, Mr. Hayes.”

Mr. Hayes. Shannon filed the name because Hayes land lay north of the river and because she vaguely remembered a Hayes boy two grades ahead of Emily, maybe, or perhaps this one was too young for that and she was conflating people again. Marlow did that to memory. It stacked generations until names felt recycled.

The man turned then and stepped aside from the counter, and she recognized him with a half-second lag. Colt Hayes. She had seen him around town over the years without really seeing him—at football games in hooded jackets, in line at the bank, once at Rosie’s with three other men and mud on his boots. He was one of those county fixtures a person could fail to register until proximity sharpened the outline.

His eyes moved over her face in the polite, trying-to-place way of small-town acquaintance, then away. Nothing lingered. He picked up a box of hydraulic fittings from the floor.

The office girl behind the counter finally looked up. “Help you?”

“Mize Farm & Garden,” Shannon said. “Y’all got our mineral pallet.”

The girl hunted for the invoice. Colt shifted the box to one arm and opened the office door with the other for a teenager carrying in a coil of hose. Shannon noticed the scar along his jaw only because he passed near enough for it to show pale against the tan. A healed thing.

“Mize,” the girl said, finding the paper. “Yeah, loading bay two. They’ll fork it for you.”

“Thanks.”

Shannon took the invoice and turned back toward the yard. As she reached the door, Colt held it again, already stepping out himself.

“Careful by bay two,” he said. “They dropped feed there this morning. Slick under the dust.”

She nodded. “Appreciate it.”

He tipped his chin once and kept going.

By the time she got to bay two, she had already dismissed the noticing. Men existed in the county without meaning anything. The loading bay was full of broken pallets and forklift alarms and two workers arguing over whether the shipment tags were color-coded for cattle or equine. It swallowed attention easily.

Still, later, when she told Emily about the visit to the co-op for no particular reason, she heard herself say, “Colt Hayes was there.”

Emily, sorting mail at Shannon’s kitchen counter, did not look up fast enough. “Was he.”

Shannon glanced over. “Why’d you say that like I’d missed a memo.”

Emily folded a utility bill. “No reason.”

“Emily.”

“What? He’s just… there. Around.”

“That is the least useful description of a person I’ve heard this month.”

Emily smiled into the mail. “Then it’s accurate.”

It was Wednesday after work. Shannon had gone back to Rosie’s because she was too tired to cook.

Emily met her there late, fresh from the office in a navy blouse and sensible heels she’d kick off under the table within five minutes.

Rosie’s had thinned to the supper crowd that came in after shifts and before ballgames. Ceiling fans turned the same air over and over. Television above the pie case played weather with the sound off. A table of volunteer firemen occupied the back, plates shoved aside, loud in a way that meant no one minded.

Shannon and Emily sat in a booth by the window with sweating glasses of tea. Emily was telling a story about a woman at the clerk’s office who had filed a quitclaim deed with the notary line unsigned and then cried when told it would not, in fact, materialize into legal effect by force of will.

“People think documents are wishes with staples,” Emily said.

“That seems unfair to wishes.”

The bell over the door rang. Shannon looked up because the evening light behind the glass made everybody entering into a silhouette first. Colt Hayes came in with two men Shannon recognized from the county road crew. They paused near the register to speak to Rosie. One of the road crew men peeled off toward the bathroom. The other slid into a booth.

Colt stayed standing with one hand on the back of the bench, listening while Rosie talked and pointed a spatula toward the kitchen. He laughed at something, brief and low. Then he turned and scanned the room for a seat, gaze moving across tables without sticking until it passed over Shannon’s booth and stopped.

Not on Shannon.

On Emily.

Emily looked up at the same moment, recognized him, and gave the slightest nod.

Colt came over because the room left him the angle to. He rested his knuckles once on the top of the booth divider.

“Evenin’, Emily.”

“Colt.”

Shannon looked between them. Emily made no move to explain.

“You still need that survey copy?” Colt asked.

Emily took a sip of tea first, making him wait half a beat. “I need the signed page from 2018 if your uncle found it.”

“He found somethin’. Whether it’s signed is another matter.”

“That usually matters.”

“So I’ve been told.” His mouth shifted like a private concession. “I can bring it by tomorrow.”

“Bring it by Mr. Vance’s office, not the courthouse. The clerk lost patience with your family in 1997.”

“That late?”

Emily almost smiled. “Don’t make it worse.”

He looked at Shannon then, as if remembering he was standing at a booth with two sisters and one of them had not spoken yet.

“Evenin’,” he said to her.

“Evening.”

“Y’all all right?”

Shannon nodded. “We’re managing.”

He tipped his chin and left them there, going back to his table.

When he sat down, Emily’s fingers stayed around her tea glass a moment longer than before.

Shannon said, “Survey copy.”

Emily looked bland. “For work.”

“I can hear the defensiveness from here.”

Emily slid one look across the table. “Do not start.”

“Start what?”

“Whatever sisterly narrative you’re building because a man asked whether I still needed a document.”

Shannon took a bite of her grilled chicken sandwich. “I wasn’t building anything.”

“That is visibly false.”

Shannon chewed, swallowed. “He seems capable.”

Emily snorted. “That’s the kind of thing women say when they notice a man’s shoulders and want to sound objective.”

Shannon stared at her.

Emily’s brows lifted. “See? Nobody likes being observed back.”

Rosie saved them from the next line by arriving with a refill pitcher and a comment about the weather radar. Across the room Colt said something to the road crew men and all three bent over a phone screen. Shannon let it go. Or appeared to.

Chapter 4

What the Records Said

The first piece came from the tax assessor’s office, though she had not gone there for that. Mr. Mize sent her to drop off an agricultural exemption form because his nephew “couldn’t be trusted not to charm everybody into a delay.” Shannon took the folder and went, grateful for an errand that counted as work.

The tax office occupied a fluorescent suite on the courthouse annex side, all metal shelves and low partitions. Ms. Bernice at the front desk wore reading glasses on a chain and had known Shannon since she was eleven and tried to pay library fines in Canadian coins.

“Lord, look at you,” Bernice said as if the shock were new. “Every time I see you, you’re still somebody’s little girl and also not. What a trick.”

“I practice.”

Bernice stamped Mize’s form with more force than necessary. “Tell him if he misses another deadline, I’m charging him extra for my patience.”

“I’ll relay the threat.”

As Bernice turned to put the form in the out tray, a rolled plat map slid halfway off the shelf beside her desk and dropped against the cabinet. Shannon bent automatically to catch it. The paper was old, linen-backed, edges browned. Across the corner in block script: SAND CREEK SECTION 14 REVISED ACCESS ROAD.

Bernice saw what she was holding. “Don’t you open that. It’ll disintegrate out of spite.”

Shannon smiled and set it carefully back. “No faith in county storage?”

“No faith in county humidity.” Bernice lowered her voice as if the file cabinets might hear. “Why you lookin’ at Sand Creek maps anyway?”

“I wasn’t.”

“Mhm.”

Shannon leaned one hip against the counter. “Everybody’s saying Turner land like it’s one thing. Emily made it sound like that wasn’t true.”

Bernice gave her the assessing look women over sixty reserved for younger women who were half asking and half pretending otherwise. Then she glanced toward the back cubicles. No one close.

“Depends what year you ask,” she said. “Junior Turner inherited the west parcel proper. Creek shifted. Fence lines shifted with it. There’s an old lane crossing the north strip that was never deeded clean because folks used to just agree to things and keep moving.”

“Whose lane?”

“Used to be Bryant land before Culpepper bought around it in pieces.” Bernice clicked her pen against the desk. “Then Turner stock crossed there some. Then Culpepper timber trucks wanted wider access. Then everybody got lawyers because somebody put up a gate where there hadn’t been a gate.”

Shannon kept her face neutral. “In 2021.”

Bernice’s eyes narrowed. “You don’t know that from me.”

“I know it from my sister existing.”

“Well.” Bernice adjusted her glasses. “That suit never resolved all the practical access. They settled enough to stop hemorrhaging money, not enough to stop hating each other. Best kind of county solution.”

“What does that mean on the ground?”

“It means there are stretches out there where one man thinks he owns the road, another thinks he only owns the dirt under it, and a third thinks his granddaddy promised free use in 1974 so none of it applies.” Bernice leaned closer. “It also means somebody could pull in and out of those parcels at night without every approach being as private as folks are now making it sound.”

Shannon let a beat pass. “Could Eli.”

Bernice shrugged. “Could Eli. Could half the people who ever hauled hay out west.” She handed over Mize’s receipt copy. “I’m not sayin’ he didn’t do a thing. I’m sayin’ maps and stories are two different problems.”

The second piece came from the courthouse records room, where Shannon should not have been but could be because the old easement filings were public and she knew how to ask for them like somebody looking up a boring family matter.

The records clerk, a college girl home for summer with acrylic nails too long for paper handling, disappeared into the back and came out with a cardboard tray of plats, petitions, and a survey from 2018 signed by a Hayes. She took the tray to the metal table near the back, the one she had started using without thinking about it, where people worked without being in anyone’s way. Shannon spent forty-five minutes there reading handwriting that leaned in legal loops across yellowing pages.

The names on the filings braided together more than town talk admitted. Turner. Bryant. Culpepper Timber Holdings. Hayes Surveying & Earthworks on one set of references, though that might be an uncle or cousin rather than Colt himself. An easement petition noted “customary agricultural passage” across a lane bordering the north edge of the Turner tract to reach watered grazing. A later filing disputed the width of allowable vehicle access after timber cutting damaged ruts near the creek. A handwritten notation in the margin of one copy—likely from a lawyer’s prep, not official—read: south gate installed after dispute / prior open use contested.

She copied the filing numbers onto a receipt stub in her purse because writing them on anything too formal felt like admission.

When she left the records room, she nearly ran into Mr. Amos Reed on the annex steps. He had been county road foreman for thirty years before retiring and now spent most mornings in a seersucker cap telling younger men how drainage used to work when people respected ditches.

“Joan’s girl,” he said, as if this were a surprise. “You after deeds?”

“Something like that.”

He glanced at the courthouse, then at her hand where the receipt stub showed. “Sand Creek?”

She ought to have learned by now that in Marlow the safest way to keep a question from growing was sometimes to answer it halfway. “I was looking at old access filings.”

Mr. Reed grunted. “Mess out there. Always has been.”

“Because of the Turners?”

“Because of everybody.” He hooked his thumbs under his suspenders. “Culpepper bought too fast, Turner held too hard, Bryant sold pieces he hadn’t fully thought through, and the creek don’t care what any deed says. Washed the cut bank out in nineteen and folks still actin’ shocked roads moved.”

“Could a livestock trailer get through the north lane at night?”

Reed gave her a keen look, seeing more of the question than she offered. “Depends how wet. Depends who left what gate where. Depends whether you’re askin’ can it be done or can it be done without most people knowin’.”

“Either.”

“It can be done.” He spat neatly into the weeds by the steps. “Problem is, after the 2021 suit, everybody started rememberin’ the lane different.”

“That convenient?”

“That human.”

He started down the steps, then paused. “One thing though.”

Shannon waited.

“They keep sayin’ south pasture like that stock walked out of one neat place.” He shook his head. “Culpepper leases a strip by Sand Creek from old Mrs. Bryant’s nephew to square off the grazing. Don’t know if that was in the papers you read. Folks say own when they mean control.”

“Lease?” she said.

“For water access, last I heard. Been a few years.” He looked at her a moment. “That matter?”

“I don’t know yet.”

He nodded as if that was a decent answer and went on.

That evening at Emily’s office, Shannon stopped by under the pretext of dropping off a casserole dish their mother had returned through the wrong daughter. Emily was alone at the front desk, shoes off, typing with one hand and eating peanuts with the other.

“You look sneaky,” Emily said.

“I brought pyrex.”

“That is not the sneaky part.”

Shannon set the dish down. “Was Culpepper grazing some of that Sand Creek strip under lease instead of outright ownership?”

Emily’s hands stilled over the keyboard. “Where’d you hear that.”

“So yes.”

Emily shut her eyes briefly. “You have got to stop sounding pleased when you’re right.”

Shannon leaned on the counter. “Is it true?”

Emily blew out a breath. “There are overlapping interests. Lease, maintenance rights, old crossing permissions. None of which makes cattle any less stolen if they were stolen.”

“I know that.”

“Do you? Because you’re collecting procedural irregularities like they add up to innocence.”

“They add up to not simple.”

“That I’ll grant.” Emily pushed the peanut jar aside. “One concrete thing that bothered Mr. Vance—don’t ask how I know, because I won’t answer—was where exactly the cut tags were found. Early incident notes say in a feed sack near the shed wall. Later inventory calls them recovered from inside the locked shed.”

“But the shed wasn’t locked.”

“According to whom?”

“Several people.”

Emily spread one hand. “Exactly. Somewhere between first telling and formal file, details are hardening.”

“Hardened by who?”

Emily gave her a flat look. “You know I can’t say that.”

The office printer kicked on. Shannon watched the first page feed through. “Did Eli use that north lane regularly?”

Emily hesitated, just enough to tell her yes before the answer came. “His father did. Eli did when he had reason. Feed runs. Fence repairs. Getting to the creek side.” She rubbed her forehead. “The issue is, a thing can be customary and still become incriminating if the relationship sours.”

“Meaning access starts looking like opportunity.”

Emily pointed a peanut at her. “That was almost lawyerly.”

“I’m very worried.”

A smile flickered and vanished. “There’s something else. The state’s talking as if Turner land equals Eli’s place, singular. But his name’s not on every parcel people are pointing at. Some of it’s still in his father’s estate. Some in joint title with the brother who moved to Alabama. One small corner borders land used by two other men for equipment storage by handshake agreement.” Emily let that sink in. “So when people say they found things on his property, that phrase is doing a lot of work.”

Shannon’s fingers tightened on the casserole lid. “Does Wade know that.”

Emily looked at her for a long moment. “Of course he knows.”

She picked up the empty dish again. “Thanks.”

Emily eyed her. “That wasn’t for your use.”

“Then maybe stop feeding me pieces.”

“Maybe stop asking with your whole face.”

Shannon went home by the long road west of town, windows down, dust lifting behind the car. She passed the turnoff toward Sand Creek without taking it.

Emily texted at eleven: Can you bring lunch? I’m buried. Turkey on wheat. No tomato.

Shannon was free for an hour, so she picked up sandwiches at the deli counter in the back of the pharmacy and carried them to Vance & Harlow in a paper sack already damp at the bottom from pickle brine. The reception desk was empty again. She heard no voices this time, only the murmur of a portable AC unit straining against the heat.

Emily called from the back, “Leave it there if you love me.”

“I don’t, but I’ll leave it.”

Shannon set the sack on the desk. As she turned to go, the side door from the alley opened and Eli came in, holding it with one shoulder while he maneuvered a battered cardboard tube and two rolled plats. He stopped when he saw her.

For a second neither of them spoke. He looked from her to the lunch sack, then toward the back hall where Emily still rustled papers.

“She’s in there,” Shannon said.

“Yeah.”

He shifted the tube in his hand. One of the plats started to slip and she caught the end of it before it hit the floor. Her fingers closed over brittle paper and the edge of his knuckles brushed hers, brief and dry as static.

“Sorry,” he said.

“It’s fine.”

She held the roll steady while he got a better grip. Up close, she could see the red clay dried in the welt of one boot sole and a nick along the side of his thumb that looked newer than the bruise had been. He smelled faintly of cut wood and heat.

“What are those?” she asked.

“Surveys.”

“For Vance?”

“Yeah.”

He took the roll from her carefully, making sure not to pull it against her hand.

Emily called again from the back, “Whoever that is, I will name my next child after you if you brought food.”

Eli’s mouth shifted, almost there and gone. “She says that to everybody?”

“Only people carrying lunch or documents.”

He nodded toward the sandwich sack. “Then I ought’ve brought food.”

She should have stepped aside, let him pass, let the moment close. Instead she heard herself say, “Emily told me some of the land’s still in your father’s estate.”

His face did not close exactly. It leveled.

“Did she.”

“I didn’t ask for file material.”

“Didn’t say you did.”

The air conditioner rattled in the window. A truck down on the square hit a pothole hard enough to jolt something in its bed.

“People keep saying your place like it’s one fenced square,” she said. “It isn’t.”

“No.”

The answer hung there. She waited.

Finally he said, “My daddy never finished probatin’ half what he should’ve before he died. Then he died. That’s about the size of it.”

“So some of it isn’t clean title.”

“No.” His gaze shifted once toward the front windows. “Some of it’s title. Some’s use. Some’s just what folks always done till somebody got mean enough or rich enough to write it down.”

“Culpepper,” she said.

His eyes came back to her. “You said it.”

“I can.”

“You can do a lot of things.”

She leaned one shoulder against the desk because standing there squarely had started to feel like confrontation. “Did your father fight them over the road.”

“He fought most people over most things by the end.”

“Is that why people talk about him like he was trouble?”

Eli looked at her for a beat that nearly qualified as surprise. “People talk about him like that?”

She let him have the smallest look.

His mouth moved once, humorless. “Then yeah. Maybe.”

Emily appeared at the end of the hall with reading glasses in her hair and stopped dead when she saw them together. “I leave a room for thirty seconds.”

“I brought lunch,” Shannon said.

“I see that.” Emily took in the surveys, then Eli. “Mr. Vance wants those in conference.”

Eli nodded and started past Shannon. At the doorway to the back hall he stopped without turning. “My daddy didn’t trust Culpepper fences,” he said.

“Why not?”

He turned then, just enough for her to see his face in profile. “Said a fence built by a man with more lawyers than cows ain’t really for holdin’ stock.”

Emily made a faint impatient noise that was mostly nerves. Eli ignored it.

Shannon said, “Did you believe him?”

He looked at her.

“About fences?” he said. “Sometimes.”

Then he went down the hall with the survey rolls.

Shannon stood there with the AC rattling and the pharmacy sandwich sack sweating through onto Emily’s desk blotter.

Emily came and snatched up the lunch bag. “What did he tell you.”

“That his father didn’t trust Culpepper fences.”

Emily’s brows rose despite herself. “Well. That’s something.”

“Is it?”

“In this office, anything somebody says without being compelled is something.” Emily glanced back down the hall. “You need to leave before Mr. Vance starts charging you paralegal rates.”

Shannon looked toward the conference room door, shut now. “He said his father fought everybody by the end.”

“That I could have told you for free.”

“But he said it.”

Emily studied her face a second too long. “Don’t build intimacy out of scraps.”

“I’m not.”

“Mhm.”

Shannon picked up her purse and went out through the front office. On the sidewalk, she paused with the glass door still swinging shut behind her. My daddy never finished probatin’ half what he should’ve. A fence built by a man with more lawyers than cows ain’t really for holdin’ stock.

That afternoon the heat had broken into a low gray sky without rain, the kind that made the whole county feel suspended and irritable. Shannon left Mize early because the pallet truck had gone down and Mr. Mize turned sentimental about “everybody taking the blessing of an extra hour.” She drove west out of town with no plan more precise than motion.

She did not take the road to Old River at first. She took the Sand Creek road.

The turnoff came under a stand of loblolly, shaded enough to smell of pine sap and wet dirt even dry. She told herself she only wanted to see the land once with actual boundaries in mind. North lane. South gate installed later. Creek strip under lease. It was one thing to hear access argued in offices and courthouse rooms. Another to understand what access meant where the ground rose, dipped, narrowed, opened.

She drove slow. Past a rusted mailbox with the numbers half shot off. Past a pasture with three horses under the tree line. Past a stretch of ditch where Queen Anne’s lace leaned white and ragged against the fescue. The road turned from asphalt to patched gravel and back again. When she reached the split where one lane slanted toward the creek and another climbed west between wire fences, she pulled off onto the shoulder and cut the engine.

Silence after the car ticked cool was never true silence in the country. Cicadas. A far chain saw. Somewhere cattle calling, the sound carrying flatter than human voices ever did.

She got out and stood by the hood.

Fences ran in pieces and joins, some old barbed wire gone loose between posts, some newer stretch with bright staples. A gate down the lower lane hung on a chain and looked recently rehung—different wire twist at the top hinge than the bottom. Beyond it the ground sloped toward tree cover and, she guessed, the creek. To the west, a shed roof showed above scrub and sweetgum, more distant than the phrase Turner property made it seem in conversation.

A red-wing blackbird flashed up from the ditch. Shannon leaned her forearms on the hood and studied the gate.

There were tire marks in the mud by the shoulder, half dried, overlapping enough to be useless to any imagination except an overactive one. The road here could have carried anybody with a reason. Feed truck. Fence repair. Hunters. A deputy. A thief.

She heard an engine before she saw it. Slow, older, coming from the west lane. She straightened.

The pickup that emerged between the trees was Eli’s only because she knew him now well enough to know how he held a wheel. One hand high, one low, gaze forward as if driving was a task with no emotional content. The truck itself was dull blue under dust, older Ford, one dent over the rear wheel. He saw her and slowed further, then stopped with his front bumper a few yards from hers.

For a second neither of them moved.

Then he cut the engine and got out.

“This your car?” he asked, glancing at it as if checking whether she had company.

“Mine.”

He nodded. “You lost?”

“No.”

“Then you’re in worse shape.”

She looked at the gate again. “I wanted to see.”

“What.”

“The road.”

He came around the front of his truck and stopped on the other side of the ditch from her. Close enough to speak without raising his voice. He looked toward the lower lane she’d been studying.

“Road’s not much to look at.”

“Neither are court filings.”

“That so.”

She crossed her arms lightly, not defensive. The sky pressed lower, dull silver at the edges. “Your lawyer would probably prefer I weren’t here.”

“Yeah.”

“And you?”

His gaze moved to her, unreadable. “Depends why you came.”

She could give him a polished answer. Observation. Curiosity. Context. Instead she said, “I wanted to know if it made sense.”

He looked back at the gate. “And.”

“And it looks like more than one person could use it.”

“That a surprise.”

“No.”

“Then what don’t make sense.”

She took a breath. “Everybody talks like the state found a straight line from Culpepper pasture to your shed. It doesn’t look straight from here.”

When he spoke, his voice was low and even.

“Straight’s for court.”

She looked at him.

“Meaning?”

“Meaning a jury don’t want mud. They want boards laid end to end.” His eyes stayed on the lane. “Most folks do.”

The wind shifted, bringing the smell of creek rot and cut grass.

“Did you use this road that night?”

He turned his head and looked at her directly. No anger in it. No offense. Just a boundary.

“I told you before,” he said. “You ask too much.”

“And you answer very little.”

“Works better for me.”

She should have let the rebuke hold. Instead she found herself saying, “I’m not trying to trap you.”

He considered that. “I know.”

A drop hit the hood of her car. Then another, fat and slow, not yet rain, just the sky testing.

Eli glanced upward. “You don’t want this shoulder if it opens up. Clay’ll swallow you.”

She looked at her tires, then at the ditch. “I can back out.”

“Maybe.”

He came around the ditch line then, not all the way to her, but closer, eyeing the shoulder angle and the rut by her rear tire. He pointed. “If you cut hard right too soon, you’ll slide that back wheel.”

She stepped aside to let him see. The move put them near enough that she could hear the rasp of denim at his knee when he crouched to look at the rut. He reached toward the tire, checked the softness of the shoulder with two fingers, and stood again in one clean motion.

“Turn left first,” he said. “Then back. Don’t spin.”

The sky darkened another shade. Rain would either arrive or keep threatening for an hour. Shannon moved toward the driver door. Eli stepped with her, probably only to keep pointing out the angle, but the space between car and ditch was narrow. Her boot slipped on the grass turned slick by the first drops.

It was not a dramatic slip. Just enough that her balance went and she caught herself against the car at the same moment Eli’s hand came out.

He did not grab her. His fingers closed around her upper arm for one brief, firm second while she steadied. Warm through the cotton of her sleeve. Callused palm. Then he let go immediately.

“You all right.”

“Yes.”

Her voice sounded lower than normal to her own ears. She stayed braced against the car a moment longer than necessary because moving right away would have looked like reacting. Rain ticked louder on the hood now, scattered.

“Left first,” he said again, eyes on the back tire rather than on her. “Then back.”

“Okay.”

He nodded and moved toward the front of her car so he could watch the wheel clear the rut. Shannon opened the driver door and got in. Her hands felt steady enough until she reached for the key and found they were not, quite.

She started the engine. Rain hit the windshield in heavier drops but still not full. Eli stood by the shoulder, one hand up to indicate the direction. She followed it. Left first. Then back. The rear wheel slid half an inch and caught. She corrected. The car eased free onto firmer gravel.

When she rolled down the window, he was by the ditch again, rain spotting his shirt dark at the shoulders.

“Thank you,” she said.

He rested one hand on the gate post chain, not leaning. “You ought not come out here alone if you don’t know the roads.”

“I know enough.”

“Mm.”

The rain thickened another degree. She should have driven on. Instead she said, “What did you mean before. Straight’s for court.”

Finally he said, “You ever see folks tell a story till they sand off every place it catches?”

“Yes.”

“That.”

She held the steering wheel and looked at him through the widening scatter of rain. “And what gets sanded off here.”

A beat passed.

“Depends who’s talkin’,” he said.

A truck sounded somewhere down the road behind her. Distant yet. Another engine on the lane.

Eli heard it too and straightened. The moment thinned at once, practical again.

“You better go,” he said.

“Because of the rain?”

“Because people notice trucks.”

She put the car in drive.

As she eased past him, he stepped back from the road edge. The window was still down two inches. Rain spattered in cool across her forearm.

“Eli,” she said before she could stop herself.

He looked at her.

She did not know what she meant to ask. Not really. So she asked the smallest true thing. “Did somebody use you because the story fit?”

His jaw worked once. “There’s always somebody will borrow what’s already true—”

Then the other truck came around the curve behind her, and the moment closed. Eli’s gaze shifted over her shoulder toward the approaching headlights.

“Go on,” he said.

She drove.

In the mirror she saw him once more through rain and dust, hand still on the gate chain, standing between road and field like he had grown there by accident and stubbornness.

By the time she reached asphalt, the rain had stopped without ever becoming real weather.

She told no one about Sand Creek.

She washed the car mud off at the self-service bay that evening and stood there with the wand in her hand, spray battering the wheel wells, while the sun broke under the cloud line and turned the puddles copper.

At home, the red dog met her in the yard carrying one of her gardening gloves in his mouth. She took it from him, fed him, and ate leftover chicken salad standing at the sink. After dark she pulled the docket page from her purse where it had lived too long and laid it on the table beside the receipt stub with the filing numbers and the gas station receipt and a grocery list that said peaches, detergent, dog food.

She sat at the table in the kitchen, one bare foot hooked under the rung of the chair, and looked at the paper until the words blurred into blocks. The house was quiet except for the refrigerator motor and the dog’s nails ticking once across the floorboards before he settled.

Her phone lit on the table. Emily: You alive? Mom says you forgot Sunday lunch.

Shannon looked at the message and did not answer right away. Then she typed: Got held up. Sorry.

Emily: By what.

Shannon stared at that longer.

At the back door, the latch gave its familiar little catch when the evening air shifted against it.

Instead she set the phone facedown, took the folded docket page and smoothed it flatter with the heel of her hand, then copied Eli Turner’s next court date in neat block letters onto the dry-erase calendar hung beside the refrigerator—between dog shots due and Mize inventory—using the black marker because it wrote darker than blue.

Emily had three yellow flags stuck to the same supplemental report and a coffee ring dried over the deputy’s signature.

Shannon stood in the doorway of the office until Emily looked up. The office was too small for the amount of paper in it. Discovery boxes were open on the floor. A legal pad sat on top of one with dates written down the margin in Emily’s narrow hand. There was a half-eaten pack of crackers by the keyboard and a fountain pen with the cap chewed white at the end.

“You look terrible,” Shannon said.

Emily snorted once. “Good morning to you too.”

Shannon stepped in and closed the door with her heel. “You called before eight. I assumed it was either bad or illegal.”

Emily slid the report across the desk with one finger. “Probably both.”

Shannon didn’t sit. She bent over the page instead. The words were the same ones she’d been seeing in different forms for two weeks now—inventory, location, access, recovery—but the phrasing had been cleaned up again. Not corrected. Cleaned.

Recovered from inside locked shed.

Not near shed wall. Not adjacent to outbuilding. Not west side of structure. Inside locked shed.

She looked up. “They went harder.”

“They went neater,” Emily said. “Which is worse.”

“Can you challenge it?”

“I can challenge anything. That doesn’t mean the judge is going to enjoy hearing me do it.” Emily took the report back and tapped one yellow flag. “This deputy didn’t write the first note. His trainee did. The trainee wrote ‘near shed wall.’ Then we get the typed inventory and it’s ‘inside locked shed.’ Then this morning I get a supplement using ‘inside locked shed’ like it has always been that. Like nobody ever said anything else.”

Shannon glanced toward the boxes. “You have the 2021 suit file?”

“Not enough of it.” Emily leaned back in her chair and rubbed her eyes with thumb and forefinger. “I pulled the docket. I’ve got scanned pleadings, not the exhibits. I need the exhibits. The photographs, especially. The gate installation affidavit. Anything that places that shed and that lane the way they actually were instead of how the state wishes they were.”

Shannon stayed quiet.

Emily opened one eye and looked at her. “Don’t.”

“I didn’t say anything.”

“You had a whole look.”

“It was the face you make right before you do something I told you not to do.”

Shannon folded her arms. “If the exhibits are in records, I know where records are.”

Emily sat forward. “No.”

“You need the file.”

“I need the file without my sister turning herself into an unpaid investigator in a felony case.”

Shannon looked down at the report again. The yellow flag had been placed beside the cleanest lie on the page. Locked shed. Turner property. Exclusive access. Story first. Then the pieces sanded to fit.

Emily watched her too closely. “Shannon.”

“Give me the case number.”

Emily said nothing.

Shannon lifted her chin a little. “If you don’t, I’ll ask Mrs. Lott for the whole Culpepper docket and go through every one till I hit the right year.”

“That is not the threat you think it is.”

“Case number.”

Emily blew out a tired breath through her nose, reached for the legal pad, tore off a sheet, and wrote something on it in quick, sharp strokes. She held it a second before handing it over.

“This is not me telling you to do anything,” she said.

“No.”

“This is me acknowledging that if I don’t give you the number, you’ll make a mess for at least three county employees before lunch.”

Emily pointed her pen at her. “You are looking for exhibits from the easement suit and anything filed with attachments around the gate dispute. Not criminal file, not sheriff records, not witness statements. You stay in civil land. You hear me?”

Shannon took the sheet. “I hear you.”

Emily let out another breath. “And if you happen to find photographs, look at the backs. Sometimes those old exhibits got marked by hand before they were scanned. Dates, directions, whatever. People write more carelessly when they think a thing’s temporary.”

Shannon folded the paper into her wallet.

Emily’s voice went flatter. “This is not helping him because you think he looks better in trouble.”

Shannon looked at her. “That what you think?”

“I think you’re not as detached as you keep trying to act.”

“That makes two of us, apparently.”

Emily held her stare for a beat longer, then looked away first, back at the flagged report. “Just bring me paper. Not theories. I have enough theories.”

Shannon nodded once and turned for the door.

“Shannon.”

She looked back.

Emily’s voice had softened, though not by much. “Paper only.”

The courthouse records office still smelled like lemon cleaner and old dust and the kind of dry air that came off copy machines run too long. Mrs. Lott sat behind the counter in a cardigan the color of oatmeal with half-moon glasses low on her nose and a crossword turned sideways under one elbow.

She looked up as Shannon came in. “You here for tags again?”

“Not tags.” Shannon set her purse on the counter. “Civil suit. Culpepper and Turner. Twenty-twenty-one.”

Mrs. Lott’s brows went up. “Everybody in this county fixing to become a lawyer?”

“Wouldn’t that be awful.”

Mrs. Lott sniffed in amusement and turned to her monitor. “File number?”

Shannon read it off the paper in her wallet.

Mrs. Lott typed with two fingers, frowning at the screen. “Scanned pleadings are here. Exhibits are archived.”

“I’ll wait.”

“That means downstairs.”

“I know.”

Mrs. Lott looked at her over the glasses. “You know you always say that like it’s a virtue.”

“It saves time.”

Mrs. Lott pushed back from the desk with a sigh that was mostly theater. “Help me God. You sure do take after your mother when you decide a thing.”

Shannon leaned on the counter while Mrs. Lott disappeared through the side door. She could hear the heavy metal door to the archive room open, then the muted rumble of rolling shelves. Somewhere farther down the hall a clerk laughed too loud and got shushed. The fluorescent lights buzzed overhead. On the wall near the door hung a framed photograph of the courthouse square before the awnings had been redone, all the brick facades bleached by sun.

Mrs. Lott came back with a banker’s box hugged against her middle and set it on the counter with a grunt.

“Don’t take anything out of order,” she said. “And if you get fingerprints all over glossy exhibits, I’m blaming you personally.”

Shannon lifted the lid.

The file smelled older than 2021. Dust and cardboard and the faint sweet rot of paper that had sat in heat one summer too many. Pleadings on top. Motions. Responses. A stack of oversized plats folded into quarters. Then exhibit envelopes clipped shut with rusted fasteners.

She took the first envelope out carefully and opened it on the counter. Photographs. Each one printed on letter paper with exhibit stickers in the corners. Fence posts. A gate. Tire tracks in dried mud. She flipped through slow at first, then faster. South gate from road. South gate from inside fence line. Chain and new post. Close shot of latch. The date stamp sat in the corner of each image.

Mrs. Lott had gone back to her crossword. Shannon kept flipping.

Exhibit 11: survey overlay. Exhibit 12: north lane approach. Exhibit 13: creek crossing after rain.

Exhibit 14 stopped her.

A low shed with a sagging tin roof and one door hanging crooked. The hasp had been torn away. A length of chain hung useless from a bent staple. Someone had written EAST FACE in blue marker across the bottom margin. Under that, smaller: old stock shed.

She took the photo closer. The door wasn’t just unlocked. There wasn’t much left to lock. Sunlight showed through a seam near the frame. Grass had grown right up to the threshold.

On the back, in hurried pen, was an exhibit notation and a date from September 2021.

She looked at the next photo. Same shed from farther back. The lane running beside it. The north lane, narrow and rutted, but open all the way down toward the creek turn. A mineral tub sat just past the corner of the shed. No gate in that frame. No barrier.

“Mrs. Lott.”

Mrs. Lott didn’t look up. “What now.”

“I need copies of fourteen and fifteen. Front and back.”

Mrs. Lott glanced over, saw the photographs, and sighed again. “Of course you do.”

Shannon kept going. There was an affidavit about installation of the south gate after the dispute. A contractor statement. A signed note by Culpepper’s attorney referring to “then-existing north lane access pending further resolution.” A deposition excerpt from a ranch hand named Chet Blevins.

She stopped on one page and read it again.

Question: Prior to gate installation, how did you check the back strip cattle? Answer: Usually north lane if south was churned up or chained.

Shannon put that one in the copy stack too.

By the time she looked up again, Mrs. Lott was standing on the other side of the counter holding out a pair of thin cotton gloves.

“Put those on if you’re gonna keep handling the photos,” she said. “You’re making me nervous.”

Shannon took them. “You were born nervous.”

“And yet I still outlived three clerks and both my husbands. Put the gloves on.”

Not Colt’s name. His father’s. But the measurement notes were clear enough even before she unfolded it all the way. North access easement. Existing turnoff. Outbuilding. The shed sat closer to the lane than the south fence.

She made a second copy stack.

She adjusted. Ran them again. Fronts and backs. She wrote the exhibit numbers lightly in pencil on the copies so Emily wouldn’t have to cross-check each one with the file later.

When she was done, she put the originals back exactly where they’d come from, closed the envelopes, replaced the plats.

Mrs. Lott took the box from her. “You found what you wanted?”

“Part of it.”

“That usually means trouble.”

Shannon stacked the copies neatly. “It usually means paperwork.”

Mrs. Lott’s mouth twitched. “That too.”

At Road and Bridge, the office smelled like diesel and paper cups. A ceiling fan turned slow above a desk covered in orange work orders and a busted radio with its back off. Shannon gave them to her. Mrs. Lott. Road and Bridge. Colt Hayes field copy, office note only. Emily wrote as she talked.

When she got to Colt’s name, Emily’s pen paused a fraction. Not enough that most people would have caught it. Enough.

“You have his card too?” Emily asked without looking up.

“Mm-hm.”

“Leave it.”

Shannon slid it across. Emily put it under the legal pad without comment.

For a minute the only sound in the office was pen on paper and the weak rattle of the vent.

Then Emily set the pen down and looked at her sister across the desk. “This is actual help.”

Shannon leaned one shoulder on the bookcase. “You sound surprised.”

“I am surprised. Usually when you get involved in something, it’s because a shelf needs redoing or somebody at church has lost their mind.”

“I have range.”

Emily looked back at the photo of the broken latch. “What you have is a way of finding the one thing in a room everybody walked past.”

Shannon didn’t answer that. She watched Emily slide the photograph to the top of the pile, then the work order underneath it, the Blevins deposition on top of both.

Emily gathered the papers square. “Status conference is Friday. I’m going to make them say locked out loud.”

“And if they do.”

Emily’s eyes came back to her. “Then I start asking how they know.”

Shannon nodded.

Emily took up the pen again. “Go on and get out of here before I put you to work labeling tabs.”

“You say that like it’s a threat.”

“It is a threat. Don’t test me.”

Shannon turned toward the door.

“Shannon.”

She paused.

Emily was already looking back down at the papers. “Don’t confuse being useful with being entitled.”

Shannon left anyway.

Chapter 5

The Car, The Yard

By the time she closed at Mize’s, the light had gone flat and yellow and the air held that early-evening damp that said rain was somewhere thinking about it.

Wade was leaning against the hood of her car.

He had one ankle crossed over the other and his arms folded, like he’d been there long enough to get patient about it. He straightened when she came out carrying the day’s deposit bag and the lockbox ledger.

She stopped two steps off the porch.

“What are you doing here?”

“Waiting.”

“That much I got.”

He pushed off the hood. “You got a minute?”

“No.”

He glanced at the locked glass door behind her, then at the ledger in her hand. “Make one.”

Shannon crossed to the car anyway and unlocked the driver’s side.

Wade caught the door before she could open it all the way.

She looked at his hand, then up at him. “Move.”

“You went to Sand Creek.”

The air changed in her chest, though her face didn’t. “You need to move.”

He didn’t. “Didn’t you?”

“Who told you that?”

He took his hand off the door, but only to step back enough to keep her from climbing in. “That matter?”

“Yes.”

“County truck came around the curve and saw your car half off the shoulder. Said Eli Turner was there with you.”

She kept hold of the door handle. The metal was still warm from the day. “Then you already have your answer.”

“What were you doing out there alone?”

“That is not your business.”

“It gets to be my business when you drive yourself out to a place nobody would hear you yell and don’t tell a soul where you went.”

She gave a short, humorless breath. “You make yourself sheriff when it suits you.”

“I’m not playing anything.” He stepped closer. “I’m asking what the hell you were thinking.”

She shut the door hard enough to make him blink. “I was thinking I can go look at a road if I want to.”

“With him there.”

“He happened to be there.”

“You expect me to believe that.”

“I don’t much care what you believe.”

Wade’s jaw worked once. He looked past her toward the darkening lot, then back. “Did he touch you?”

She stared at him.

“Did he?”

Shannon set the deposit bag on the roof.

“He helped me back off the shoulder,” she said. “That is all.”

Wade’s face changed.

“He caught your arm and let go,” he said.

She went still.

His mouth hardened. “That doesn’t mean anything.”

“Get out of my car’s way.”

“You went out there alone. You let him put hands on you.”

Her laugh this time had no humor in it at all. “I let him keep me from falling in the ditch. Reckless conduct. Lock me up.”

“This isn’t funny.”

“No. It’s not.” She stepped closer until she was looking straight at him. “What exactly do you think gives you the right to ask me that?”

“You want a list.”

“I want you to hear yourself.”

His eyes held on hers. “Fine. I’m hearing myself. I don’t like it. I’m asking anyway.”

“You don’t get to.”

“Like hell I don’t.”

She folded her arms. “That’s the problem with you, Wade. You think not yelling makes it not a command.”

He breathed in through his nose. Out again. “I am trying to keep you from doing something stupid.”

“I’m not sixteen.”

“No,” he said. “You’re old enough that I expected better.”

Shannon looked at him for a long second. “Move.”

He didn’t. “You don’t go out there again.”

She almost smiled. “There it is.”

“Not alone. Not with him.”

She shook her head once. “You don’t get to set terms on where I drive.”

“Then I’ll set terms on what I’ll tolerate watching.”

She stared at him. “That yours now too. Me. Something you tolerate.”

Wade raked a hand once over the back of his neck, then put it down hard at his side. “Don’t do that.”

“Do what.”

“Twist this into something else.”

“This is already something else.”

He looked away for the first time, toward the highway beyond the lot, the line of headlights moving through town. When he looked back, his face was tighter.

“You think because he had enough sense to let go that makes him safe,” he said.

“That what you think this is about.”

“I think you get one decent minute out of a man like that and start writing the rest yourself.”

“He sees a woman taking up for him,” Wade said, voice still low, still awful in its restraint. “You think he can’t tell what that looks like. You think he won’t use it.”

The lot had gone quiet around them. Somewhere down the block a dog barked once and once again. A screen door slammed. The evening kept moving like this wasn’t happening in it.

Shannon picked up the deposit bag from the roof and held it tight enough the plastic corners pressed into her palm.

“If you’re trying to keep from being the one who hurts me,” she said, “you’ve missed the turn.”

Wade shut his eyes for half a second.

“I’m worried about you,” he said.

“That’s not a permit.”

“I know.”

“No,” she said. “You don’t.”

He moved then, one step back from the car, but he stayed in front of her. “Tell me I’m wrong.”

She laughed softly without smiling. “About which part.”

“About him.”

She looked straight at him. “You don’t get an answer to that.”

His face shut down.

“If I hear you went back out there,” he said, “I’m coming to get you myself.”

Her grip tightened on the deposit bag until the seal crackled. “Try it.”

For one second something flashed across his face that looked almost like pain.

Then he stepped away from the car.

She opened the door and climbed in without looking at him again. She didn’t start the engine right off. She set the deposit bag on the seat beside her and put both hands on the wheel.

Wade stood in the lot another second or two. Then he turned and walked to his county SUV without looking back.

When she got home, she carried the deposit bag inside and set it by the refrigerator, right under the dry-erase calendar where Eli’s next court date sat in black marker between dog shots due and Mize inventory.

Three days passed before she drove out to Eli’s place.

The place sat off a narrower road than she expected, not hidden exactly, but the kind of tucked-away farmstead people passed their whole lives without turning toward unless they already had reason. House low and square under live oaks. Paint gone dull from weather. Barn farther back. Two trucks in various stages of usefulness. A dog rose off the shade under the porch when she pulled in, barked once, then stopped when Eli came around the side of the barn wiping his hands on a rag.

He saw her car and stopped walking for half a beat before he started again.

She got out with the rolled copy tube under one arm and the envelope of papers in her hand.

“You got lost,” he said when he reached the car.

“No.”

He glanced once at the tube, then back at her face. “What’d you bring.”

“Paper.”

He looked at her another second. “That what we’re calling it.”

“That what Emily told me to bring.”

Something in his mouth shifted like the hint of a smile. He jerked his chin toward the porch. “Come on.”

The dog sniffed her jeans and lost interest. The porch boards flexed under both their weight. Up close the house looked cleaner than the yard. Window screens patched neat. A pair of work boots by the door, scrubbed of most of the dried mud. A five-gallon bucket with a coil of chain in it and a pair of gloves laid over the rim.

He took the envelope from her but not the tube. Opened the flap and scanned the top pages standing there.

“This from the suit file.”

“Mm-hm.”

His eyes lifted to hers. “You went and got it.”

“Emily needed exhibits.”

He thumbed to the photograph of the broken latch and stood very still.

After a second he said, “That’d do it.”

“I thought you might want to see.”

He looked back down at the photo, then at the road-and-bridge work order. His jaw tightened once at C. Blevins.

“Come inside,” he said.

The kitchen was plain and cooler than outside. Coffee can by the sink, stack of mail held down under a salt shaker, a calendar from the co-op turned to the wrong month. The table had ring marks in the finish and one chair leg repaired with a bracket.

He spread the papers on the table without ceremony. Shannon unrolled the larger copy and flattened the corners with both hands. The map wanted to curl back into itself.

Eli stood across from her, one palm braced on the table, reading fast and in pieces. Photo. Work order. Deposition excerpt. Field note.

He tapped Blevins’ name with one forefinger. “Chet was running the back strip for Culpepper all winter.”

“That what I figured.”

“He’d use north lane if he didn’t feel like messing with the south gate.” A beat passed. “Used to drink coffee in this kitchen before the suit.”

“Then the lane wasn’t closed.”

“It wasn’t closed enough to matter.” He looked at the photograph again. “And that shed damn sure wasn’t lock-tight in twenty-one.”

He looked up at her finally. “Why are you doing this.”

“Because it’s there,” she said.

He gave her a look.

She rested her fingertips on the edge of the map. “Because they keep smoothing things that aren’t smooth.”

He nodded once and looked back down at the papers.

Shannon let the silence sit a minute before she said, “Wade came by.”

“He know you went out there.”

“Apparently.”

Eli said nothing.

“He said I was writing the rest myself,” she said. “Because you had one decent minute in you.”

“He’s not wrong to think I got a reputation worth being careful around.”

“That what this is.” She nodded at the papers between them. “Careful around.”

He took his hand off the table and crossed to the counter. Poured himself coffee gone half-cold from the pot. Didn’t offer any. Came back with the mug in one hand but didn’t drink.

“You want the truth,” he said.

“I asked for it.”

He leaned his hip against the counter, not sitting, not pacing. “I have cut tags before.”

She stayed still.

“Not theirs.” He looked down into the mug. “Mine. Daddy’s. Once on stock that wasn’t mine by paper and had been ours by grass for months. Sold under another number. Hauled cattle after midnight. Moved them across parcels I didn’t have clean title on. Lied on forms when it kept feed in troughs.”

He took a swallow of coffee and set the mug down on the counter without looking at her. “That south gate they keep talking about. I cut that chain twice after they put it in. Once with bolt cutters from Simmons. Once with a grinder.”

“So when folks hear tags and trailer and after dark, they don’t have to reach.”

“Did Emily know.”

“Enough.”

“That all of it.”

“Enough for court. More than most.”

The kitchen window over his shoulder looked out on a patch of yard gone brown at the edges. Wind moved the pecan leaves outside in a slow shiver.

He said, “I’m not telling you so you’ll square it up pretty. I’m telling you because if you keep poking around, you’ll hear it crooked from somebody else.”

He looked away first, toward the sink, the back door. “Daddy got sick. Estate never got finished. Bank wanted numbers neat. Culpepper wanted lines neat. Nothing about any of it was neat. Some things I did because there wasn’t room not to. Some because I was mad enough to enjoy it.” He shrugged one shoulder. “Take whichever part offends you less.”

“It doesn’t have to offend me.”

His eyes came back. “No?”

“No.” She shook her head once. “Doesn’t have to make me dumb either.”

He leaned both hands on the counter behind him. “I’m not innocent, Shannon.”

She looked at the broken-latch photograph, then at the work order with Blevins’ name, then back at him. “I didn’t think you were.”

She said, quieter, “I think there’s a difference between a man being hard to defend and him doing the thing they’re saying he did.”

The dog outside barked once at nothing. A truck passed somewhere out on the road and kept going.

Finally he said, “Difference don’t count for much if nobody cares to make it.”

Shannon looked down at the map again, at the narrow run of the north lane, the shed sketched near it like it was any other piece of a place. “Maybe not.”

He picked the photo up and looked at it again. “You should probably go.”

She lifted her eyes.

“Before this starts looking like something else.”

“Too late for that,” she said.

He held the photograph between his fingers a second longer, then laid it down. “Maybe.”

She rolled the map back into the tube while he gathered the rest into a stack. He handed the papers back except for the photo copies, which he kept by the coffee mug.

At the door he opened it and stood back to let her through.

She paused on the threshold. “You said you cut that chain.”

“Yeah.”

“And you still don’t think this is your crime.”

His gaze met hers.

“No,” he said. “It isn’t.”

She held that a second. Then she nodded and stepped off the porch.

He didn’t call after her. She didn’t look back until she’d reached the car.

He was still standing in the doorway, one shoulder against the frame, the broken-latch photo in his hand.

Chapter 6

Friday Night

Friday night, after Emily called to say the prosecutor had sputtered, objected, and then sputtered some more when she put the broken-latch exhibit in front of him, Shannon drove back out there with the full-size plat in the seat beside her and a manila envelope under it.

She had not told Emily she was going. She had not told anybody anything.

The road to Eli’s place was darker after the rain. Water still held in the ditches from the afternoon shower. Her headlights caught on wet leaves, fence wire, the pale eyes of a deer before it vanished into brush.

He was in the yard when she pulled in this time, standing by the barn with the hood of one truck up. He shut the hood and watched her get out.

“You make a habit of this,” he said.

“Not yet.”

He wiped his hands on a rag and came toward her, looking at the envelope. “Good or bad?”

“Mixed.”

“That sounds right.”

Inside, the kitchen light threw a hard circle over the table. He put on coffee this time without asking if she wanted any.

She laid out the new copies. Emily’s handwritten notes in one margin. The full-size plat. A typed transcript excerpt from the status conference. A page from the supplemental report with one line underlined in yellow: shed appeared secured from outside. Appeared.

Eli read that line and let out one short breath.

“Appeared,” Shannon said.

“Lot of room in that word.”

She spread the plat open. “Colt gave me the field note copy. Door swung toward the lane. He said if they want ‘locked,’ they need to prove when.”

“Colt said that.”

“He said they want the room smaller.”

Eli gave a brief nod. He braced both hands on the table and bent over the map. “This line here—” He tapped the narrow access lane. “Folks keep talking like it dead-ends at the creek turn. It don’t. It widens enough for a trailer right there by the shed if you know how to back.”

Shannon came around to his side of the table so she could see where he pointed. Their shoulders nearly touched over the paper.

“The work order says repeated truck entry,” she said. “Blevins asked for rock at the mouth because of rain.”

“He’d have been running cubes or mineral in from that side.” Eli traced the lane again. “Or checking calves if he didn’t want to fool with south.”

She looked at the underlined appeared secured. “Then why make it sound like only one side could reach it.”

He straightened. “Because they don’t need clean facts. They need a clean story.”

She turned to look at him. “That what this is to you.”

“It’s what it looks like from here.” He took the transcript and scanned the lines Emily had marked. “Witness moved his time again.”

“Not in court. Just in how he talked around it.”

“Same thing.”

He dropped the paper back on the table. For a while they stood there with the documents between them and the coffee pot ticking softly on the back counter.

Shannon said, “Every time one part gets shaky, they smooth another.”

“Keep the picture from moving.”

“Only if nobody looks twice.”

Refrigerator hum. Coffee settling. A truck far off on the road and then gone.

“You been looking twice,” he said.

“Yes.”

“Why.”

She glanced down at the map. “Because it bothers me when somebody makes a thing neat that wasn’t.”

He said nothing.

She looked back up. “Because people around here say own when they mean control and locked when they mean shut and that’s close enough most of the time until it isn’t.”

Still he said nothing.

“And because,” she added, slower now, “I think somebody is counting on the county liking this shape more than they like asking if it’s true.”

His gaze stayed on her.

After a second he said, “You really believe that.”

She looked at the yellow line under appeared secured. “I believe this case is bent.”

He looked down at the table once, then back up. “Yeah.”

He reached for the work order and tapped Blevins’ name. “Chet hated Daddy. Hated me worse after the suit. But he ain’t smart enough to clean up language in a report.”

“You think Culpepper.”

“I think money buys people who know where to sweep.”

“Do you know?”

“No.” He shook his head once. “And I’m not giving you a villain because it’d feel better to have one.”

Eli slid the photo of the broken latch beside the map. “Somebody used what folks already know about me. Or think they do.”

Shannon looked at the hanging chain in the photograph. The bent metal. The door that would not stay square in its frame. “I think so too.”

He went still.

He leaned back from the table. “That’s a bad thing to say out loud if you’re wrong.”

“I know.”

“And if you’re right.”

“I know.”

For the first time all evening his mouth moved into something that might have been the beginning of a smile and died before it got there. “No you don’t.”

“That’s probably true.”

He picked up his coffee mug, took a swallow, set it down. “Emily tell you what the judge said after you left.”

“I didn’t stay in.”

“She said the state can use ‘appeared secured’ till they’ve got somebody with personal knowledge. That buys a little.”

“A little.”

“Not much.”

After a minute he said, “Thank you.”

She looked at him.

Shannon let out a breath she hadn’t noticed herself holding and nodded once.

She gathered the transcript pages, squared them, slid them back into the envelope. He watched her hands do it.

“You should head,” he said finally. “Before folks start counting cars.”

She put the envelope down again instead.

His eyes flicked to it and then back to her face.

The clock over the sink ticked once. Twice.

She stepped around the chair toward him.

He didn’t move, but something in him went alert and very still.

She stopped close enough to see the darker stubble along his jaw from the day, the faint white line of the scar that ran down toward the corner of his mouth. She lifted one hand and touched the front of his shirt just below the collarbone. Work shirt. Clean. Rougher than it looked.

He said her name once. Quiet.

She kept her hand where it was.

“Shannon.”

“Don’t talk me out of it.”

For one beat he did nothing.

Then he set his mug down with a careful little click on the table and put one hand against the side of her neck.

The first kiss wasn’t soft. It wasn’t careless either. It felt held back and not held back anymore in the same instant, like something with weight to it had finally found where to drop. Shannon’s fingers tightened in his shirt. He made a rough sound low in his throat she felt more than heard, and then his other hand came to her waist and pulled her the last half-step in.

The table edge hit the back of her thigh. Papers shifted under his elbow. Somewhere a spoon rattled against the side of the sink.

He broke the kiss long enough to look at her once, and she took hold of his shirt with both hands and brought him back down to her before he could say anything sensible.

He kissed her harder then, with the kind of control that was one breath from not being control at all. His hand moved from her neck into her hair, then down her back, broad and warm even through her shirt. Her own hands were not steady. They moved over his shoulders, the muscle and heat under the fabric, the ridge of the scar when her thumb found it again at his jaw.

The room smelled like coffee and clean cotton and the damp earth he’d brought in on his boots earlier. The fan in the next room chopped the air in slow, uneven turns. Outside, something tapped once against the porch rail in the wind.

He kissed the corner of her mouth, then her jaw, then stopped with his forehead against hers like stopping there cost him.

“This is bad timing,” he said against her skin.

“There’s not a good kind.”

He let out something that might have been a laugh if there had been any humor left in either of them. Then he kissed her again, one hand spanning her waist so fully she felt the pressure of his thumb at her ribs.

Her back found the doorjamb to the hall when he moved them without either of them deciding to move. She drew a breath against his mouth and he stilled just enough to let her have it, then his hand slid beneath the hem of her shirt at her side and the touch of bare skin there made her inhale sharp enough to feel him notice.

His eyes met hers.

She didn’t step away.

He kissed her slower for a moment after that, not gentler exactly—just more deliberate. His palm spread at her waist under the shirt now, heat against her skin. Shannon’s hand moved up into his hair at the back of his head and held there.

The hallway was dimmer than the kitchen. She barely registered when they crossed into it, only the shift in light and the scrape of her boot against old wood. He backed once into the wall because he was looking at her instead of where they were going, muttered something under his breath.

His hand came around the back of her neck again. He kissed the place just under her ear and she closed her eyes hard for a second because that was somehow worse, or better, than the rest of it.

A framed photograph hung crooked on the hall wall. A dog-eared church bulletin pinned near the bedroom door.

He drew back a little, both hands on her now, one at her waist and one high on her back. “You sure.”

Shannon opened her eyes. “Yes.”

His jaw tightened once. He nodded and stepped back only long enough to let her move first.

The bedroom was spare. Iron bed. Thin coverlet kicked half down already as if he never bothered making it all the way in the mornings. Window open a crack. Fan in the corner pushing warm air around.

She reached back and pulled her boots off one at a time.

He came to her then.

The bed creaked when the backs of her knees hit it, and then he was over her, not pinning, not forcing, just there in a way that filled the room and narrowed everything in it. His hands were rougher than she’d expected, not careless, just worked hard enough that the texture of them stayed. She felt it through cloth and then under it, along her side, her back, the line of her arm. She pushed his shirt over his shoulders and the fabric dragged slow over skin warm from the day.

He broke the kiss only when he needed breath. Once to look at her. Once to lay his forehead against hers. Once because she touched the scar on his jaw with two fingertips and he went very still under it.

“Don’t,” he said, but there was no force in it.

She let her hand rest there anyway.

His eyes stayed on hers.

She lowered her hand and kissed him instead.

The fan clicked in the corner. The old mattress gave under shifting weight. Her shirt caught on one wrist and then was gone. His mouth found the hollow at the base of her throat, the curve of her shoulder, came back to hers before the heat of it could turn into something she couldn’t hold.

At one point he said her name again, into her mouth this time.

She answered by pulling him closer.

The window fan pushed the curtains in and out in small tired breaths. Rain started somewhere beyond the trees, not on the house yet, just moving through distance. A dog barked and then another answered.

When the intensity of it finally eased, it did not do so gracefully. It left them there in the half-dark, breathing too hard, the sheet tangled around one leg, his hand still spread against her side as if it had landed there and hadn’t remembered to move.

The room sounded too small for breathing.

For a long minute neither of them said anything.

Then Eli rolled to sit on the edge of the bed, elbows on knees, head lowered. He reached for his jeans off the floor and found the cigarettes in the pocket without seeming surprised to find them there. He looked at the pack a second, then set it on the nightstand unopened.

Shannon pulled the sheet up without thinking and then almost laughed at herself for doing it. She let it fall back to her waist.

The fan kept clicking.

Eli stood and crossed to the window. He pushed it up another inch. Damp air came in carrying the smell of rain and wet dirt.

He didn’t turn around right away. “You want water.”

“Yes,” she said.

He went to the kitchen. She could hear the cabinet open, the faucet run, a glass set down and picked back up. She sat on the edge of the bed and reached for her shirt where it hung half off the chair. Same iron bed. Same crooked stack of paperbacks on the floor by the wall. Same old dresser with two unmatched knobs. There was a shirt draped over the footboard she had not noticed before, and one sock under the chair, and the ordinary mess of a person who lived alone.

Eli came back with a glass and handed it to her.

His knuckles brushed hers and then were gone.

She drank half of it at once. The water was cold enough to make her teeth hurt.

He sat back down on the edge of the bed, not close enough to touch unless either of them moved. In the thin light from the hall she could see the line of his shoulders, the quiet set of his face.

Neither of them reached for a joke. There wasn’t one.

After a while he said, “They’ve been watching this drive on and off since bond.”

She lowered the glass. “You mean deputies.”

“Sometimes.” He rubbed his thumb against the side of his palm. “Sometimes just folks.”

She looked toward the window. Rain had reached the roof now, a light scatter on tin.

“They saw my car then,” she said.

“Maybe.”

He looked at her, then away. “You shouldn’t come here at night for a while.”

She set the glass on the nightstand. “All right.”

He did not try to soften it. “I’m serious.”

“I know.”

The rain came harder for a minute, then eased again. He leaned forward, forearms on thighs, head bent.

After a while he said, “Doesn’t change what I said.”

“The case,” she said.

“Yeah.”

She looked at the shape of him in the dim room. “I know.”

He nodded once.

Another long pause.

Then, without looking at her, he said, “And it doesn’t change what happened either.”

Shannon stood and began dressing. There wasn’t a graceful way to do it, so she didn’t try for one. Shirt. Jeans. Boots in her hand until she reached the hall. Eli remained seated until she had finished buttoning her shirt, then got up and pulled on his own clothes.

In the kitchen the papers still sat spread on the table.

She went to gather them and stopped with her fingers on the full-size plat. The map line of the north lane ran toward the little square marked outbuilding as neatly as ever, unchanged by anything that had happened in the hall and bedroom behind her. It annoyed her, suddenly, how paper kept being itself no matter what people dragged to it.

Eli came up beside her, close enough she felt the heat off him but not touching.

“You can leave the big copy,” he said. “I want to look at it again.”

She nodded and slid it free from the rest.

He put one hand on the table, near but not on top of hers. “Friday’s not much.”

“No.”

“It’s something.”

“Yes.”

He breathed out through his nose. “All right.”

She picked up the rest of the envelope. At the door he stepped aside for her again like he had the first day.

On the porch she sat to pull her boots back on because standing for it felt too unsteady. He leaned against the post and waited, looking out into the rain-dark yard.

When she finished, she stood and looked at him.

He looked back.

So he said, “Drive slow by the curve.”

And she said, “I know where it is.”

That got the smallest tilt at one corner of his mouth. Gone almost before it appeared.

She walked to the car with the envelope under one arm. At the door she looked back once.

He was still on the porch in the yellow spill from the kitchen light, one hand braced on the post, the full-size plat left behind on the table over his shoulder.

Chapter 7

State Files Additional Evidence

The next morning at Mize’s, the county had already got hold of the story before she finished ringing up the first bag of dog food.

It started with the paper folded under Mr. Abernathy’s arm.

He set a box of nails and a gallon of weed killer on the counter and laid the paper beside them with the front page up, not because he thought she needed to read it but because he wanted to be seen carrying it.

County Chronicle. No headline big enough for front-page screaming, but the subhead was enough.

STATE FILES ADDITIONAL EVIDENCE IN TURNER LIVESTOCK CASE

Shannon rang up the nails.

Mr. Abernathy tapped the paper with one finger. “Looks like they found blood in that trailer after all.”

“Mm,” Shannon said.

“Lab’s got it in there now.” He shook his head in the slow, satisfied way some men did when the world arranged itself to match what they had already said. “Always said it’d come out.”

She slid the receipt toward him. “That’ll be forty-three sixteen.”

Lab found blood in Eli’s trailer.

Lab found cut tag pieces in Eli’s trailer.

Deputies found bolt cutters with plastic on them.

A neighbor had video now.

A cousin had seen a trailer under tarp.

Emily.

“What.”

Crime lab supplemental report.

She kept reading.

Shannon didn’t answer.

Shannon looked up fast.

Emily didn’t move. “That’s not me fishing. That’s me noticing you.”

“Nothing happened,” Shannon said.

Emily held the stare one beat longer than was comfortable and then nodded as if filing the answer under not now. “All right.”

A reporter from the Chronicle leaned against the wall with a spiral notebook and a coffee gone cold. Two men from out past Fairview stood near the vending machine talking low. Mrs. Haskins from church sat on the bench nearest the courtroom door with her hands folded over her purse and the expression she wore for funerals and committee meetings.

Wade stood at the far end of the hall.

In the hall, the room spilled outward into noise at once. The Chronicle reporter moved fast toward the prosecutor. Mrs. Haskins shook her head at nobody and everybody. One of the Fairview men said, low but not that low, “That ought to about do him.”

Shannon stood near the bench with her purse strap in her hand too tight.

Emily came out with a file under one arm and stopped beside her only long enough to say, “Don’t leave yet.”

Then she was gone again toward the clerk.

Wade remained where he was at the end of the hall, but his eyes came to Shannon once more and stayed this time.

Shannon looked off first.

Eli came through the courtroom door with the investigator behind him and the prosecutor’s people making a bottleneck near the corner. For a second it looked like he’d have to go the long way around the hall.

Then the crowd shifted and he passed closer.

Not close enough to touch. Not close enough that anyone could call it anything. Just within the distance where she could smell the soap on his skin under the courthouse air and know, with a sharp physical certainty she would have paid money to avoid, that some part of the night before still existed in the world.

He looked at her once as he passed.

Then he kept going.

Behind her, Mrs. Haskins said to someone in a low, sad voice, “I hate it for his people, but if they found it in the trailer, that’s that.”

Emily came back with fresh copies in hand and stopped in front of Shannon. Her face was tired.

“You all right.”

Shannon looked at her sister and almost said yes.

Then she heard again, clear as if it had just been spoken, You are not standing outside this anymore.

In the same breath she could still feel the ghost of Eli’s hand at her neck, the exact pressure of it, and down the hall the county was closing around his name with the clean satisfaction of people who believed they had been proved right.

She tightened her hold on the purse strap until the leather edge pressed into her palm.

“I don’t know,” she said.

Emily studied her face a second longer than Shannon liked, then handed her the extra copy of the lab report without comment.

Down the hall Wade was still standing there.

Beyond him, through the courthouse doors, the square lay washed pale in the afternoon sun after rain. Trucks moved. People got on with things. Somewhere a car horn sounded twice. Ordinary life, broad as ever.

Shannon took the report and held it in the same hand that still remembered the weight of Eli’s shirt under her fingers.

Dale’s Notebook

By ten the county yard smelled like hot diesel and wet clay. Shannon parked beside the salt shed because Dale’s office door was propped open with a cinder block and the loader he favored was idling somewhere out back. She had come for one narrow answer about the north lane after the culvert work.

“You need me or the secretary?” he asked.

“You.”

He nodded toward the office. “Come on, then. There’s coffee if you can stand what Henry made.”

“I had a question about the culvert job.”

Shannon nodded once.

“Turner.”

Dale frowned slightly. “Yes.”

“The Thursday we had that pipe open.”

She lifted her eyes. “Which Thursday.”

“He came through while we had the barrels pulled back and the trackhoe set off the shoulder. Asked if he could back in a minute and see if he’d clear the bend with that gray trailer of his. Said the south side was chained and he needed to know if the north would take him if he had to carry mineral or feed in to the back side.”

He paused. Shannon didn’t help him.

“Reece stood at the post and watched his fender,” Dale said. “That’s all.”

“With a stock trailer.”

“Yes.”

“Not a flatbed.”

“No.”

“The gray gooseneck.”

“Yes.”

Shannon swallowed once. “How far did he go.”

“Far enough to swing down by that shed line. Backed in, pulled up, backed again. He was watching the angle more than anything.”

Her mind went to the field copy in Colt Hayes’s hand. Door swung toward lane. Not locked. Room smaller. Then to the road work order she had used to make a hole in the state’s language. Repeated truck entry on the north lane four days before the theft.

Dale watched her. “I figured that was already in somebody’s folder.”

“It wasn’t.”

He held her eyes on that. “You know that.”

“Yes.”

“All right.”

Outside, the loader throttled down. Metal rang somewhere against metal.

Shannon said, “Did you put his name anywhere.”

“No. County order tracks county equipment and time. Not every fool that wants through a work zone.”

“You remember the time.”

“Morning. Between ten and eleven. Before lunch.”

“Exact.”

He reached for a spiral notebook from under a stack of permit forms and thumbed pages with a thick finger. There were grease prints on the paper. He found the date, squinted at his own writing, then turned the book so she could see.

10:14 — culvert set 10:31 — hold traffic / private trailer through 10:39 — backfill resumed

No names.

“‘Private trailer through’ was him?” she asked.

“Was.”

“You wrote that because.”

Dale said, “Reece was there. Henry too, if he’d admit to seeing anything that didn’t involve his lunch break.”

She nodded, but it was slight and late.

“Did he say why,” she asked.

“I just told you what he said.”

“You believed him?”

Dale tipped one shoulder. “Didn’t matter whether I did. He wasn’t there stealing culvert pipe. He wanted to know if that trailer would clear. We had the lane open. I said don’t tear up my barrels.”

“You remember the truck.”

He gave her a look that said now she was insulting him. “Same truck he always drives. Left front fender bent in. You can spot that thing in a line of ten.”

The hearing room rose up in her mind before it had happened—those grainy photographs, the prosecutor’s neat voice, a deputy saying exactly that. You can spot that thing in a line of ten. She pushed the thought away before it finished building.

She looked at him. “Would you.”

“If Emily asks, yes.”

“Would you if I ask.”

He watched her a moment longer than he needed to. “Yes.”

Shannon stood.

Dale took the hint and stood too. “I’m sorry if you thought I’d already told her.”

“You didn’t have a reason to.”

“No.”

At the door she stopped. “Did he ask you not to mention it.”

Dale wiped his hand on the rag again before answering. “He said if Culpepper heard he’d been in there, it’d start a fresh fight. Which, near as I can tell, was right.”

“Did he ask you not to write it.”

“No. I wrote what I write.”

She nodded.

“Yes.”

Shannon managed, “All right.”

She walked out into the white glare of the yard, past stacked pipe and a pallet of orange barrels, and didn’t stop until she was inside her car with the door shut. The cab held the heat. Her hands stayed on the steering wheel though the key wasn’t in.

Four days before the theft, Eli had taken the same trailer through the same opening to see if it would clear.

He had told her about cut tags and midnight hauls and the south gate chain twice over. He had said some of what he did was necessity and some of it was anger. He had sat at his kitchen table and let her see the worst parts he was willing to name.

And he had left this one out.

Not some old story. Not a habit from years back. The exact lane. The exact trailer. Four days before the stock went missing.

She pulled the copied road work order from the folder on the passenger seat and looked at the line she had been carrying around like a pry bar.

Repeated truck entry on north lane.

The dry heat in the car turned heavy. She rolled the window down and still didn’t start it.

Dale had not leaned on it. Had not embroidered. Had not even seemed to know it mattered until her face gave it away. If it had come from Mrs. Haskins over tomatoes or from a deputy with his chest out, she could have cut it down into rumor and meanness and let it die there.

“I got the hearing date moved up and I need—” Emily’s eyes settled properly on her face. “What happened.”

“Yes.”

“No.”

“No.”

“No.”

Wade rubbed his thumb once over the ridge of his knuckle. “That sounds like him.”

Something in the answer stung. “You say that like it helps.”

Wade’s tone stayed even. “That isn’t me talking you toward one side. That’s me talking plain.”

Wade did not move. “All right.”

“Yes.”

“No.”

“I know you can.”

She stared at him.

“Wade.”

He paused.

Something changed in his face then, not enough to soften it, enough to make it honest. “Sometimes that is the care I’ve got.”

The words sat between them in the kitchen with the smell of fried fish cooling in open paper.

Monday

The prosecutor stood. “Judge, we’ll lay our foundation witness by witness. We’re prepared to proceed.”

The judge nodded once. “Do that.”

What followed had the patient, grinding quality of machinery starting up.

The first witness was the evidence deputy. He swore in, sat, stated his name, service years, training. He was the sort of man who answered every question one inch longer than required because he liked hearing himself arrive at the point.

Each piece got an exhibit number and a plastic evidence bag raised for the judge and then for the defense.

Shannon listened to the details because there was nothing else to do. Date. Time. Seal intact. Locker number. Transfer log. The words were dry enough to seem harmless.

When the deputy stepped down, the prosecutor called the crime lab examiner.

She was a compact woman in a navy suit that had seen too many county courtrooms, with reading glasses on a chain and a voice too clear to waste. She gave her name, degree, years at the state lab, training in tool-mark comparison. Emily objected to her being tendered as an expert at the scope the prosecutor wanted. The judge allowed voir dire. Emily took it.

“Ms. Pierce,” Emily said, standing a little sideways to the witness so the judge could see both faces, “tool-mark analysis is not a date stamp, correct?”

“Correct.”

“You cannot tell this court when a given cut was made.”

“Correct.”

“You also cannot testify that a toolmark is unique in the same way a DNA profile may be.”

“No. Different discipline.”

“Your conclusions rely upon observed correspondence between marks.”

“Yes.”

“And the language used for those conclusions varies among laboratories.”

“It can.”

Emily nodded, turned a page, and sat. The judge accepted the witness as an expert for purposes of the hearing.

The prosecutor approached the monitor. “Ms. Pierce, did you receive items in this case for comparison?”

“I did.”

He lifted one bag. “State’s Fourteen.”

“Cut chain link.”

“State’s Seventeen.”

“Yellow-handled bolt cutters.”

He moved them aside. “Tell the court what you did.”

The examiner described microscopes, test cuts, photographing the jaw surfaces, comparing striations and impressions created where metal met metal. She spoke without flourish.

On the monitor, under the courtroom’s flat light, the cut chain link looked larger than Shannon’s hand. Ragged bright metal at the severed ends. Then the image changed to test cuts under magnification—grooves, raised ridges, tiny lines that meant nothing until the examiner began naming them.

“I observed corresponding class characteristics between the submitted chain link and test cuts made with the submitted bolt cutters,” she said. “I also observed a repeated microscopic irregularity consistent with a defect on the right cutting jaw of State’s Seventeen.”

The prosecutor said, “And your conclusion.”

“In my opinion, State’s Seventeen produced the cut on State’s Fourteen, or another tool bearing the same class characteristics and the same microscopic defect pattern.”

The prosecutor let it hang. “You are not excluding State’s Seventeen.”

“No.”

“Your opinion is stronger than ‘cannot exclude.’”

“Yes.”

Emily was on her feet before the prosecutor sat. “Ms. Pierce, the right-jaw defect you refer to—that is wear.”

“It is a defect pattern, yes.”

“Wear from use.”

“Use can contribute.”

“If a pair of cutters has been used repeatedly on similar chain over time, that wear develops in contact with metal.”

“Yes.”

“You were not asked to compare this cut to every chain the tool may have cut in the past.”

“No.”

“You were not asked to compare it to a prior cut from the Culpepper south gate.”

“There was no prior cut submitted.”

Emily picked up a photograph from counsel table and handed it to the witness. “You understand there is evidence in this case that my client previously cut chain on that property.”

The prosecutor started to object. Emily said, “Goes to foundation for the strength of the opinion, Your Honor.”

The judge allowed it.

The examiner glanced at the photograph, then back at Emily. “If the same tool cut a similar chain at an earlier date, I would not be able to date one cut from the other by toolmark alone.”

“Thank you.”

“And,” Emily continued, “you cannot tell this court whether the cut you examined occurred during the charged theft or before.”

“I cannot date the cut.”

When she stepped down, Shannon realized her coffee had gone cold in the paper cup between her palms.

Next came the station manager, a thin man with a polo shirt tucked too tight over his stomach and a habit of clearing his throat before each answer. The prosecutor made him say that this one had been checked the week prior. Emily made him admit glare and nighttime quality. The judge took notes and gave nothing away.

Then the prosecutor called Deputy Harlan Lusk.

The name meant little to Shannon until he stood and took the oath. Then she placed him: patrol deputy, south end of county, the one who always leaned at the post-office rail too long talking hunting leases. He had a scrubbed face and the kind of earnestness juries liked because it looked like plain work.

The prosecutor began with territory, years, roads patrolled, people known. Emily objected once on relevance. Overruled.

“Deputy Lusk,” the prosecutor said, “are you familiar with vehicles commonly operated by the defendant, Eli Turner?”

“Yes, sir.”

“How?”

“I patrol that district. I’ve observed Mr. Turner in the same truck over a period of years. I’ve been on roadside calls involving that truck. I’ve seen it parked at his residence and in town. I’m also familiar with the attached stock trailer he commonly pulls.”

“Older gray pickup, heavy-duty, with a dent to the left front fender. Missing a section of reflective tape on the trailer’s driver-side rear quarter. One welded repair to a middle slat.”

The prosecutor clicked the remote.

The first still came up on the monitor, washed in the greenish white of security footage. Date and time in the corner. 10:52 p.m.

A truck towing a trailer angled through the frame under the gas-station canopy edge, half-lit, half-dark. Grain broke the lines. Headlights smeared.

The prosecutor said, “Do you recognize that vehicle?”

“I do.”

“Whose is it?”

“In my opinion, that is Mr. Turner’s truck and stock trailer.”

Emily was standing. “Objection. Improper lay opinion. Image quality insufficient. Invades the province of the factfinder.”

The judge leaned forward and studied the screen himself. For a few seconds the whole room did the same.

“Overruled for purposes of this hearing,” he said. “Weight to be determined later. Proceed.”

The prosecutor said, “Tell the court what you see that leads you to that opinion.”

Deputy Lusk stepped down from the box so he could stand by the monitor. He used a capped pen to indicate the blurry shape of the front quarter panel.

“Dent here, left front. Marker light here appears dim or broken, consistent with prior observation. Trailer profile consistent—length, slat spacing. Missing reflective strip in this area. On the later still you can see the welded slat more clearly.”

He clicked to the second image. 1:28 a.m.

This one was worse and somehow more convincing. The truck had shifted enough in frame that the trailer side caught more light. Shannon could see the dull absence where reflective tape should have run unbroken. A dark interruption in the slat where metal had been repaired.

It looked like Eli’s trailer.

The prosecutor let the deputy point out each feature again. Dent. Tape. Weld. Familiar equipment. Local knowledge. The county was full of the same makes and models, he admitted under Emily’s cross, but not many with that exact combination.

Emily did what she could. She made him say the images were grainy. She made him agree he could not read a license plate. She made him admit the dent had not been measured or compared by overlay, only recognized. She asked how many gray pickups with goosenecks moved through that station on any given sale night. More than one. How many with repaired slats. He didn’t know. How much of his identification depended on him already believing the truck belonged to Eli. He said none, then had to walk that back into “my familiarity informs my opinion.”

The prosecutor on redirect brought him right back to the plainest line. “Deputy, as you sit here now, are you comfortable saying those stills depict the defendant’s truck and trailer?”

“Yes, sir.”

The judge admitted the stills for pretrial purposes subject to objections at trial. He denied the motion to limit the deputy from offering identification testimony, saying the foundation was sufficient and the jury could sort the rest.

“Your Honor, between the toolmark result linking the defendant’s cutters to the cut chain, the trace bovine blood and severed tag stems in the trailer, and the surveillance images placing that truck and trailer on the road during the relevant period, the state’s case is not speculative.”

Emily stood. “And still no individual animal DNA. Still no eyewitness to any taking. Still a witness whose identification depends on local familiarity and an expert who cannot date the cut she examined. The state is stringing strong-sounding fragments into a straight line the physical evidence does not yet support.”

The prosecutor looked almost pleased to answer. “That is exactly what juries are for.”

The judge adjusted his glasses and ruled from the bench in a voice so dry it made everything sound already settled. Tool-mark opinion in, subject to full cross at trial. Surveillance stills in. Deputy identification allowed. Weight for the jury. Lab report previously admitted subject to foundation, still standing.

Shannon sat through the end with her hands folded in her lap because if she picked up the coffee cup again she might crush it.

Eli had not looked back once.

When the judge rose, everyone else did. Emily leaned to Eli and spoke low. He nodded without much expression. The prosecutor snapped his binder shut. Benches scraped. People turned back into county people immediately, eager to have faces on whatever had just happened.

Wade was off the wall before she reached him. He didn’t touch her. “How bad?”

Emily came out behind her with both legal pads in one arm and the look she wore when she had already started working on the next thing because stopping would mean feeling the last one. “It hurt,” she said. “That’s the honest version.”

Emily nodded. “And the stills.”

Behind her, the courtroom door opened again. The prosecutor came out laughing softly at something the deputy said. A Chronicle reporter shifted closer without pretending otherwise.

She moved off toward the stairwell with Eli beside her, speaking low. Shannon watched them go. Eli still did not look her way.

Wade said, “Come on.”

She let him guide her down the corridor with a hand hovering at the center of her back, not quite touching, only there. In the stairwell the air was hotter and smelled of dust. They passed a woman from church who glanced from Wade’s face to Shannon’s and then to the rail.

Outside, the square had gone bright enough to hurt.

“I need a minute,” Shannon said.

Wade studied her once, then pointed toward the narrow strip of shade beside the veterans’ monument. “There.”

She went because arguing about the direction of the shade would have been stupid. Wade stayed half a step off, broad enough that nobody drifting across the square came too close. He stood that way while she leaned against the warm stone and stared at the courthouse steps.

She could see it with a clarity that made her sick: Eli in that same button-down at the defense table months from now, only smaller somehow because verdicts shrank people before sentencing ever did it. Men from the county talking about him in the past tense of freedom. His truck sitting too long under dust. The house on his place dark at supper. Feed bins going empty if nobody kept up. Somebody else cutting across his pasture because a line weakens fast when the owner isn’t there to stare at it.

And underneath even that, private and uglier, the memory of his kitchen and bedroom still living in her body like it had happened yesterday instead of weeks ago. The turn of his shoulders under her hands. The way he had paused once and then not again. Water in a jelly jar after. His voice saying it didn’t change what he’d told her about the case and didn’t change what had happened either.

Wade stood beside her without speaking. A truck backfired across the square. Somebody laughed too loud outside the clerk’s office.

Wade turned his head.

“In the stills,” she said. “The trailer.”

He didn’t answer right away. “All right.”

“That doesn’t make it him that night. It just—”

“I know what it means.”

She looked at him. “Do you?”

His jaw set. “It means it got easier for twelve people who don’t know a damn thing about cattle or roads or how people live out there to say they do.”

She closed her eyes once. Opened them.

Wade said, lower now, “You don’t have to decide everything today.”

“That’s not how court works.”

“No,” he said. “That’s exactly how court doesn’t work. It stacks. It pushes. It makes a person feel like if they don’t settle it in their own head by noon, they’re failing somewhere.”

Eli’s shirt darkened at the spine with sweat. He took the folded hearing notice from Emily, tucked it into the back pocket of his jeans, and said something that made her stop and look at him hard. Then she nodded once.

He turned toward the parking lot. His eyes lifted as if by habit more than intent and found Shannon under the monument shade.

For a second they held.

Then he looked away and kept walking.

Wade shifted closer. “We’re leaving.”

She pushed off the stone and went with him to the truck.

The house was hotter than outside. Shannon set the courthouse papers on the kitchen table and stood over them without taking off her shoes.

Tool-mark opinion in. Stills in. Deputy identification in.

She heard Emily’s voice again. It hurt. That’s the honest version.

The dog nosed at her calf and she bent automatically to scratch behind his ears. After a minute she fed them because that at least had an order to it. Feed bin. Scoop. Water pan. Gate latched.

The paper flattened slow. The north lane, the shed, the south gate, parcel lines, old fence trace, drainage cuts. She laid Dale’s work order beside it. Then Colt’s copied field note page. Then the hearing notice with Emily’s scribbles in the margins.

For a while she only looked.

The state’s line was clean now. Too clean. Truck and trailer on the road at 10:52 p.m. and 1:28 a.m. Cut chain tied to Eli’s bolt cutters. Blood and tag stems in the trailer. That was enough for most people, and after today it was enough for a courtroom to let in.

Her eyes went back to the still photographs Emily had finally gotten printed. In the second image, under the murk and glare, the trailer shape showed clearer than it had on the monitor. Long gooseneck neck. Full side slats. Forward side gate. Not a short utility stock rig. Not something a man turned on a dime.

The same trailer Dale said Eli had backed through the north lane while the county had the culvert open.

While the county had it open.

She sat very still.

While we had the barrels pulled back. After we stacked pipe and rock back on the shoulder, I wouldn’t have liked it.

Shannon pulled the road order closer and read the date again. Then Dale’s little notebook line she had copied from memory onto the margin. 10:31 — private trailer through.

She looked at the plat. At the bend into the north lane. At the west shoulder. At the narrow throat where the county had set culvert pipe and riprap off the road edge once the job was done.

A trailer that size could make it when the county crew had the work zone opened up. Dale had said so because he had watched it happen.

After, he wouldn’t have liked it.

Wouldn’t have liked it was not couldn’t.

Shannon reached for Colt’s field copy. On the margin below the note about the shed door she had paid so much attention to, there was other writing she had barely bothered with at the time because it seemed ordinary:

old packed track at lane west shoulder held no fresh break at edge

She stared at the words.

West shoulder held.

She remembered Colt at the diner, one hand over the page, saying nobody says locked unless they want the room smaller. She had been listening for language then, not dirt.

Now she read the notes the way Colt would have written them: quick, for himself, things worth remembering later because they were ordinary enough to disappear by the next rain. Shoulder held. No fresh break. No fresh break at edge.

If a full-size trailer had gone in and out loaded through that throat after the culvert job was closed up, in damp ground, across that shoulder—would it have held.

She sat back.

No single thought answered it. What came instead was a press of small remembered things. The morning after the theft had been wet under top crust. She knew because the dogs had tracked clay into the mudroom and she had cursed them while toweling paws. Dale’s work order had shown heavy equipment over that lane four days before, which meant the ground at the edge would already have been worked up and softer than it looked. Colt’s note said the shoulder held. No fresh break. The state’s stills, which had just gotten stronger, showed the large trailer, not some short borrowed rig that might pivot tighter. And Dale’s offhand remark that now sounded less offhand than exact: during, he got one in there.

During.

Shannon stood so suddenly the chair legs barked against the floor.

She took the papers and went back to the car.

The drive to the north lane took less than twenty minutes if you didn’t get behind a pulpwood truck. She didn’t. The afternoon had gone still in that dead way before evening where every mailbox post looked sharper than it ought to. At the turnoff she slowed and looked automatically for anyone coming. Nobody.

The lane itself sat quiet, grass grown back along the edges since the county work, but the shape of it hadn’t changed. The road widened, then narrowed where the culvert work had been. Pipe no longer stacked there now, just the memory of where it had sat in the scuffed pale gravel and a scatter of riprap sunk half into the shoulder.

Shannon pulled off and got out.

Heat came up through the soles of her shoes. Cicadas rasped in the trees. The shed line lay back from the lane just as Colt’s field copy had fixed it in her mind. Door toward lane. Bend too tight unless you knew how to take it.

She walked the shoulder slowly.

At the throat of the lane she turned and faced back toward the road the way a driver would have. A trailer that size would have to come in wide. Hug the ditch. Let the truck nose past before cutting the wheel. If there had been pipe stacked on the west shoulder and barrels left tight after the job, room would have been mean.

Maybe still passable empty by a patient man.

Loaded was different.

Loaded, the trailer bit where it wanted. Loaded, rear wheels tracked shorter. Loaded, the shoulder took the strain if there was not enough road.

She could feel that truth in her hands from a dozen ordinary hauls in ordinary years. Hay. Feed. Panels. Not even cattle. Weight was weight when it came to where wheels laid down and where they slipped.

Shannon stood there a long time, looking at the edge where Colt had written shoulder held. The dirt now was dry and hard, unhelpful as bone. She crouched and touched it anyway, then straightened and looked toward the road.

The state’s story had Eli’s trailer in one clean motion: on the road, to the property, back on the road. The stills had helped that story. The toolmark testimony had helped it more. Shannon could feel how a jury would take it because she had almost taken it herself.

But the land would not stop being the land because a courtroom wanted straight lines.

She took her phone from her pocket and called Dale.

He answered on the fourth ring. “Road and Bridge.”

“It’s Shannon.”

“You need something else.”

“At Culpepper’s north lane after y’all finished that culvert job, what exactly did you leave on the west shoulder?”

A pause. “Pipe till Monday. Some riprap. Two barrels.”

“How close?”

“To the edge?”

“Yes.”

“Close enough I didn’t want school buses crowding it.”

“Could a full-size loaded stock trailer come through there without dropping the shoulder?”

Another pause. Longer this time.

“Why,” Dale said.

“Just answer me.”

He let out a breath through his nose. “I wouldn’t’ve wanted to try it.”

“That’s not what I asked.”

“No,” he said. “It ain’t.”

She waited.

Finally Dale said, “Empty, maybe, if a man knew what he was doing and didn’t mind kissing the ditch. Loaded, I’d expect him to take a bite out of that edge unless he had somebody spotting and a little luck.”

Shannon looked at the shoulder. “If he did.”

“We’d have seen it. Reece would’ve complained all Monday about fixing it.”

“You sure?”

“As I am of anything from that week. Why?”

Shannon stared toward the bend where the trailer would have had to swing. “Colt’s field note says the west shoulder held. No fresh break.”

Dale went quiet on the other end.

Then he said, “That so.”

“Yes.”

He did not speak for several seconds. When he did, his voice was flatter. Thinking. “Well.”

“That’s all you’ve got?”

“For now, yes.”

She almost asked him if he had any work photos. Almost asked whether Reece remembered the barrels’ exact position. Instead she said, “All right.”

“Shannon.”

She waited.

“I told you what I knew about Eli because you asked and it was true. Don’t go making that into more certainty than it is.”

“I’m not.”

“You sound like you are.”

She looked down at her own shadow on the dirt. “I’m standing in the lane.”

Another pause. “That’ll do it.”

He hung up first.

Shannon put the phone away and walked farther down toward the shed. The door still swung the way Colt had marked. Toward the lane.

She stood with the papers under one arm and looked from the bend to the shoulder to the shed door and back again.

This did not clear Eli.

But something in the state’s neat line had begun to bother loose.

Not enough to call it wrong. Not enough to call anybody a liar.

Just enough that she could not make the picture settle flat anymore.

Maybe the truck in the stills was his and the state had the route wrong. Maybe he had used another access. Maybe there had been another driver or another set of hands spotting him through. Maybe the lane had taken less damage than she thought it should and Colt had missed it. Maybe she was doing exactly what Emily had warned her not to do—seeing what she wanted where facts ought to stand on their own.

She looked at the west shoulder again.

West shoulder held.

No fresh break at edge.

The cicadas rose and fell. Far off, a dog barked twice. The light had started changing, taking the hard white out of everything and leaving the land truer and meaner both.

Shannon took out her phone and found Emily’s name. Her thumb hovered over it.

She slid the phone back into her pocket.

Chapter 10

The Shoulder

Shannon waited until the courthouse annex had mostly emptied before she went up the narrow side stairs to Emily’s office. She knew the secretary left at five and Emily stayed when she was irritated enough to need the quiet. The hall smelled like old paper and copier heat. Somebody downstairs rolled a trash can over the tile and let a door bang.

Emily’s office door stood open six inches. Shannon knocked on the frame anyway.

Emily looked up from behind a desk buried in folders, legal pads, and three coffee cups in different stages of dead. She had her reading glasses low on her nose and one shoe off, the other half-on.

“If you’re here to tell me not to worry,” Emily said, “save it.”

“I’m not.”

Emily leaned back and studied her face for half a second longer than polite required. “Then come in and shut the door.”

Shannon did. She stayed standing.

Emily capped her pen. “You look like you’ve either found religion or trouble. I don’t need either one unless it’s documented.”

“It’s not documented the way you want.”

Emily let out a breath through her nose. “Then this had better be good.”

“There’s a piece of it won’t sit flat.”

“Shannon.”

“I know how that sounds.”

“It sounds like a feeling.” Emily took off her glasses and set them down with care. “I am about one inch from trial prep on a case that already got worse in front of my face. I do not have time for a feeling.”

“It ain’t that.”

“Then speak in nouns.”

Shannon stepped closer to the desk. “The north lane.”

Emily said nothing.

“The shoulder.”

Still nothing.

Shannon put both hands on the back of the chair opposite Emily’s desk and leaned just enough to hold herself there. “After the hearing I went out there. I stood in that lane and looked at where the county filled in after the culvert job. Dale came by later and I asked him plain. If a full-size loaded stock trailer backed through there after they’d closed that job up, would it take the west edge?”

Emily’s eyes sharpened. “And?”

“He said loaded, he’d expect it to bite the west shoulder. Said bad enough they’d have noticed and complained first thing Monday.”

Emily reached for a legal pad. “Slow down.”

Shannon waited while Emily dragged the pad in front of her and clicked a fresh pen.

“When exactly did you ask Dale?”

“Day of the hearing, after.”

“Where.”

“At the lane. Then again at Road and Bridge yard next morning.”

Emily wrote something. “What exactly did he say the first time.”

“That loaded he’d expect it to take a bite out of the west shoulder edge. Said empty was one thing. Loaded was another.”

Emily drew a square, then two lines branching off it. “This is the culvert?”

“Yes.”

She drew another line. “Fence?”

“Yes.”

Emily looked up. “How loaded.”

“Loaded enough to matter. Two or three calves. More.”

“That is not a measurement.”

“No.”

Emily’s mouth tightened. “Did you measure the lane?”

“No.”

“So we don’t know width.”

“We know the shape.”

Emily slapped the pen once against the pad, not hard. “Shannon, I need to know whether you are bringing me something that has bones or something you are making from air because you need there to be a door.”

“I didn’t come because I need a door,” she said. “I came because the dirt and the story they told about it are not saying the same thing.”

Emily looked at her a long moment.

Then she said, “Keep going.”

“Colt’s field copy.”

That made Emily’s hand still.

“What about it.”

“In the margin where he was noting the lane.” Shannon heard her own voice flatten. “He wrote, ‘old packed track at lane / west shoulder held / no fresh break at edge.’”

Emily’s gaze lifted sharp off the pad. “Where did you see that?”

“In the copy he dropped by the county.”

“When.”

“Last week.”

“And you are just now bringing me this.”

“Yes.”

“Why.”

“Because I needed to know I wasn’t making it from wanting.”

Emily stood up so fast the chair rolled back into the file cabinet. “You had a survey note in your hand with language that could undercut route proof and you sat on it because you needed to feel pure about your motives?”

Shannon’s jaw locked. “Don’t do that.”

“Don’t tell me what not to do in my own office when you have been carrying around half a bridge under your shirt.”

“I didn’t know what it was.”

“You knew enough to come here.”

“I know enough now.”

Emily planted both hands on the desk and leaned across it. “Do you think he didn’t do it?”

Shannon met her eyes. “I think if they’re saying he backed a loaded trailer through that lane that night, the shoulder ought to show it.”

Emily stared at her. The office was suddenly quiet except for the fluorescent hum and a phone ringing somewhere down the hall.

“Did Eli send you here?” Emily asked.

“No.”

“Did he tell you what to say.”

“No.”

“Did he tell you anything after Mize’s besides yes.”

Shannon swallowed once. “No.”

Emily straightened, but the anger did not leave her face. “Good. Because I am not building anything off a man who has discovered silence as a legal strategy.”

Shannon almost smiled despite herself and didn’t.

Emily looked back at the pad. “Could have been empty.”

“Yes.”

“Could have gone out another way.”

“Yes.”

“Could have been loaded lighter than Dale means.”

“Maybe.”

Emily circled something. “Could have clipped less if the driver knew his trailer.”

Shannon thought of the lane, the drop, the soft edge where fresh fill met old shoulder. “Maybe. But not enough to leave it whole.”

Emily looked up again. “That’s not engineering testimony.”

“No.”

“Did Dale say he’d swear to it.”

“He said he’d tell it straight.”

She picked up her phone. “Sit down.”

Shannon sat.

Emily hit speaker and dialed from memory. It rang four times before Dale answered, breathless like he had come in from outside.

“Road and Bridge.”

“Dale, it’s Emily Rusk.”

Pause. “Evening.”

“I need to ask you something, and I need you to tell it to me exactly like you’d tell it on a stand.”

Another pause, longer. “All right.”

Emily looked at Shannon once and then away. “At the north lane on the Culpepper property, after your crew closed the culvert work, if a full-size stock trailer came through there loaded, backing west and cutting through, what would you expect to see at the west shoulder edge.”

Dale did not answer quickly. Shannon could hear a fan turning somewhere on his end of the line.

“I’d expect some fresh crumble or break on that edge,” he said finally. “Depends on weather and driver and load, but loaded, yes. Enough to notice.”

“Would your crew have noticed Monday morning.”

“If it was fresh, yes ma’am. We’d’ve fussed over it. We don’t like our patch work tore up first weekend.”

Emily wrote as he talked. “Do you have any notes from that work.”

“I got my pocket book. Dates and crew.”

“Do those notes reflect any complaint or repair at that west edge after the theft date.”

“No ma’am.”

Emily glanced at Shannon. “Are you certain.”

“I can look right now.” There was the sound of paper and a drawer. “No. Nothing on that edge till rain wash two weeks later.”

Emily said, “Dale, if I asked you to sign an affidavit tomorrow morning, would you.”

“If it says what I said and not more.”

“That is exactly what it’ll say.”

“All right then.”

Emily thanked him and hung up.

For a second she only stood there with the pen in her hand. Then she went to the side cabinet, took out a county road map, and spread it over a clean strip of desk.

“Tell me where in Colt’s note you saw it.”

Shannon got up and came around the desk. She pointed at the lane on the map though the words had not been written there.

“In the margin next to his sketched line.”

“Did anyone else see you reading it.”

“I don’t know.”

“Did Colt.”

“No.”

“Good.” She marked a time on her pad. “Tomorrow at seven, we go to the lane with Dale. I’ll get measurements and photographs. After that I file to compel supplemental disclosure and to reopen detention. Maybe more.”

Shannon looked at the pad where Emily had written in hard block letters: SHOULDER / ROUTE / DISCLOSURE.

“You think it matters.”

Emily’s mouth went flat. “I think if the state’s physical theory depends on a loaded trailer through that lane, and the ground says no, then it matters whether they know that and whether they told me.” She capped the pen again. “That is not the same thing as innocence.”

“I know.”

Emily gave her a look that was part fatigue, part something meaner and kinder both. “Do you.”

Shannon did not answer.

Emily crossed her arms. “You bring me something like this again, you bring it the same day.”

“I know.”

“No.” Emily shook her head. “I am not scolding you for being late. I’m telling you you don’t get to protect yourself from being involved by slowing down the truth.”

Shannon looked at the map instead of Emily.

After a moment Emily’s voice lowered, not soft but less sharp. “You were right to bring it.”

Shannon let out a breath she hadn’t realized she was holding. “It still may not mean what we want.”

Emily gave a short humorless smile. “I’m a defense lawyer in this county. Nothing means what we want. It means what survives being hit.”

She picked up her phone again. “Go home. Eat something. And don’t tell anybody what you brought me.”

“Not even Wade?”

Emily’s eyes lifted. “Especially not Wade.”

Shannon nodded.

When she reached the door, Emily said, “Shannon.”

She turned.

“If this opens, it opens because the dirt says so. Not because either one of us needs it to.”

“I know.”

The next morning the light came up thin and gray over the lane, with dew still holding at the grass tips and the red dirt dark where shade hadn’t burned off. Shannon got there first and stood by her car with her coffee cooling in her hand. The lane looked meaner early. Narrower. The culvert shoulders the county had packed and feathered out were harder in the morning light, every edge visible.

Dale came in his county truck with mud on the doors and a tape measure in the passenger seat. Emily arrived three minutes later in jeans, boots, and a linen shirt that already looked wrung from the day. She had a camera, a yellow folder, and the clipped expression of somebody who had slept four hours and turned all of them into work.

Nobody wasted time.

Dale walked the west edge first, boots careful where the fill dropped toward the ditch. He planted one heel, squinted down the lane, and spat into the weeds.

“See there,” he said.

Emily crouched by him. “Show me.”

He traced with one finger the line where packed shoulder met the drop. “If your truck’s here and your trailer’s swinging through loaded, rear tire wants this edge. Not all the time, not every fool pass. But loaded and tight like this? That edge is your problem.”

Emily stood and sighted it herself. “How much room from fence post to the shoulder break.”

Dale hooked the tape to the post and walked it out. “Thirteen foot ten to where I’d stop calling it shoulder and start calling it trouble.”

Emily wrote it down.

Shannon moved only when asked. She held the end of the tape. She took the photographs Emily told her to take: straight on from the lane mouth, angled from the west ditch, standing where the driver’s front tire would have to ride if he were backing blind and keeping the trailer off the post. In each frame the lane pinched itself into a fact.

Dale said, “Empty you might baby through clean enough. Loaded, I’d’ve expected to see a fresh shave. Especially with that fresh pack not settled good yet.”

Emily looked up. “You said that yesterday.”

“I know what I said.”

“I need to know how firm you are.”

He looked at her then, really looked. “Firm enough to sign it.”

Emily nodded.

She walked the lane once more by herself, from the road inward and back, then stopped at the culvert and read from a copy she had made from Shannon’s description. “‘Old packed track at lane / west shoulder held / no fresh break at edge.’ That sound like something a survey man would write if he was noting conditions as found.”

Dale shrugged one shoulder. “Sounds like Colt.”

“Does ‘west shoulder held’ mean intact to you.”

“To me it means it held.”

Emily almost smiled. “That’ll do.”

She got Dale’s affidavit signed by nine-fifteen at the bank, the notary stamping it while Shannon stood by the fake ficus and watched traffic on the square. By eleven Emily had filed a motion thick enough to make the clerk sigh. Motion to Compel Supplemental Disclosure. Motion to Reconsider Detention. Motion for Leave to Conduct Site Inspection and Present Additional Physical Evidence. Emily walked the copies herself down the hall to the prosecutor’s office.

The assistant district attorney took them in his doorway with two fingers like they were damp. He was a neat man with a careful tie and the habit of speaking through a smile that did not include the rest of his face.

“We are not relitigating the hearing because your investigator found a muddy shoulder,” he said.

Emily said, “Good thing it wasn’t my investigator.”

He looked past her and saw Shannon on the bench by the window. His smile thinned.

Emily said, “You might want to read page four before you posture. Or page six, where your own route theory starts stepping in a hole.”

He flipped the first page without really reading. “Field notes from a surveyor who is not an accident reconstructionist and a roads man speculating about trailer movement does not become exculpatory because you underline it.”

Emily’s voice stayed level. “What it becomes is material when your case asks a court to believe a loaded trailer went through a lane your own county records say held intact.”

He said, “That is your spin.”

She said, “Then let’s spin it in front of the judge.”

By one o’clock the judge had set a hearing for Friday morning.

The courthouse hall on Friday smelled like floor wax and rain coming in under the door. Shannon sat three benches back while Emily stood at counsel table with the same yellow folder and a stack of photographs clipped square. The prosecutor had another lawyer with him now and a new crease between his brows.

They called it a detention review and supplemental disclosure matter. The bailiff had to go fetch extra chairs.

Bond didn’t hold. It was revoked at the supplemental setting, not for anything that could be said clean in a room, but for how the same facts had started to stack.

Eli came in through the side door in county khaki and the soft plastic sandals the jail used when they didn’t trust laces. He had lost some weight since the hearing. Not enough to be remarked on. Enough that the collar sat looser. He looked once toward the benches and found her. Just a brief holding of the eyes, then away.

She looked back at Emily.

Emily called Dale first.

Dale sat straight and answered carefully, like he was laying boards across a ditch. Yes, his crew closed the culvert on that date. Yes, a loaded stock trailer backing through the north lane after closure would likely have stressed or broken the west shoulder edge. Yes, his notebook reflected no complaint, patch, or fresh repair there Monday morning. Yes, if there had been a fresh bite, somebody would have mentioned it.

The prosecutor tried to widen the room around the answer.

“Mr. Hensley, you are not a physicist.”

“No sir.”

“You are not trained in vehicular dynamics.”

“No sir.”

“You cannot testify what absolutely had to happen.”

Dale took a breath. “I can testify what road edge does when folks put weight where it don’t want it.”

A few people in the back shifted.

Then Emily called Colt Hayes.

He came from the hall with his field book under one arm and stood where the clerk swore him. Colt wore a clean work shirt and did not look left. Emily did not look at him until she had the note copy in her hand.

“You made field observations at the Culpepper north lane on Monday morning following the reported theft,” she said.

“Yes.”

“You made margin notes.”

“Yes.”

She handed him the copy. “Are these yours.”

He looked at it. “Yes.”

“Read the line I’ve highlighted.”

He read it without changing his voice. “‘Old packed track at lane / west shoulder held / no fresh break at edge.’”

“What did you mean by ‘west shoulder held.’”

“That it was intact where I looked.”

“What did you mean by ‘no fresh break at edge.’”

“That I didn’t observe fresh failure on the west edge.”

The prosecutor was on his feet before Emily could take the next step. “Judge, the witness has already testified he is not offering reconstruction opinions.”

Emily did not turn. “I didn’t ask for one.”

The judge said, “Overruled. Sit down.”

Emily faced Colt again. “Were you noting old, packed-in track as distinct from fresh disturbance.”

“Yes.”

“Did you observe any fresh rear-wheel bite or collapse at the west shoulder consistent with a heavy trailer dragging through.”

He paused. “Not that morning.”

The prosecutor stood again. “Consistent according to whom.”

Emily said, “I withdraw ‘consistent with.’”

The judge pinched the bridge of his nose. “Counsel.”

Emily nodded once. “Did you observe any fresh shoulder break.”

“No.”

Deputy Lusk came last. He sat in uniform, jaw set too high.

Emily took him to the route first. He admitted there were no photographs in evidence showing fresh west-shoulder damage. He admitted his original reports did not note the condition of that edge. He admitted the north lane had become “the likely route” partly because of property layout, partly because of prior disputes between Eli and Culpepper, partly because a gray stock trailer had been seen on the road.

“How much of your certainty,” Emily asked, “depended on the fact that you already believed my client was the kind of man who would do it.”

“None,” Lusk said too fast.

Emily slid a transcript page toward him. “You testified under oath two weeks ago that identification of the trailer was based on your experience with this defendant and his vehicle.”

He took longer then. “My familiarity helped identify the trailer, yes.”

“Not just the trailer, Deputy. The route. The theory. The story you told yourself about what fit.”

The prosecutor objected. The judge sustained. Emily nodded and changed direction.

By the time they broke for lunch the prosecutor’s tie was loosened and Emily’s voice had gone hoarse. Shannon stood in the hall by the vending machine while courthouse people moved around them pretending not to look. Emily came out with Colt’s field book copied and stapled under her arm. Colt came out ten seconds later and stopped at the far end of the hall as though some invisible line held him there. Emily did not look up.

The prosecutor asked for a continuance that afternoon. Emily said no before he had finished the sentence. The judge gave him until Monday at nine to advise whether the state intended to proceed on the existing indictment, amend, or dismiss. Eli went back through the side door to county holding. The chain at his waist made a short metal sound Shannon felt in her teeth.

On Monday the Chronicle put out a thin extra line under the weather box on page one:

STOCK THEFT CASE REVIEWED AFTER NEW EVIDENCE RAISED

It said “newly raised evidentiary concerns regarding route and access.” It said “defense counsel presented supplemental witness testimony.” It said “the matter remains under review.”

At Mize’s that morning Mrs. Haskins folded the paper against the counter and said, “That girl lawyer found herself a ditch to hide in.”

Mize, who had been counting out cigarettes, said, “It says the state’s looking at it.”

Mrs. Haskins sniffed. “It says they got hung up on something small. Cattle still don’t walk themselves.”

Shannon was bagging two pounds of horse feed into a brown sack. She tied the string tighter than it needed.

Mrs. Haskins looked at her. “You heard me.”

“I heard you.”

“Well.”

Shannon set the sack on the counter. “Road edge ain’t small if your whole story runs over it.”

Mrs. Haskins gave her a long measuring look, then tucked the paper under her arm. “That boy’s still got trouble on him whether the county can stack it right or not.”

She left without waiting for change until Mize called her back. Mize looked at Shannon and then away.

At the county yard later that afternoon, Dale was standing by a grader with his notebook in his back pocket. Men were changing blades and talking weather. Nobody said Eli’s name. Shannon handed Dale a copy Emily had left for him and he folded it once, exact.

“You all right being in this?” she asked.

He shrugged. “I told it the way it was.”

“That enough for folks?”

He gave the road yard, the trucks, the men pretending not to hear, one look. “Folks don’t ask enough. They ask useful.”

Then he climbed into the grader and started it up.

Chapter 11

Dismissed Without Prejudice

Monday at nine-thirty the courtroom filled slower and quieter than it had Friday. The prosecutor did not bring the second lawyer. Emily had no smile on at all now. She stood with one hand on the table and the other on a file thick with tabs. Shannon sat further back. She did not know if Eli had seen her come in.

The prosecutor rose and said the state moved to dismiss the indictment without prejudice pending further investigation and evidentiary review.

Emily did not sit down. “Your Honor, given the defendant’s current custodial status, I would ask the dismissal enter forthwith and that a release order issue immediately.”

The prosecutor said, “The state has no objection to release on the dismissed matter.”

The judge looked over his glasses. “No objection now.”

He signed three places in quick blue strokes. The clerk stamped them. The sound of the stamp echoed harder than it should have.

No one on the benches breathed out loud. The prosecutor stacked his papers as if he had only rescheduled a meeting. The judge moved to the next case before Eli had been brought back through.

Emily turned then and for the first time that morning looked toward Shannon.

The jail took another forty minutes to let Eli out.

Shannon waited in the corridor outside holding with Emily and two other people whose people were being processed that morning. The walls were painted a yellow that made everyone look unwell. A deputy behind the glass slid forms through the slot, took them back, asked for signatures, disappeared again. Somewhere beyond the locked door metal hit metal in practiced sequence.

Emily signed something, then another thing. “They’ll give him back his clothes if they still have them sorted,” she said. “If not, he walks out in county issue and comes back later for property.”

Shannon said, “That legal.”

Emily gave her a look. “Legal enough to happen.”

When Eli finally came through, he had his own jeans on and the same work shirt from the hearing, wrinkled from a property bag. He carried a clear plastic sack with a belt, wallet, keys, and whatever else the county had decided belonged to him. He looked at the open hall like a man stepping from one kind of weather into another.

Emily moved first. Not to touch him. To hand him papers.

“Dismissed without prejudice,” she said. “They can refile if they think they’ve fixed what broke. I don’t think they have much to fix it with. Don’t leave me without telling me where to find you.”

A corner of his mouth moved once. “All right.”

She pointed at the packet. “Read the release copy. Don’t lose it. If anybody stops you, that paper matters more than your face does.”

“I know.”

Then Emily stepped back.

That was when he saw Shannon.

There were ten feet between them and no reason in the world to close it in a courthouse hall with a deputy behind glass and strangers listening to the vending machine hum. He did not smile. She did not.

He gave one short nod.

She gave one back.

He went down the hall with the plastic bag in one hand and the release papers in the other. The outside door opened on glare. Then it shut.

Emily exhaled hard enough Shannon could hear it. “I need coffee and a week of sleep,” she said. “I will get neither.”

“You knew they’d do it?”

“I knew they were scared to try it on the version they had.” Emily gathered her bag. “That’s not the same as justice.”

“No.”

Emily looked at her then, closely. “Are you going after him today?”

“I don’t know.”

“Maybe let him get home without a witness first.”

Shannon nodded.

Emily pushed her hair back. “The town will call this a loophole by supper.”

“It already started.”

Emily’s laugh was brief and mean. “Of course it did.”

When they came out onto the square, Wade was leaning against his county truck under the live oak by the monument, hat pushed back, arms folded. He had not gone into court. He never did unless he had to. He saw Shannon and straightened.

“How’d it go,” he asked.

“Dismissed for now,” Emily said before Shannon could. “Released.”

Wade took that in with no visible surprise. His eyes went once to the courthouse door Eli had used and back to Shannon. “You all right?”

She could have lied. She said, “I’m standing here.”

“That wasn’t my question.”

“I know.”

He looked like he might say more. Then didn’t. “All right then.”

Emily headed for her car without another word. Wade stayed where he was, one hand on the truck door.

“You need a ride?” he asked.

“No.”

“You sure.”

“Yes.”

He nodded. “Then I’ll be at the county till five.”

He got in the truck and pulled away slow around the monument.

At Mize’s, men who had said “open and shut” the week before now said “they moved too fast,” which was not the same as saying they had been wrong. Mrs. Haskins came back for scratch grain and said, “Well, if the state can’t hold it, I suppose that’s that for now,” with a little extra weight on the last two words.

A man Shannon had known since school, Lester Burk, stood by the ice freezer reading the Chronicle and said to nobody and everybody, “This is what happens when you make a jury trial out of common sense. Technical holes. Boy’s still dirty.”

Mize said, “You know that?”

Lester folded the paper. “I know enough.”

Shannon was restocking canned beans. She kept lining labels forward. “That seems to be popular.”

Lester looked over. “You taking up for him.”

She kept lining labels forward. “I’m taking up for saying what we know and what we don’t.”

He gave a short laugh. “That’s how lawyers talk when they lose cattle.”

Later, when the store was quiet, Mize came around the counter with a broom and said, “You don’t owe the whole square an argument.”

“I know.”

“Do you.”

She took the broom from him. “Maybe not.”

He watched her sweep two strokes. “You think he’s clean.”

She shook her head. “I think the county told it too clean.”

Mize considered that and did not answer. He went back to the register.

Chapter 12

What the Land Keeps

Three days passed before Shannon drove out to Eli’s place.

She did not tell Emily. She did not tell Wade. She did not tell herself until she had already taken the rolled plat from the back seat and laid it on the passenger seat where she could see it.

It was late enough in the afternoon for the light to have gone softer at the edges but not cooler. Dust rose behind her tires and hung low in the ruts.

His gate stood open. That surprised her first.

The second thing was the quiet.

Not empty quiet. Working quiet. Metal on wood once, then nothing, then the slide of something dragged across packed dirt. She pulled in and saw him near the side of the barn, coiling a hose onto a hook.

He looked up when her engine cut off.

For a second neither moved. He had on a faded gray shirt with the sleeves rolled and a cap low over his brow. He looked thinner than he had before county had him.

Shannon sat with both hands on the wheel. Then she reached for the plat, opened the car door, and got out.

She closed the door behind her and walked toward him with the rolled paper in one hand.

He waited where he was. He did not come meet her. She was grateful for that.

When she stopped six feet away, she held out the plat.

“You left this.”

He took it. “I know.”

He did not unroll it. He leaned it against the barn wall.

She said, “I should’ve brought it sooner.”

“It’s paper.”

“That’s not what I mean.”

“No,” he said. “It ain’t.”

She looked at the dirt by his boot instead of at him. “I came to say what I didn’t say at Mize’s.”

He waited.

“You should have told me about that trailer.”

He looked at her without flinching. “Yes.”

“You don’t get to say yes like that and have it cover the ground.”

“I didn’t say it did.”

“I stood up in court with half a thing missing that I should’ve known.”

“Yes.”

“That matters.”

“Yes.”

She made herself lift her eyes. “Why didn’t you tell me.”

He took his time before he answered.

“Because it was a bad fact,” he said. “Because I’d already left it out. Because by the time I knew I needed to say it, saying it looked too much like fixing myself.”

“You let me find it by accident,” she said.

“Yes.”

“You let Emily build around a hole.”

His jaw moved once. “Yes.”

She folded her arms tight across her middle, not for emphasis but because something in her had gone loose. “I don’t trust you clean.”

He nodded once. “You shouldn’t.”

A breeze lifted the edge of the shirt on the line by the house and dropped it again. Somewhere beyond the back fence a calf bawled.

She said, “I didn’t take the shoulder to Emily for you.”

“I know.”

“I took it because it wouldn’t leave me alone.”

“I know that too.”

“You keep saying that.”

“Because it’s true.”

She looked past him then and saw a cardboard box just inside the barn door with halters looped over it and a ratchet strap lying across the top. Another box sat near the porch steps, not full but not empty either. She registered it without knowing why.

“Were you loaded that night four days before,” she asked.

He knew which night. “Yes.”

“With what.”

“Two yearlings.”

“Not Culpepper’s.”

“No.”

She let that sit. “That still doesn’t answer the rest.”

“No,” he said. “It don’t.”

He bent, picked up the plat again, and this time carried it to the porch rail, setting it there with the care he gave tools. When he turned back he said, “You want water?”

She thought about saying no. Then she heard herself say, “Yeah.”

He went inside. She followed two steps later and stopped in the kitchen doorway.

The table was clear except for a jar of screws and a folded bill. Two cabinet doors stood open.

He handed her a glass from the sink. Their fingers did not touch.

She drank and set it down.

He leaned a hip against the counter. “Emily said the lane came from you.”

“She didn’t tell me not to tell you.”

“No.”

“I didn’t know if she would.”

“Didn’t matter much after they put Dale up.”

Shannon nodded.

He said, “I didn’t ask you to do that.”

“I know.”

“And you didn’t owe me.”

“No.”

She looked at him properly then. “What did you think was going to happen, leaving it out.”

He did not pretend not to understand. “I thought maybe it would stay mine.”

“That was stupid.”

“Yes.”

She said, “You could’ve told me at Mize’s.”

“I could have.”

“But you didn’t.”

“No.”

“Why.”

He glanced toward the open window over the sink where the screen hummed faint with flies. “Because by then I was tired of handing the county rope and calling it honesty.”

“Emily says they can refile if they want.”

“They can.”

“You think they will.”

He shifted one shoulder. “Maybe. Maybe they talk big and do nothing. Maybe they wait till folks forget the shoulder. Maybe they don’t need court anymore.” He looked at her. “Town already made up most of what it needs.”

She said, “Mrs. Haskins said the lawyer found a ditch to hide in.”

A line moved at the corner of his mouth. Not a smile. “Sounds like her.”

“Lester said technical holes.”

“That sounds like him too.”

She looked down at the water ring her glass had left on the table. “I came because I didn’t want to leave it where we left it.”

He was quiet for a beat. “All right.”

She picked up the glass and took it to the sink, because doing something with her hands felt better than standing still. He moved to make room and they passed close without touching. She could smell sun on his shirt, the faint soap the jail used still clinging somewhere under it, coffee gone stale in the pot on the stove.

At the door she stopped.

“I still don’t know,” she said.

“I know.”

“That ain’t the same thing as done.”

“No.”

She looked back at him. “I’m not promising you anything.”

“I didn’t ask.”

“No,” she said. “You don’t.”

He took that. “Drive careful going out.”

She almost laughed at the plainness of it. Instead she nodded and went.

When she pulled back onto the road, the boxes by the porch stayed with her longer than his face did.

He came to Mize’s the next Wednesday near closing.

The day had been hot enough to leave the whole store sticky. Shannon was wiping down the drink cooler handles while Mize counted the drawer. The evening light came low through the west windows and laid a bar of gold across the feed sacks.

She heard the truck before she saw it. The idle was familiar even after all the weeks of avoiding familiarity on purpose.

Mize looked up at the same time she did. He said nothing.

Eli came in with dust on his boots and a folded slip of paper in his hand. He bought diesel, paid cash, and stood while Mize wrote the ticket. The whole exchange took maybe forty seconds. Then he turned to Shannon.

“You got a minute.”

Mize kept looking at the register. “Take all the minutes you want out on the dock. I’m not hearing anything.”

The back loading dock held the day’s heat in the boards. Out by the dumpsters a dog was nosing through a grease-stained sack. The sky over the cotton gin had gone that washed pale color it got before full dark.

Eli stood with one hand on the rail. Shannon came out and let the screen door slap behind her.

“What.”

He looked at the lot once, then back at her. “I’m leaving Monday.”

He let the silence stay.

“For work?” she asked finally.

“Yes.”

“Where.”

“West Texas. Outside Dalhart.”

She stared at him. “That’s arranged already.”

“Yes.”

“How long.”

“As long as it holds.”

She crossed her arms and leaned back against the wall because her knees had done something small and unreliable. “Since when.”

“A man I worked with years back called when he heard. I said maybe. Then after Monday I said yes.”

“You had it half set before they dropped it.”

“Yes.”

The screen door behind her creaked with the store fan drawing air through. Somewhere in front, Mize coughed on purpose loud. The town went on with the ordinary sounds of itself and Shannon stood on the dock listening to a piece of it tilt.

“Why are you telling me,” she said.

He looked at her as if the answer ought to be plain. “Because leaving without telling you would be another way of hiding.”

A truck passed on the highway and the wind of it carried hot dust up to the dock.

“Is this because of the case,” she asked.

“It’s because of all of it.”

“That don’t mean anything.”

He rested both hands on the rail and looked out over the lot. “This place already did what it was going to do to me,” he said. “I don’t see much point standing here to find out the rest.”

She waited.

After a moment he added, “And I stopped believing this ground was worth what it asks.”

“What about the place,” she asked. “Your place.”

“Lease is near done. My cousin’ll take the equipment I’m not carrying. Rest can sit till I decide different.”

“You sound like you’ve been packing.”

He glanced at her. “I have.”

The boxes. The halters. The clear table.

“When were you going to tell me if you hadn’t come here today.”

“Today.”

“That’s convenient.”

“It’s when I had it settled.”

The old irritation flared. “You like giving people things once they can’t change them.”

His face closed a little. “That might be fair.”

She looked away immediately, sorry and not taking it back.

After a beat he said, “I didn’t come for you to bless it.”

“I know.”

“I came because you ought to hear it from me.”

She nodded once. The evening light had dropped enough now that his face was more shadow than not. “All right.”

He straightened from the rail. “I’ll be down at the old landing tomorrow after work. Got a jack and some straps stored in the shed there from spring. Need to pick them up before I go.”

She looked at him.

“If you want the rest said somewhere quieter than a loading dock,” he said, “I’ll be there.”

Then he went back through the store, leaving the screen to slap once behind him. Shannon stayed on the dock until she heard the diesel pump kick on.

The old landing off Old River Road sat in a bend of the river where the bank had given up trying to be neat years ago. Cottonwoods leaned over the water and one half-dead willow stretched its roots down through mud that cracked in dry weather and swallowed boots in wet. The county no longer maintained the ramp. Somebody from time to time dragged beer cans out there or launched a flat-bottom, but mostly it stayed quiet except for fishermen and people with things to say they did not want town windows on.

Shannon got there a little after six.

The road in had grass up the middle and scratches from branches along one side where cars hugged away from the washout. Cicadas had started up in the trees and the air by the water held that darker, cooler smell rivers got even when the day itself was still hot. Mud. Leaf rot. Slow water against old concrete. Somewhere upriver a heron barked its ugly sound and flapped up through the cottonwoods.

Eli’s truck was backed in near the landing with the tailgate down. A rusted farm jack lay in the bed beside a coil of chain and two ratchet straps. He was sitting on the edge of the tailgate with his forearms on his knees, looking out toward the water.

He got down when he heard her car.

She parked beside the tree line and walked over, boots sinking a little where the ground stayed damp. The river was low. Streaks of clay showed along the bank. Mosquitoes found her almost at once.

“You weren’t kidding about stored out here,” she said, nodding at the jack.

“No.”

He stood by the tailgate and waited while she came the last few steps. The evening had gone that soft gold-gray where faces showed and did not show. She could hear the tick of his engine cooling.

She stopped beside the truck, close enough to touch if either of them had reached and neither doing it.

“You always keep equipment in odd places?” she asked.

“Only the things I don’t mind losing.”

She looked at the jack again. “That doesn’t sound true.”

“No,” he said. “It doesn’t.”

He picked up the chain and laid it farther into the bed, making room without saying sit. She didn’t.

They stood facing the water.

A dragonfly skimmed low over the surface and vanished into shadow.

She said, “Emily thinks they won’t refile unless something new comes in.”

“What does she call new.”

“Something stronger than what they had.”

He nodded. “Maybe.”

“You don’t believe it.”

“I believe they’d rather leave it hanging over me than spend more money proving what they can’t prove.”

The river slid by slow and brown. Somebody had cut initials into the ramp rail years back. Shannon ran her thumb over one of the grooves without reading it.

“I don’t know whether leaving now makes sense or just feels clean,” she said.

“It doesn’t feel clean.”

She looked at him.

He rested one hand on the tailgate. “It just feels decided.”

She asked, “You hate it here that much now.”

“No.”

“Then why.”

He looked out over the water a long time before he answered. “Because staying starts looking too much like asking permission. From people I’m not going to ask.”

She let that settle.

“It isn’t everybody,” she said after a while.

“No,” he said. “But enough.”

She thought of Mrs. Haskins folding the paper. Lester by the ice chest. Men on the square switching from certainty to contempt without ever once touching doubt. She thought of Wade under the courthouse tree, of Emily saying this is what you get, don’t mistake it.

“Enough,” she said.

A fish turned near the bank with a small wet sound.

He said, “You staying.”

It was not quite a question.

“Yes.”

“You want to.”

She gave a short breath that wasn’t laughter. “Some days.”

He nodded as if that fit what he already knew.

“You think I fit here better than you do,” she said.

“Yes.”

“That’s not always a comfort.”

“No.” He looked at her then, fully. “I know.”

The mosquitoes got worse as the light dropped. He reached into the truck for a can of spray and held it out. She took it, sprayed her wrists and neck, handed it back. Their fingers brushed this time, quick and accidental and not nothing.

He said, “I’m not leaving because of you.”

She stared at the river. “I didn’t think you were.”

“I’m telling you anyway.”

She nodded.

He clicked the cap back on the spray. “And I’m not staying because of you.”

That one she turned toward. “I didn’t ask you to.”

“I know.”

She leaned her hip against the truck bed. “You mind if I tell you something ugly.”

“No.”

“I still don’t know if you did it.”

He took that without any visible change. “All right.”

“I know the lane doesn’t fit. I know the shoulder doesn’t fit. I know what they proved and what they overreached. I still don’t know.”

His gaze held on hers. “I know.”

She almost said stop saying that. Instead she said, “Doesn’t bother you.”

“It bothers me plenty.” He looked back at the water. “Just don’t change much.”

“That’s not fair.”

“No,” he said. “But it’s true.”

Silence opened again, broader this time.

The trees at the edge of the bank clicked with insects. Somewhere across the water a cow lowed from deep in the timber. The air had cooled just enough that the sweat at the back of Shannon’s neck no longer felt like heat but the memory of it.

She said, “Why did you ask me out here.”

He rested both hands on the tailgate and thought. “Because I didn’t want the last real talk we had to be on that dock behind Mize’s.”

She said, “That sounds like planning.”

“It is.”

“You usually don’t.”

“No.”

The corner of her mouth moved despite herself. He saw it and let it pass without remark.

She asked, “You ever think maybe you’re leaving too soon.”

“Yes.”

“And.”

“And I’m going anyway.”

The light had gone low enough now that the river turned almost black where it moved under the trees. Eli reached into the cab and switched on the parking light over the bed, more for the jack and chain than for them. The small yellow glow cut one side of his face and left the other in shadow.

Shannon looked at his hands, the scar at the base of one thumb, the fresh redness where county cuffs had rubbed his wrist days before. She had not wanted to notice that in the hall. She noticed it now.

He followed her gaze and dropped his sleeve without fuss.

“You didn’t ask how the job pays,” he said.

“I don’t care what it pays.”

He nodded. “All right.”

She looked at the river and then back at him. “Would you have gone without telling me.”

“No.”

“Why not.”

He took one breath. “Because I did enough leaving things unsaid.”

The landing seemed to narrow around them. Shannon could hear the blood in her ears over the cicadas.

She said his name once. “Eli.”

He looked at her and did not move.

She stepped closer before she had time to improve the decision. Not all the way. Enough to put one hand against the front of his shirt just below the collarbone. The cotton was warm from the day and the body under it steadier than hers felt.

He looked down at her hand, then back up. He waited one full beat—long enough that she could have taken it away and called the whole thing a gesture, long enough that what happened next belonged to both of them.

Then he lifted his hand to her jaw.

Not cupping. Just his fingers under her chin, turning her face up slightly, asking the last question without words.

She did not step back.

He kissed her.

It was not soft. It was not rushed. It was the plain, held thing two people do when they have run out of safer ways to tell the truth. His mouth was dry from coffee and road dust. The first pressure of it was careful, almost formal, and then less so when she answered. Her hand tightened in his shirt. His other hand came to her waist and stayed there, solid and warm, not dragging her in so much as refusing to let the moment thin itself out.

She tilted into him. The tailgate hit the back of her thighs. He stepped closer, enough that she could feel the hard line of his belt buckle, the day’s heat still held in his skin through the shirt, the roughness of his jaw at the corner of her mouth. When his mouth opened against hers, hers did too without decision, and the kiss deepened by inches instead of leaps. No desperation. No sweetness added after the fact. Just the steady pressure of him and the answering fact of her there with him.

She slid her hand up from his shirtfront to the back of his neck. His hair was shorter than it had been weeks ago, the ends rough against her palm. He made a low sound into her mouth that was not a word. She felt it more than heard it.

The river moved somewhere behind them. The bed light hummed faint over the truck. Mosquitoes whined at the edge of the warmth between their bodies and seemed very far away.

When he broke the kiss, he did it slow, not jerking back, giving the space back to her by degrees. His hand stayed at her waist one second longer than the rest of him. They were close enough still that if either breathed deeper they would be back in it.

Neither did.

Shannon kept her hand at his neck until that became impossible not to notice. Then she let it fall.

He looked at her mouth once and then away toward the river.

Her own breath came strange in the quiet. She set both hands on the edge of the tailgate behind her because she needed something solid under them.

He said her name, low.

She shook her head once. Not no. Not don’t. Just enough.

He understood.

After a moment he said, “It’ll be dark on the road back.”

“I know.”

The place where his hand had been at her waist still felt marked by heat.

He took one step back, then another, and reached into the truck for the jack handle as if that were why they were there. She watched him do it and was grateful for the clumsy mercy of ordinary motion returning.

“I’ll go,” she said.

He nodded. “All right.”

She walked back to her car with the feel of his mouth still actual on hers, not memory yet. At the driver’s door she looked back once. He was standing by the tailgate with one hand on the farm jack, the bed light behind him, the river dark at his shoulder. He lifted a hand once, small and level.

She got in and left.

The morning he went out dawned cooler than the week before, not cold, just enough of a break in the heat to make the open car window feel like something other than necessity. Shannon woke before the alarm and lay there knowing why. By six-thirty she had coffee in a travel mug and Eli’s road under her tires.

Mist sat low over the bottoms. The fields looked washed and temporary in the half-light. She had not decided on the drive whether she was there to say goodbye or only to watch him go far enough that goodbye would not help.

When she turned in at his place, the gray gooseneck trailer was already hitched.

A toolbox was strapped in the bed. A duffel sat on the passenger side of the cab. The porch was bare except for one chair and a rolled-up hose.

He was at the trailer tongue checking the chains when he heard her engine. He straightened, wiped his hands on a rag, and stood still while she parked.

The air smelled like cut weeds and cold engine metal.

She got out with her coffee still in hand. “You start early.”

“Wanted the state line before noon.”

She looked at the trailer. “You’re taking that thing.”

“Yes.”

He came a few steps closer. Not all the way to her car. “You didn’t have to come.”

“I know.”

He nodded.

The morning was so quiet she could hear the hitch click once as the truck settled.

She asked, “Everything packed.”

“Enough.”

“That place in Dalhart got a bunkhouse.”

“For now.”

“For now sounds temporary.”

“It probably is.”

She sipped coffee that had gone too hot to taste. He had shaved. Or trimmed enough that the stubble lay closer. She could see the line where the cuffs had rubbed healing at his wrist.

He glanced toward the road. “I need to get moving.”

“I know.”

She said, “I didn’t come to stop you.”

“I know.”

“I came because I wanted to see you go.”

“All right,” he said.

He came the rest of the way and stopped close. Not close enough for touch. The sun was just beginning to hit the top of the barn roof behind him.

“Take care of yourself,” she said.

“You too.”

He went back to the truck. She stood by her door and watched him climb in, settle, pull the door shut. The engine turned over once, twice, then caught. He checked the mirrors, eased forward, stopped to be sure the trailer cleared the porch post, and pulled out slow through the gate.

At the road he paused and looked left, then right. For one second she thought he might look back again. He didn’t. He turned west.

The trailer tires rattled over the cattle guard and then found the county road. Dust rose pale behind him and flattened in the cool air. Shannon stood with the coffee cooling in her hand and watched the truck move between the hedgerows, gray trailer following steady and straight.

He passed the bend by the Johnson place.

Then the next one by the ditch.

Then he was smaller than the road and finally only a shape between trees.

She watched until the last of the dust settled back into the road and there was nothing to call after but morning.

By the time Shannon got back to Marlow the square looked the way it always did on a weekday near noon: courthouse flag hanging lazy in the heat, the Chronicle rack crooked by the drugstore, a delivery truck blocking half of Main while somebody argued over invoices at the back door of the hardware store.

She went to the records room in the annex.

The room sat on the second floor over the clerk’s office, long and narrow, with one high window looking out toward the square and another over the alley. It smelled like blueprints, dust, and the sweet chemical bite of copy toner. A map table took up most of the center. On the back wall metal drawers held plats in long labeled rows.

There were three new survey copies waiting in a cardboard tray with routing slips clipped to them. Shannon set down her purse, rolled up her sleeves, and went to work.

The first sheet wanted to curl at both ends. She spread it on the table and laid a brass weight on one corner, then another on the opposite edge. The paper flattened slowly under her hands. Black lines, section marks, bearings, a creek line wandering through the lower quarter. Ordinary. Exact.

Down on the square a county truck pulled in nose-first by the monument. Through the high window she could see only the cab roof at first, then Wade stepping out on the far side, one hand on the door, hat brim catching the sun. He said something to one of the road crew and started toward the annex. Halfway to the steps he looked up and saw her at the map table. He stopped with one hand on the rail. Then he turned back toward the truck instead.

Shannon bent over the plat and wrote the file number in the lower right corner in clean block letters. The copier thumped once in the next room. Somebody downstairs laughed. A phone rang and rang and was answered.

She slid the finished sheet into a cardboard tube, pressed the cap on with the heel of her hand, and set it in the outgoing rack. Then she reached for the next one, laying the brass weight down before the corner could curl.

Outside, a breeze finally moved across the square hard enough to show the flag’s shape.
